Lecture 1 (Latter Part): Observed Evolution & Experimental Evidence

A. Observations of Evolution in the Wild

  • Core Concept: Evolution can be observed directly in natural populations, especially when selective pressures change or when species are introduced to new environments.

  • Example: House Sparrows in North America

    • Background: Introduced from Europe in the 19th century.

    • Evolutionary Process: Adapted to different climates across North America due to genetic variation and varying selective pressures.

    • Observed Adaptation:

      • Northern populations: Generally larger and darker colored.

      • Southern populations: Generally smaller and lighter colored.

    • Adaptive Advantages in Cold (North):

      • Darker colors: Absorb more sunlight (warmth).

      • Larger size: Smaller surface area-to-volume ratio, reducing relative heat loss (related to Bergmann's Rule).

    • Significance: Demonstrates natural selection leading to micro-evolution observable within human timescales.

B. Experiments Demonstrating Evolution

  • [INSTRUCTOR EMPHASIS]: "Study well all the experimental designs that we talked about, like how evolution can be tested in a lab. Those sections are important."

  • 1. John Endler's Guppy Experiments in Trinidad

    • Background Observation:

      • Female guppies prefer colorful males (sexual selection).

      • Colorful males are more easily seen and eaten by predators (natural selection by predation).

      • Wild populations: Males are more colorful in areas with fewer/less dangerous predators; duller where dangerous predators are common.

    • Experimental Design:

      • Endler transferred guppies from a high-predation area (dull males) to a new stream region with less dangerous predators.

      • Observed them for ~2 years (approx. 15 guppy generations).

    • Results:

      • In the low-predation environment, sexual selection became dominant.

      • The transplanted guppy population rapidly evolved to have significantly more brightly colored males.

    • Key Takeaways:

      • Demonstrates that populations can evolve rapidly when selective pressures change.

      • Illustrates the interplay between natural selection and sexual selection.

      • Genetic variation for color was present in the population, allowing for this evolutionary response.

  • 2. David Conover & Stephan Munch's Atlantic Silverside Experiment (Evolution in the Lab)

    • Hypothesis: Fish populations evolve different body sizes when harvested based on size.

    • Experimental Organism: Atlantic silversides.

    • Experimental Setup:

      • 6,000 fish, divided into six 1,000-fish populations (two replicates for each treatment).

      • Experiment ran for four generations.

      • In each generation, 900 fish were removed from each tank before spawning (simulating harvesting).

    • Treatment Groups (Selective Harvesting):

      1. Populations A1, A2 (Remove Biggest): Only the 100 smallest fish reproduced.

      2. Populations C1, C2 (Remove Smallest): Only the 100 largest fish reproduced.

      3. Populations B1, B2 (Random Removal): 100 fish of random sizes reproduced (control).

    • Results (Mean Weight of Harvested Fish Over Generations):

      • Blue Line (Removed Smallest; Largest Reproduced): Mean body weight increased.

      • Red Line (Random Removal): Mean body weight did not change significantly.

      • Green Line (Removed Biggest; Smallest Reproduced): Mean body weight decreased.

    • Core Conclusion: Body size evolved predictably based on the type of size-selective harvesting.

      • Harvesting large fish leads to evolution of smaller average body size.

      • Harvesting small fish leads to evolution of larger average body size.

    • Real-World Implication: Fisheries-induced evolution. Consistently harvesting the largest fish can cause wild populations to evolve smaller average body sizes over time, impacting fishery yields.

    • [Instructor Emphasis]: This is an observable example of an evolutionary process demonstrated in a lab setting.

Lecture 2: Prokaryotes (and the Origin of Eukaryotes via Endosymbiosis)

A. General Characteristics & Classification of Prokaryotes

  • Definition: Unicellular microorganisms lacking a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. DNA is in the cytoplasm (nucleoid region).

  • Two Domains:

    1. Domain Bacteria (Kingdom Eubacteria):

      • Cell walls contain peptidoglycan.

      • Diverse habitats. Example: E. coli.

    2. Domain Archaea (Kingdom Archaebacteria):

      • Cell walls lack peptidoglycan.

      • Cell membranes have different lipids (ether-linked, branched isoprenoids/phytanyl chains; some form monolayers) compared to Bacteria (ester-linked, unbranched fatty acids).

      • DNA sequences of key genes (for replication, transcription, translation) are more similar to Eukaryotes than to Bacteria.

      • Often extremophiles:

        • Methanogens: Produce methane, anaerobic.

        • Halophiles: Live in high salt.

        • Thermophiles: Live in high temperatures.

  • Evolutionary Relationship: Archaea and Eukarya share a more recent common ancestor than either does with Bacteria.

B. Prokaryotic Structures

  • Common to All Cells: Plasma membrane, cytoplasm (cytosol), DNA, ribosomes.

  • Nucleoid: Region containing the main circular DNA chromosome. DNA is associated with Nucleoid-Associated Proteins (NAPs) (analogous to eukaryotic histones).

  • Plasmids: Small, circular, extrachromosomal DNA molecules; often carry genes for antibiotic resistance or other advantages. Used in genetic engineering.

  • Ribosomes: 70S type (composed of 50S large subunit and 30S small subunit). Site of protein synthesis.

    • Eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S (60S + 40S).

    • Mitochondrial and chloroplast ribosomes are also typically 70S-like.

  • Cell Wall:

    • Bacteria: Contains peptidoglycan (polymer of sugars and amino acids, including D-amino acids).

      • Gram-positive bacteria: Thick peptidoglycan layer, teichoic acids (including lipoteichoic acids anchoring wall to membrane). Stain purple.

      • Gram-negative bacteria: Thin peptidoglycan layer in periplasmic space, surrounded by an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides (LPS) (endotoxin) and porins. Stain pink/red.

    • Archaea: NO peptidoglycan. Walls can be pseudopeptidoglycan, polysaccharides, glycoproteins, or S-layers (protein).

  • Capsule/Glycocalyx: Outer sticky layer (polysaccharides/proteins) outside the cell wall in some prokaryotes.

    • Capsule: Well-organized. Aids in attachment, protection from desiccation, phagocytosis, and host defenses.

    • Slime layer: Less organized, loosely attached.

  • S-layer: Protein/glycoprotein layer outside cell wall (or as cell wall in some Archaea).

  • Appendages:

    • Flagella (singular: flagellum): Long, whip-like structures for motility. Made of flagellin.

    • Pili (singular: pilus):

      • Fimbriae (short attachment pili): Numerous, short, hair-like. For adhesion to surfaces and other cells. Made of pilin.

      • Sex Pili (long conjugation pili): Longer, fewer. Used for conjugation (transfer of genetic material, often plasmids, between bacterial cells).

  • Mesosomes (in some bacteria): Invaginations of the plasma membrane.

    • Functions: Cell wall synthesis, DNA replication/segregation, increased surface area for respiration (contain ETC enzymes, analogous to mitochondria).

  • Photosynthetic Structures (in cyanobacteria & photosynthetic bacteria): Infoldings of the plasma membrane containing photosynthetic pigments (e.g., thylakoids in cyanobacteria; chromatophores in others).

  • Endospores (in some bacteria, e.g., Bacillus, Clostridium):

    • Dormant, highly resistant structures formed during unfavorable conditions. Protect the bacterial genome.

    • Process of formation: Sporulation.

    • Not a form of reproduction, but survival.

C. Prokaryotic Diversity: Size, Shape, Nutrition, Metabolism, Reproduction

  • Size: Typically 1-5 µm.

  • Shape: Bacilli (rod), Cocci (sphere), Spirilla (spiral).

  • Movement: Flagella, gliding, non-motile.

  • Nutrition (Energy & Carbon Acquisition):

    • Heterotroph: Organic molecules for energy and carbon.

    • Photoheterotroph: Light for energy, organic molecules for carbon.

    • Photoautotroph: Light for energy, CO2 for carbon (e.g., cyanobacteria).

    • Chemoautotroph: Chemical reactions (inorganic substances) for energy, CO2 for carbon.

  • Metabolism (Energy Release):

    • Obligate Aerobe: Requires O2 (cellular respiration).

    • Obligate Anaerobe: O2 is toxic; uses fermentation.

    • Facultative Anaerobe: Can use O2 (respiration) or not (fermentation).

  • Reproduction & Genetic Change:

    • Binary Fission: Asexual reproduction (genetically identical daughters).

    • Sources of Genetic Variation:

      • Mutation: Random DNA changes.

      • Conjugation: Horizontal gene transfer via sex pili (plasmids).

D. Ecological Roles of Prokaryotes

  • Decomposers: Break down dead organic matter, recycle nutrients. Essential in sewage treatment.

    • Overgrowth (e.g., due to nutrient pollution) can lead to oxygen depletion (eutrophication).

  • Producers: Photosynthetic prokaryotes (e.g., cyanobacteria like Prochlorococcus) form the base of many aquatic food webs, produce O2.

  • Nitrogen Fixers: Convert atmospheric N2 into usable forms (ammonia, NH3).

    • Example: Rhizobium in root nodules of legumes (symbiotic relationship).

    • Provide ~90% of nitrogen used by other organisms.

E. Endosymbiotic Theory (Origin of Eukaryotes)

  • [INSTRUCTOR EMPHASIS/CASE STUDY]

  • Core Concept: Eukaryotic cells evolved from a symbiotic relationship between different prokaryotic cells. An ancestral host cell (likely an archaeon) engulfed other prokaryotes, which became organelles.

  • Kwang Jeon's Amoebae & X-bacteria (Accidental Experiment):

    • Amoebae infected by X-bacteria initially became sick.

    • Survivors developed a mutualistic relationship: amoebae became dependent on a protein produced by the X-bacteria, and X-bacteria thrived inside.

    • Illustrates how a parasitic relationship can evolve into mutualism and dependence.

  • Origin of Mitochondria:

    • Descended from an engulfed alpha-proteobacterium (similar to Rickettsia).

    • Host cell benefited from ATP production; bacterium benefited from protection/nutrients.

  • Origin of Chloroplasts:

    • Descended from an engulfed cyanobacterium.

    • Occurred later, in the lineage leading to algae and plants.

  • Evidence for Endosymbiosis (Mitochondria & Chloroplasts):

    1. Membranes: Have double membranes (outer similar to host, inner similar to prokaryote).

    2. DNA: Possess their own circular DNA genome (like prokaryotes).

    3. Ribosomes: Have 70S-type ribosomes (like prokaryotes).

    4. Reproduction: Replicate by binary fission, independently of host cell nuclear division.

    5. Size: Similar in size to bacteria.

  • Gene Transfer: Over evolutionary time, many genes from the organelle genome were transferred to the host cell's nucleus. This solidified the dependence and integrated the organelle into the eukaryotic cell.

  • Impact on Tree of Life: Suggests the Tree of Life involves not just branching (divergence) but also merging of lineages (convergence through symbiosis). Eukaryotes have a "hybrid" ancestry.

Lecture 3: Eukaryotes and Protists

A. Characteristics of Eukaryotes

  • Origin: Evolved from prokaryotes ~2.7 billion years ago (via endosymbiosis).

  • Defining Features:

    • Membrane-bound nucleus containing linear DNA (organized with histones into chromosomes).

    • Numerous membrane-bound organelles.

    • Larger cell size (10-100 µm) and more complex than prokaryotes.

    • Can be unicellular or multicellular.

  • Key Eukaryotic Organelles (Structure & Function):

    • Nucleus: Contains DNA; control center.

    • Nucleolus: Inside nucleus; site of rRNA synthesis and ribosome assembly.

    • Ribosomes: 80S type (60S + 40S subunits) in cytoplasm and on ER. Protein synthesis.

    • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):

      • Rough ER (RER): Studded with ribosomes; protein synthesis, modification, and transport.

      • Smooth ER (SER): Lipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage.

    • Golgi Apparatus (Complex): Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids from ER for secretion or delivery to other organelles.

    • Mitochondria: "Powerhouses"; site of cellular respiration, ATP production. (Double membrane, cristae, matrix, own DNA & ribosomes).

    • Lysosomes (Animal cells): Contain digestive enzymes; break down waste, cellular debris, ingested materials. (Vacuoles in yeast/plants have similar functions).

    • Peroxisomes: Metabolic compartments; break down fatty acids, detoxify harmful substances.

    • Cytoskeleton: Network of protein filaments (microtubules, microfilaments/actin filaments, intermediate filaments) providing structural support, cell shape, movement, and organelle transport.

    • Centrioles (Animal cells): Involved in cell division (organize spindle fibers).

    • Cell Wall (Plants, Fungi, some Protists): Rigid outer layer for support and protection. (Cellulose in plants, chitin in fungi).

    • Chloroplasts (Plants, Algae): Site of photosynthesis. (Double membrane, thylakoids, grana, stroma, own DNA & ribosomes).

    • Vacuoles (esp. large central vacuole in Plants): Storage, maintaining turgor pressure, waste disposal.

  • Animal vs. Plant Cells - Key Differences:

    • Plant Cells Have: Cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, large central vacuole, plasmodesmata.

    • Animal Cells Have: Centrioles, lysosomes (typically); lack cell walls, chloroplasts, large central vacuoles.

B. Kingdom Protista

  • General Characteristics:

    • Eukaryotic.

    • The most diverse eukaryotic kingdom; a "miscellaneous drawer."

    • Mostly unicellular, some colonial or simple multicellular.

    • Can be autotrophic, heterotrophic, or mixotrophic.

    • Reproduce asexually (mitosis, binary fission) and/or sexually.

    • Found in diverse, mostly aquatic or moist environments.

  • Informal Groups (based on ecological roles/resemblance):

    1. Protozoa (Animal-like Protists):

      • Unicellular, heterotrophic (ingestive or absorptive).

      • Classified by motility:

        • Amoebas (Sarcodina): Move and feed using pseudopodia ("false feet"). No cell wall. Example: Amoeba proteus.

          • Naegleria fowleri ("brain-eating amoeba"): Pathogenic amoeboflagellate causing Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM). Found in warm freshwater.

        • Flagellates (Mastigophora): Move using one or more flagella. Examples: Euglena (can be mixotrophic), Trypanosoma (causes African sleeping sickness).

        • Ciliates (Ciliophora): Move and feed using numerous cilia. Complex cellular structure. Example: Paramecium.

          • Paramecium features: Oral groove, food vacuoles, contractile vacuoles (osmoregulation), macronucleus (daily functions), micronucleus (sexual reproduction - conjugation).

        • Sporozoans (Apicomplexa): Non-motile (in adult stage), parasitic, form spores. Complex life cycles. Example: Plasmodium (causes malaria).

          • Malaria life cycle involves mosquito (vector, definitive host) and human (intermediate host). Sporozoites infect liver, merozoites infect red blood cells.

    2. Algae (Plant-like Protists):

      • Photosynthetic (autotrophic), contain chlorophyll and other pigments.

      • Produce significant atmospheric oxygen.

      • Form the base of aquatic food webs (phytoplankton).

      • Can be unicellular (e.g., diatoms, dinoflagellates, Euglena), colonial (e.g., Volvox), or multicellular (e.g., seaweeds like kelp - brown algae; red algae; green algae).

      • Euglena: Unicellular, flagellated, mixotrophic (photosynthetic with chloroplasts and eyespot/stigma for phototaxis; can also absorb nutrients heterotrophically). Contractile vacuole for osmoregulation.

    3. Fungus-like Protists (Slime Molds & Water Molds):

      • Heterotrophic decomposers (saprophytic).

      • Differ from true fungi:

        • Can be motile at some life stage.

        • Cell walls typically lack chitin (or have cellulose).

      • Slime Molds: Amoeboid feeding stage, form sporangia. Often brightly colored.

      • Water Molds (Oomycetes): Filamentous, live in water or moist soil. Some are plant pathogens (e.g., downy mildews, Phytophthora infestans - Irish potato famine).

Lecture 4: Kingdom of Fungi

A. General Characteristics of Fungi

  • Eukaryotic microorganisms.

  • Heterotrophic: Obtain nutrients by absorption (osmotrophic).

    • Saprophytes (Saprobes): Decompose dead organic matter (major ecological role as recyclers).

    • Parasites: Live on/in living organisms, causing diseases (mycoses).

    • Mutualists: e.g., lichens (fungus + alga/cyanobacterium), mycorrhizae (fungus + plant roots).

  • Cell Wall: Composed of chitin (a polysaccharide).

  • Cell Membrane: Contains ergosterol (target for antifungal drugs) instead of cholesterol.

  • Body Structure:

    • Yeasts: Unicellular, typically oval/round. Reproduce by budding or fission.

    • Molds: Multicellular, filamentous. Basic structural unit is the hypha (plural: hyphae).

      • Mycelium: A tangled mass/network of hyphae.

      • Hyphae Types:

        • Septate hyphae: Divided by cross-walls (septa) with pores allowing cytoplasmic flow.

        • Coenocytic (aseptate) hyphae: Lack septa, multinucleate.

        • Vegetative hyphae: Obtain nutrients.

        • Aerial (reproductive) hyphae: Project above surface, bear spores.

  • Dimorphism: Some fungi (esp. pathogenic ones) can switch between yeast and mold forms depending on environmental conditions (e.g., temperature).

  • Reproduction:

    • Asexual:

      • Spores: Conidia (chains, not in sac), sporangiospores (in a sac/sporangium), arthrospores (fragmentation of hyphae), chlamydospores (thick-walled resting spores), blastospores (buds).

      • Budding (yeasts).

      • Binary Fission (some yeasts, e.g., Schizosaccharomyces).

      • Fragmentation of hyphae.

    • Sexual: Involves fusion of compatible nuclei from different mating types (+/-).

      • Three Phases:

        1. Plasmogamy: Fusion of cytoplasm.

        2. Karyogamy: Fusion of nuclei (forms diploid zygote).

        3. Meiosis: Zygote undergoes meiosis to produce haploid sexual spores.

      • Sexual Spores: Ascospores (in an ascus), basidiospores (on a basidium), zygospores (fusion of gametangia), oospores.

      • Significance: Increases genetic variation.

    • Teleomorph: Sexual reproductive stage.

    • Anamorph: Asexual reproductive stage.

    • Holomorph: The whole fungus (both stages if present).

  • Growth Conditions:

    • Prefer acidic pH (~5.0-5.5).

    • Most are aerobic (molds); yeasts can be facultative anaerobes (fermentation - e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae in baking/brewing).

    • Tolerate high osmotic pressure (high sugar/salt).

    • Require less moisture than bacteria.

    • Can metabolize complex carbohydrates (lignin, cellulose).

  • Food Storage: Glycogen and lipids.

B. Major Fungal Groups (Phyla/Divisions)

  • (Instructor indicated not to memorize specific classification names for Plantae/Gymnosperms, but these fungal phyla are fundamental).

  • 1. Ascomycota (Sac Fungi):

    • Produce sexual spores (ascospores) in a sac-like structure called an ascus.

    • Asexual reproduction commonly by conidia.

    • Hyphae are septate.

    • Includes most yeasts (e.g., Saccharomyces), morels, truffles, Penicillium, Aspergillus, many plant pathogens.

  • 2. Basidiomycota (Club Fungi):

    • Produce sexual spores (basidiospores) externally on a club-shaped structure called a basidium.

    • Hyphae are septate, often with complex clamp connections.

    • Includes mushrooms, puffballs, shelf fungi, rusts, smuts.

  • 3. Zygomycota (Conjugation Fungi):

    • Produce sexual spores (zygospores) from the fusion of two compatible gametangia.

    • Asexual reproduction by sporangiospores.

    • Hyphae are typically coenocytic (aseptate).

    • Example: Rhizopus stolonifer (black bread mold).

  • 4. Deuteromycota (Fungi Imperfecti) - Historical Grouping:

    • Artificial group for fungi where no sexual stage (teleomorph) was known.

    • Reproduce asexually by conidia.

    • Many have since been reclassified into Ascomycota or Basidiomycota once their sexual stages were discovered.

    • Historically included many human pathogens (e.g., Candida, Cryptococcus).

  • Chytridiomycota (Chytrids): (Often included as a fifth major phylum)

    • Primarily aquatic, primitive fungi.

    • Produce motile spores (zoospores) with flagella.

    • Some are parasitic (e.g., on amphibians).

C. Significance of Fungi

  • Ecological:

    • Decomposers: Essential for nutrient cycling.

    • Mycorrhizae: Mutualistic symbiosis between fungi and plant roots (fungus enhances nutrient/water absorption for plant; plant provides carbohydrates to fungus).

    • Lichens: Symbiosis between fungus (usually ascomycete) and alga or cyanobacterium. Pioneer organisms.

  • Economic/Human Use:

    • Food: Mushrooms, truffles, yeasts in food production (bread, cheese, soy sauce).

    • Beverages: Fermentation for beer, wine (yeasts).

    • Antibiotics: Penicillin (from Penicillium), cephalosporin.

    • Other Drugs: Cyclosporine (immunosuppressant), statins (cholesterol-lowering).

    • Industrial Products: Citric acid, enzymes.

    • Research Models: Saccharomyces cerevisiae (yeast) is a key model organism in genetics and cell biology.

  • Detrimental:

    • Plant Diseases: Rusts, smuts, mildews, blights (major agricultural losses).

    • Animal/Human Diseases (Mycoses):

      • Superficial: Athlete's foot, ringworm (dermatophytes).

      • Systemic: Candidiasis (Candida albicans), cryptococcosis, aspergillosis (often in immunocompromised individuals).

    • Food Spoilage.

    • Toxin Production (Mycotoxins): Aflatoxins (produced by Aspergillus, carcinogenic).

Lecture 5: Kingdom of Plantae

A. General Characteristics of Plants

  • Eukaryotic, multicellular.

  • Autotrophic: Primarily photosynthetic, using chlorophyll (in chloroplasts) to convert light energy into chemical energy (sugars).

  • Cell Wall: Composed mainly of cellulose.

  • Non-motile (generally anchored).

  • Life Cycle: Exhibit alternation of generations (multicellular haploid gametophyte and multicellular diploid sporophyte).

  • Specialized Structures: Roots, stems, leaves (in most), vascular tissues (in most), reproductive structures (spores, cones, flowers, seeds, fruits).

B. Classification Criteria for Plants

  1. Plant Body Differentiation: Presence/absence of true roots, stems, leaves.

  2. Vascular System: Presence/absence of xylem and phloem.

  3. Seed Formation: Presence/absence of seeds; if seeds present, whether enclosed in fruit.

C. Major Plant Groups & Evolutionary Trends

  • 1. Non-Vascular Plants (Bryophytes)

    • Examples: Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts.

    • "Amphibians of the plant kingdom": Require moist environments, especially for sexual reproduction (sperm swim to egg).

    • Lack true roots, stems, leaves, and vascular tissue.

    • Anchored by rhizoids.

    • Gametophyte (n) is the dominant, photosynthetic, independent generation.

    • Sporophyte (2n) is smaller, dependent on the gametophyte for nutrition.

    • Reproduce by spores.

    • Liverworts (Hepaticopsida): Thalloid or leafy gametophyte. Asexual reproduction by gemmae.

    • Mosses (Bryopsida): Gametophyte has protonema and leafy stage. Sporophyte often more complex than in liverworts. Sphagnum (peat moss) is economically important.

    • Ecological role as pioneer species, soil formation, prevent erosion.

  • 2. Seedless Vascular Plants (Pteridophytes)

    • Examples: Ferns, Horsetails, Club Mosses (Lycopodium, Selaginella).

    • First true vascular plants (xylem and phloem present).

    • Have true roots, stems, and leaves (fronds in ferns).

    • Sporophyte (2n) is the dominant, photosynthetic, independent generation.

    • Gametophyte (n) (prothallus) is small, free-living, photosynthetic, but less conspicuous.

    • Require water for fertilization (flagellated sperm).

    • Reproduce by spores (produced in sporangia, often clustered in sori on fern fronds, or in strobili/cones in club mosses/horsetails).

    • Homosporous: Produce one type of spore -> bisexual gametophyte (most ferns).

    • Heterosporous: Produce two types of spores (microspores -> male gametophyte; megaspores -> female gametophyte) (e.g., Selaginella, some aquatic ferns). Heterospory is a key step towards seed evolution.

    • Lack seeds and flowers.

  • 3. Seed Plants (Spermatophytes) - Phanerogams

    • Key Adaptations for Terrestrial Life:

      • Seeds: Protect and nourish the embryo, aid dispersal.

      • Pollen: Male gametophyte, allows sperm delivery without free water.

      • Reduced, dependent gametophytes.

    • Gymnosperms ("Naked Seeds")

      • Examples: Conifers (pines, firs, spruces), Cycads, Ginkgo, Gnetophytes.

      • Seeds are not enclosed within a fruit; typically borne on scales of cones (strobili).

      • Non-flowering.

      • Mostly woody trees or shrubs, often evergreen.

      • Vascular tissue: Xylem primarily tracheids; phloem sieve cells (no companion cells).

      • Sporophyte (2n) is dominant.

      • Gametophytes (n) are highly reduced and dependent on the sporophyte.

        • Male gametophyte = pollen grain.

        • Female gametophyte = develops within an ovule (contains archegonia with egg cells).

      • Pollination: Primarily by wind (anemophilous).

      • Fertilization: Pollen tube grows to ovule (siphonogamy). No double fertilization.

      • Endosperm (nutritive tissue in seed): Is haploid female gametophyte tissue, formed before fertilization.

    • Angiosperms (Flowering Plants)

      • Most diverse and widespread plant group.

      • Key Features: Flowers, fruits, seeds enclosed within fruits.

      • Flowers: Reproductive structures, attract pollinators.

      • Fruits: Develop from the ovary (and sometimes other floral parts), protect seeds and aid in dispersal.

      • Vascular tissue: Xylem has vessels (tracheae) and tracheids; phloem has sieve tubes and companion cells (more efficient).

      • Sporophyte (2n) is dominant.

      • Gametophytes (n) are extremely reduced and dependent.

        • Male gametophyte = pollen grain (typically 2-3 cells).

        • Female gametophyte = embryo sac (typically 7 cells, 8 nuclei) within ovule.

      • Pollination: By wind, water, or animals (insects, birds, bats).

      • Double Fertilization (Unique to Angiosperms):

        1. One sperm nucleus fuses with the egg cell -> diploid (2n) zygote (develops into embryo).

        2. Other sperm nucleus fuses with two polar nuclei in central cell -> triploid (3n) primary endosperm nucleus (develops into endosperm - nutritive tissue for embryo).

      • Sub-Classes:

        • Monocotyledonae (Monocots): One cotyledon, parallel leaf venation, flower parts in 3s, fibrous roots, scattered vascular bundles in stem, no true secondary growth. (e.g., grasses, lilies, orchids, palms).

        • Dicotyledonae (Dicots) - now often considered paraphyletic, with Eudicots being the major clade: Two cotyledons, net-like leaf venation, flower parts in 4s or 5s, taproot system, vascular bundles in a ring in stem, can have secondary growth. (e.g., roses, oaks, sunflowers, beans).

D. Plant Life Cycles (General Patterns)

  • Alternation of Generations: All plants alternate between a multicellular haploid (n) gametophyte stage and a multicellular diploid (2n) sporophyte stage.

  • Evolutionary Trend: From non-vascular plants to seed plants, there is a progressive reduction of the gametophyte generation and increasing dominance of the sporophyte generation.

  • Life Cycle Types (Broader Biological Context):

    • Haplontic: Dominant phase is haploid gametophyte; zygote is the only diploid stage, undergoes meiosis immediately. (e.g., many algae like Volvox, Spirogyra).

    • Diplontic: Dominant phase is diploid sporophyte; gametes are the only haploid stage. (e.g., Animals, some algae like Fucus; Gymnosperms and Angiosperms are strongly diplontic with highly reduced gametophytes).

    • Haplo-diplontic (Alternation of Generations): Both haploid gametophyte and diploid sporophyte are multicellular and significant.

      • Bryophytes: Gametophyte dominant.

      • Pteridophytes: Sporophyte dominant, but gametophyte is free-living.

Lecture 6: Kingdom of Animalia (Vertebrates and Invertebrates) & Early Concepts of Evolution

A. General Characteristics of Animals

  • Eukaryotic, multicellular.

  • Heterotrophic: Obtain nutrients by ingestion (eating other organisms or organic matter), followed by internal digestion.

  • Lack cell walls.

  • Motile at some stage of life.

  • Possess nervous tissue and muscle tissue (most).

  • Reproduce sexually (most), with diploid stage usually dominant.

  • Characteristic embryonic development (e.g., blastula, gastrula stages).

B. Classification of Animals

  • Major Division:

    • Invertebrates: Animals without a backbone (vertebral column). Constitute ~97% of all animal species.

    • Vertebrates: Animals with a backbone. (Subphylum Vertebrata within Phylum Chordata).

  • Criteria for Invertebrate Classification: Body symmetry, tissue organization, body cavities, developmental patterns.

    • Symmetry:

      • Asymmetrical: No definite shape (e.g., Sponges).

      • Radial Symmetry: Body parts arranged around a central axis (e.g., Cnidarians, adult Echinoderms).

      • Bilateral Symmetry: Body can be divided into mirror-image left and right halves (most animals). Associated with cephalization (development of a head).

C. Major Invertebrate Phyla/Groups

  • Phylum Porifera (Sponges):

    • Asymmetrical, simplest multicellular animals.

    • Sessile (attached) filter-feeders; water drawn through pores.

    • Lack true tissues and organs.

  • Phylum Cnidaria (Cnidarians):

    • Radial symmetry.

    • Two basic body forms: polyp (sessile, e.g., sea anemones, corals) and medusa (free-swimming, e.g., jellyfish).

    • Possess cnidocytes (stinging cells) with nematocysts for prey capture and defense.

    • True tissues (diploblastic - two germ layers).

  • Phylum Platyhelminthes (Flatworms):

    • Bilateral symmetry, acoelomates (no body cavity).

    • Flattened body.

    • Some free-living (e.g., Planaria), many parasitic (e.g., tapeworms, flukes).

    • Triploblastic (three germ layers).

  • Phylum Nematoda (Roundworms):

    • Bilateral symmetry, pseudocoelomates (false body cavity).

    • Unsegmented, cylindrical body, tapered at ends.

    • Extremely abundant, diverse habitats.

    • Free-living decomposers, predators, many parasitic (e.g., hookworms, Ascaris).

  • Phylum Annelida (Segmented Worms):

    • Bilateral symmetry, coelomates (true body cavity).

    • Segmentation (body divided into repeating units).

    • Closed circulatory system.

    • Examples: Earthworms (Oligochaeta), leeches (Hirudinea), marine worms (Polychaeta).

  • Phylum Mollusca (Mollusks):

    • Bilateral symmetry (most), coelomates.

    • Soft-bodied, often with a hard protective shell (calcium carbonate).

    • Three main body parts: muscular foot (locomotion), visceral mass (contains organs), mantle (secretes shell).

    • Many have a radula (rasping tongue-like organ for feeding).

    • Diverse classes:

      • Gastropods (snails, slugs)

      • Bivalves (clams, oysters, mussels)

      • Cephalopods (squids, octopuses, nautiluses) - intelligent, well-developed eyes, often reduced/internal shell.

  • Phylum Arthropoda (Arthropods):

    • Most diverse and abundant animal phylum.

    • Bilateral symmetry, coelomates.

    • Key Features:

      • Segmented body (head, thorax, abdomen - or cephalothorax).

      • Hard exoskeleton (chitin), requires molting for growth.

      • Jointed appendages (legs, antennae, mouthparts).

    • Major Subphyla/Classes:

      • Crustaceans: Mostly aquatic; crabs, lobsters, shrimp, barnacles. Two pairs of antennae.

      • Arachnids: Spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites. Four pairs of legs, no antennae, chelicerae (fangs).

      • Insects (Hexapoda): Largest group. Three body segments (head, thorax, abdomen), three pairs of legs (on thorax), usually one or two pairs of wings (on thorax), one pair of antennae. Compound eyes.

      • Myriapods (centipedes, millipedes).

  • Phylum Echinodermata (Echinoderms):

    • Marine animals.

    • Pentaradial symmetry in adults (larvae are bilaterally symmetrical).

    • Spiny skin (calcareous endoskeleton).

    • Water vascular system with tube feet (locomotion, feeding, respiration).

    • Examples: Starfish (sea stars), sea urchins, sand dollars, sea cucumbers, brittle stars.

D. Vertebrates (Subphylum Vertebrata, Phylum Chordata)

  • Key Chordate Features (present at some life stage): Notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, post-anal tail.

  • Vertebrate Defining Feature: Vertebral column (backbone) replacing or surrounding the notochord. Endoskeleton.

  • Thermoregulation:

    • Ectotherms ("cold-blooded"): Body temperature matches environment (Fish, Amphibians, Reptiles).

    • Endotherms ("warm-blooded"): Regulate internal body temperature (Birds, Mammals).

  • Major Classes of Vertebrates:

    1. Fish:

      • Aquatic, gills for respiration, scales, fins for locomotion/stability.

      • Most have a swim bladder for buoyancy.

      • Jawless Fish (Agnatha): Primitive, eel-like, no true jaw, sucker mouth (e.g., lampreys, hagfish).

      • Cartilaginous Fish (Chondrichthyes): Skeleton of cartilage, ventral mouth, placoid scales (e.g., sharks, skates, rays).

      • Bony Fish (Osteichthyes): Skeleton of bone, operculum (gill cover), swim bladder, diverse (e.g., ray-finned fish, lobe-finned fish - ancestors of tetrapods).

    2. Amphibians:

      • Smooth, moist skin (gas exchange).

      • Aquatic larval stage (gills), terrestrial adult stage (lungs, skin respiration).

      • Undergo metamorphosis.

      • Ectothermic.

      • Examples: Frogs, toads, salamanders, newts.

    3. Reptiles:

      • Dry, scaly skin (prevents water loss).

      • Lungs for respiration.

      • Ectothermic.

      • Lay amniotic eggs (shelled, allows reproduction on land) or live birth.

      • Examples: Lizards, snakes, turtles, crocodiles, alligators. (Birds are now often considered a specialized lineage of reptiles).

    4. Birds (Aves):

      • Endothermic.

      • Feathers (insulation, flight).

      • Hollow bones (lightweight for flight).

      • Wings (modified forelimbs).

      • Beak (no teeth).

      • Efficient respiratory system (air sacs).

      • Hard-shelled amniotic eggs.

    5. Mammals:

      • Endothermic.

      • Hair/fur (insulation).

      • Mammary glands (produce milk to feed young).

      • Differentiated teeth.

      • Three middle ear bones.

      • Diaphragm for breathing.

      • Most give live birth (viviparous).

      • Complex brains.

      • Major Groups:

        • Monotremes: Egg-laying mammals (e.g., platypus, echidna).

        • Marsupials: Pouched mammals (e.g., kangaroos, opossums). Young born very immature, complete development in pouch.

        • Placental (Eutherian) Mammals: Young develop within uterus, nourished by placenta. Most diverse group.

E. Early Concepts of Evolution (Historical Context for Darwin)

  • Jean Baptiste Lamarck (Early 1800s):

    • Proposed that species change over time (evolution).

    • Mechanisms:

      1. Principle of Use and Disuse: Organs used more become stronger/larger; unused organs shrink.

      2. Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics: Changes acquired during an organism's lifetime are passed to offspring. (e.g., giraffe's neck stretching).

    • Believed in an innate drive towards increasing complexity; no extinction.

    • Significance: First comprehensive theory of evolution, though mechanisms largely incorrect.

  • Karl von Baer (Embryologist, early-mid 1800s):

    • Criticized "recapitulation theory" (idea that embryos replay adult stages of ancestral forms).

    • Von Baer's Laws of Embryology:

      • General characters of a group appear earlier in development than specialized characters.

      • Specialized characters develop from more general ones.

      • Embryos of different species diverge from a common form rather than passing through each other's adult stages.

    • His work highlighted developmental similarities among related species (e.g., pharyngeal arches in vertebrate embryos), providing evidence for common ancestry used by Darwin.

    • Tunicates (Sea Squirts): Larval notochord showed a link between invertebrates and vertebrates.

  • Natural Selection (Charles Darwin & Alfred Russel Wallace - mid-1800s):

    • Independently conceived the mechanism of natural selection.

    • Influences:

      • Geology (Lyell - Earth is old, gradual change).

      • Fossil record (extinction, succession of forms).

      • Biogeography (Galapagos finches - Darwin; Malay Archipelago - Wallace).

      • Thomas Malthus's Essay on the Principle of Population (population growth outstrips resources, leading to a "struggle for existence").

      • Artificial selection (selective breeding by humans).

    • Core Principles of Natural Selection:

      1. Variation: Individuals within a population vary in their traits.

      2. Inheritance: Some of this variation is heritable.

      3. Differential Survival and Reproduction: In a given environment, individuals with certain traits are more likely to survive and reproduce than others (they are "fitter").

      4. Adaptation: Over generations, this leads to an increase in the frequency of advantageous traits in the population, and the population becomes better adapted to its environment.

    • Darwin's On the Origin of Species (1859): Presented extensive evidence for evolution and proposed natural selection as its primary mechanism.

    • Reception: Evolution by common descent was accepted relatively quickly; natural selection as the mechanism took longer to gain full acceptance (solidified by the modern synthesis with genetics in the 20th century).

Lecture 7: Human Evolution

A. Early Discoveries and Interpretations

  • Darwin's Initial Omission: Darwin avoided discussing human evolution in Origin of Species due to societal prejudices and limited fossil evidence.

  • First Neanderthal Fossil (1857, Neander Valley, Germany):

    • Skullcap: thick, massive brow ridge.

    • Initial Debate: Ancient extinct human-like species vs. extreme Homo sapiens variant.

    • Thomas Huxley: Viewed Neanderthals as a "slightly lower position within Homo sapiens."

  • Darwin's The Descent of Man (1871):

    • Argued humans evolved from an ape-like common ancestor, likely in Africa.

    • Proposed sexual selection as an additional evolutionary pressure in humans.

    • Alfred Russel Wallace disagreed on human brain evolution, suggesting divine intervention.

  • Eugene Dubois & Pithecanthropus erectus (now Homo erectus) (Java, late 1800s):

    • Fossil showed upright posture but a smaller brain than modern humans.

    • Faced initial skepticism but eventually accepted as an early hominid.

B. The Hominid Fossil Record and Evolutionary Patterns

  • Hominids: Members of the human taxonomic family (Hominidae), including modern humans and their direct extinct ancestors.

  • 20th Century Discoveries: Vast increase in hominid fossils.

    • Revealed a "bushy" tree of evolution, not a linear progression.

    • Multiple hominid species often co-existed.

    • Strong evidence for an African origin.

  • Humans and Chimpanzees:

    • Closest living relatives (DNA ~99% identical).

    • Lineages split ~6 million years ago (mya) in Africa.

C. Key Hominid Genera and Species (General Chronological Order & Significance)

  • Ardipithecus ramidus (~4.4 mya): Early hominid, possibly bipedal with arboreal adaptations.

  • Australopithecus Species (~4.2 - ~2 mya):

    • "Southern Apes"; lived in Africa.

    • Clearly bipedal (e.g., A. anamensis, A. afarensis "Lucy").

    • Small brains (ape-sized), prognathic faces, large molars.

    • Examples: A. afarensis, A. africanus, A. garhi.

  • Paranthropus Species (Robust Australopithecines) (~2.7 - ~1.2 mya):

    • Side branch of hominid evolution.

    • Massive jaws, large molars, sagittal crest (for large chewing muscles). Adapted for tough, fibrous diet.

    • Examples: P. boisei, P. robustus, P. aethiopicus.

  • Homo habilis ("Handy Man") (~2.4 - ~1.4 mya):

    • First widely recognized member of genus Homo.

    • Larger brain than australopithecines.

    • Associated with early stone tool use (Oldowan tools).

  • Homo erectus / Homo ergaster ("Upright Man") (~1.9 mya - ~110,000 ya):

    • Larger brain, smaller teeth/face than H. habilis.

    • More modern body proportions (longer legs, shorter arms).

    • More sophisticated tools (Acheulean tools, e.g., hand axes).

    • First hominid species to migrate out of Africa into Asia and Europe.

    • Likely used fire.

  • Homo heidelbergensis (~700,000 - ~200,000 ya):

    • Transitional species between H. erectus and later hominids.

    • Large brain, robust build.

    • Found in Africa, Europe, Asia.

    • Likely ancestor of Neanderthals and Homo sapiens.

    • Hunted large game, built shelters.

  • Homo neanderthalensis (Neanderthals) (~400,000 - ~40,000 ya):

    • Lived in Europe and Western/Central Asia.

    • Adapted to cold climates: short, stocky build, large nasal cavity.

    • Large brains (on average, slightly larger than modern humans, but different shape).

    • Sophisticated tools (Mousterian tools), hunted large animals, used fire, buried their dead, possible symbolic behavior.

    • Co-existed and interbred with early Homo sapiens.

  • Homo sapiens (Modern Humans) (~300,000 ya - Present):

    • Emerged in Africa.

    • Lighter build, smaller brow ridges, more vertical forehead, distinct chin compared to earlier Homo species.

    • Development of complex language, art, symbolic thought, diverse toolkits (Upper Paleolithic tools).

    • Migrated out of Africa, eventually populating the entire globe.

    • Became the sole surviving hominid species by ~25,000-30,000 ya.

D. Key Milestones in Human Evolution (Summary from Slide 14)

  1. >5 mya: Split from chimpanzee lineage (Africa).

  2. >4 mya: Australopithecus anamensis bipedal (Kenya).

  3. 3 mya: Australopithecus afarensis ("Lucy") lived (Africa).

  4. 2.5 mya: Early tool making (chipped stones); multiple hominid species co-exist in Africa.

  5. 2 mya: First Homo clade members, larger brains (Africa).

  6. 1.5 mya: Hand axes used; hominids spread out of Africa (ancestors of Neanderthals in Europe, H. erectus in Asia).

  7. 100,000 ya: Homo sapiens brains reach current size range (Africa); H. neanderthalensis & H. erectus elsewhere.

  8. 50,000 ya: Cultural explosion (cave paintings, adornment, burials); H. sapiens range extends beyond Africa.

  9. 25,000 ya: Only Homo sapiens remain, spread throughout Old World.

E. Common Misconceptions About Evolution

  • (The instructor directed students to the Berkeley Evolution website for clarifications. The exam might test awareness of these common misunderstandings, so be familiar with the categories.)

  • Misconceptions about evolutionary theory and processes: (e.g., evolution is "just a theory," random, always progressive, individuals evolve).

  • Misconceptions about natural selection and adaptation: (e.g., organisms "try" to adapt, selection gives what's "needed," acts for "good of species," "survival of the fittest" means strongest).

  • Misconceptions about evolutionary trees: (e.g., reading "advancement" across tips, adjacent taxa always closest relatives).

  • Misconceptions about population genetics: (e.g., one gene-one trait simplicity).

  • Misconceptions about evolution and the nature of science: (e.g., not observable/testable, gaps in fossil record disprove it).

  • Misconceptions about the acceptance of evolution: (e.g., theory in crisis, scientists reject Darwinism).

  • Misconceptions about the implications of evolution: (e.g., leads to immorality, "might makes right").

  • Misconceptions about evolution and religion: (e.g., incompatible).

  • Misconceptions about teaching evolution: (e.g., "teach both sides" with creationism).

Consolidated Glossary of Key Terms (Alphabetical)

(This glossary combines key terms from all lectures covered. Focus on understanding terms relevant to the post-midterm topics and those emphasized by the instructor.)

  • Absolute Dating (Numerical Dating): Assigning a specific numerical age (e.g., in millions of years) to rocks or fossils, often using radiometric methods.

  • Acoelomate: An animal lacking a fluid-filled body cavity (coelom).

  • Adaptation: A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness (survival and reproduction) in its specific environment.

  • Adventitious Roots: Roots that arise from non-root tissue, such as stems or leaves.

  • Aerial Hyphae: Fungal hyphae that project above the growth surface, often bearing reproductive spores.

  • Aerobe (Obligate): An organism that requires oxygen for cellular respiration.

  • Algae: A diverse group of mostly aquatic, photosynthetic eukaryotic protists.

  • Alternation of Generations: A life cycle in which there is both a multicellular diploid form (sporophyte) and a multicellular haploid form (gametophyte). Characteristic of plants and some algae.

  • Amniotic Egg: A shelled egg with internal membranes (amnion, chorion, allantois, yolk sac) that allows reptiles, birds, and monotreme mammals to reproduce on land.

  • Amoeba: A type of protozoan that moves and feeds using pseudopodia.

  • Amoeboflagellate: An organism capable of existing as both an amoeba and a flagellate.

  • Amphibians: A class of ectothermic vertebrates that typically begin life as aquatic larvae (with gills) and undergo metamorphosis into terrestrial or semi-aquatic adults (with lungs).

  • Anabolism: Metabolic pathways that construct molecules, requiring energy.

  • Anaerobe (Obligate): An organism for which oxygen is toxic and that uses fermentation or anaerobic respiration to generate energy.

  • Anaerobe (Facultative): An organism that can make ATP by aerobic respiration if oxygen is present but can switch to fermentation or anaerobic respiration if oxygen is absent.

  • Anal Fin: A fin located on the ventral (bottom) side of a fish, posterior to the anus, used for stability.

  • Anamorph: The asexual reproductive stage of a fungus.

  • Anemophilous: Pollination by wind.

  • Angiosperms (Flowering Plants): A major group of seed plants characterized by flowers, seeds enclosed within fruits, and double fertilization.

  • Annelida: A phylum of segmented worms (e.g., earthworms, leeches).

  • Annulus (Fern): A ring of specialized cells on a fern sporangium that aids in spore dispersal.

  • Anther: The pollen-producing part of a stamen in an angiosperm flower.

  • Antheridiophore: A specialized stalk in some bryophytes (e.g., Marchantia) that bears antheridia.

  • Antheridium: The male gametangium (sex organ) that produces sperm in plants (bryophytes, pteridophytes) and some algae/fungi.

  • Antibiotic: A substance produced by a microorganism that inhibits the growth of or destroys other microorganisms.

  • Antipodal Cells: Three haploid cells in the mature embryo sac of angiosperms, located at the end opposite the micropyle.

  • Apogamy: The development of a sporophyte directly from a gametophyte without fertilization.

  • Apospory: The development of a gametophyte directly from sporophyte tissue without meiosis.

  • Archaea: One of the two domains of prokaryotic life, distinct from Bacteria. Often extremophiles.

  • Archegoniophore: A specialized stalk in some bryophytes (e.g., Marchantia) that bears archegonia.

  • Archegonium: The female gametangium (sex organ) that produces an egg in plants (bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms) and some algae/fungi.

  • Arthropoda: The largest animal phylum, characterized by a segmented body, hard exoskeleton (chitin), and jointed appendages (e.g., insects, arachnids, crustaceans).

  • Arthrospore: An asexual fungal spore formed by the fragmentation of a hypha.

  • Artificial Selection: The selective breeding of domesticated plants and animals by humans to encourage the occurrence of desirable traits.

  • Ascocarp: The fruiting body of an ascomycete fungus, which contains asci.

  • Ascomycota (Sac Fungi): A major phylum of fungi characterized by the production of sexual spores (ascospores) within a sac-like structure (ascus).

  • Ascospore: A sexually produced fungal spore formed within an ascus.

  • Ascus (plural: asci): The sac-like structure in ascomycete fungi in which ascospores are formed.

  • Asexual Reproduction: Reproduction without the fusion of gametes, resulting in offspring genetically identical to the parent (e.g., binary fission, budding, spore formation by mitosis).

  • Asymmetrical: Lacking symmetry; no plane can divide the body into mirror-image halves.

  • Autotroph: An organism that produces its own organic compounds (food) from inorganic sources, using light energy (photoautotroph) or chemical energy (chemoautotroph).

  • Bacillus (plural: bacilli): A rod-shaped prokaryotic cell.

  • Bacteria: One of the two domains of prokaryotic life.

  • Basidiomycota (Club Fungi): A major phylum of fungi characterized by the production of sexual spores (basidiospores) on a club-shaped structure (basidium). Includes mushrooms.

  • Basidiospore: A sexually produced fungal spore formed on a basidium.

  • Basidium (plural: basidia): A club-shaped reproductive structure in basidiomycete fungi that produces basidiospores.

  • Bergmann's Rule: An ecogeographical rule stating that within a broadly distributed group of animals, populations and species of larger size are found in colder environments, and species of smaller size are found in warmer regions.

  • Bilateral Symmetry: Body symmetry in which a single plane divides the body into two mirror-image halves (left and right).

  • Binary Fission: A method of asexual reproduction in prokaryotes and some unicellular eukaryotes in which a cell divides into two genetically identical daughter cells.

  • Biogeography: The study of the geographic distribution of species over time.

  • Blastospore (Blastoconidium): An asexual fungal spore produced by budding from a parent cell.

  • Bryophyta: A group of non-vascular plants including mosses, liverworts, and hornworts. Gametophyte generation is dominant.

  • Budding: A form of asexual reproduction in which a new organism develops from an outgrowth or bud on the parent body (e.g., in yeasts).

  • Capsid: The protein shell enclosing the viral genome.

  • Capsule (Bacterial): A well-organized glycocalyx layer outside the bacterial cell wall, aiding in adherence and protection.

  • Capsule (Moss): The sporangium of a moss, part of the sporophyte, where spores are produced.

  • Cartilage: A flexible connective tissue found in various parts of the body, forming the entire skeleton of cartilaginous fish.

  • Cartilaginous Fish (Chondrichthyes): A class of jawed fish with skeletons made of cartilage (e.g., sharks, rays).

  • Caudal Fin (Tail Fin): The fin at the posterior end of a fish, used for propulsion.

  • Cell Wall: A rigid outer layer surrounding the plasma membrane of plant, fungal, algal, and most prokaryotic cells, providing support and protection.

  • Cellulose: A structural polysaccharide of plant cell walls, consisting of glucose monomers.

  • Centrioles: Cylindrical structures found in animal cells and some lower plants, involved in organizing microtubules, particularly during cell division (formation of spindle fibers).

  • Cephalization: The evolutionary trend toward concentration of sensory equipment and nervous tissue at the anterior (head) end of an animal.

  • Chemoautotroph: An organism that obtains energy by oxidizing inorganic substances and uses carbon dioxide as its carbon source.

  • Chemoheterotroph: An organism that must obtain both energy and carbon from organic compounds.

  • Chitin: A structural polysaccharide found in the cell walls of fungi and the exoskeletons of arthropods.

  • Chlorophyll: Green photosynthetic pigment found in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria that captures light energy.

  • Chloroplast: An organelle found in plant and algal cells that is the site of photosynthesis.

  • Chordata: A phylum of animals characterized by a notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, and a post-anal tail at some stage of development. Includes vertebrates.

  • Chromatophore: A pigment-containing cell or (in prokaryotes) a membranous organelle that is the site of photosynthesis.

  • Cilia (singular: cilium): Short, hair-like appendages on the surface of some eukaryotic cells, used for locomotion or moving fluid.

  • Clade: A group of organisms believed to have evolved from a common ancestor, according to the principles of cladistics.

  • Cnidaria: A phylum of aquatic animals with radial symmetry and cnidocytes (stinging cells) (e.g., jellyfish, corals, sea anemones).

  • Cnidocyte: A specialized stinging cell found in cnidarians.

  • Coccus (plural: cocci): A spherical prokaryotic cell.

  • Coelom: A fluid-filled body cavity lined entirely with mesoderm, found in many triploblastic animals.

  • Coenocytic Hyphae: Fungal hyphae that lack septa (cross-walls), resulting in a continuous, multinucleate cytoplasm.

  • Cold-blooded (Ectotherm): An animal whose body temperature is regulated by the external environment.

  • Colonial: Organisms (often unicellular) that live together in a group or colony, sometimes with some division of labor.

  • Compound Eye: An eye found in arthropods (especially insects) composed of many individual light-detecting units called ommatidia.

  • Cone (Strobilus): A reproductive structure in gymnosperms and some pteridophytes, consisting of sporophylls (spore-bearing leaves) arranged around a central axis.

  • Conidia (singular: conidium) / Conidiospore: Asexually produced fungal spores formed at the tip or side of a specialized hypha (conidiophore), not enclosed in a sporangium.

  • Conidiophore: A specialized fungal hypha that bears conidia.

  • Conjugation (Bacterial): The direct transfer of genetic material (usually a plasmid) between two bacterial cells that are temporarily joined by a sex pilus.

  • Conjugation (Ciliate): A form of sexual reproduction in ciliates involving the exchange of micronuclei.

  • Contractile Vacuole: An organelle in some protists (e.g., Amoeba, Paramecium) that pumps excess water out of the cell, maintaining osmotic balance.

  • Cotyledon: A seed leaf in an angiosperm embryo; monocots have one, dicots have two.

  • Cryptogam: A plant that reproduces by spores, without flowers or seeds (includes algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes).

  • Crustaceans: A large group of mostly aquatic arthropods (e.g., crabs, lobsters, shrimp).

  • Cuticle: A waxy, waterproof covering on the surfaces of stems and leaves of most land plants, preventing desiccation.

  • Cyanobacteria: Photosynthetic prokaryotes (bacteria), also known as blue-green algae.

  • Cyst: A dormant, resistant stage in the life cycle of some protists and invertebrates, allowing survival in unfavorable conditions.

  • Cytoskeleton: A network of protein filaments (microtubules, microfilaments, intermediate filaments) in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells that provides structural support, cell shape, and enables cell movement and intracellular transport.

  • Decomposer: An organism (e.g., bacterium, fungus) that breaks down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients.

  • Descent of Man, The: Book by Charles Darwin (1871) discussing human evolution and sexual selection.

  • Deuteromycota (Fungi Imperfecti): A historical, artificial grouping of fungi for which no sexual stage (teleomorph) was known.

  • Dicotyledonae (Dicots): A traditional class of angiosperms characterized by having two cotyledons in the embryo.

  • Dimorphism (Fungal): The ability of some fungi to exist in two distinct morphological forms (e.g., yeast and mold) depending on environmental conditions.

  • Diploid (2n): Having two sets of chromosomes, one inherited from each parent.

  • Dominant Generation: In an alternation of generations life cycle, the generation that is larger, more conspicuous, and/or longer-lived.

  • Dorsal Fin: A fin located on the dorsal (back) side of a fish, used for stability.

  • Double Fertilization: A unique reproductive process in angiosperms where two fertilization events occur: one sperm fuses with the egg to form the zygote (2n), and the other sperm fuses with the two polar nuclei to form the triploid (3n) endosperm.

  • Echinodermata: A phylum of marine animals with spiny skin, pentaradial symmetry (in adults), and a water vascular system (e.g., starfish, sea urchins).

  • Ectotherm: An animal that relies on external environmental sources to regulate its body temperature ("cold-blooded").

  • Egg Cell: The female gamete.

  • Embryo: A multicellular diploid eukaryote in its earliest stage of development, from the time of first cell division until birth, hatching, or germination.

  • Embryo Sac: The female gametophyte of angiosperms, typically containing the egg cell, synergids, polar nuclei, and antipodal cells.

  • Endoskeleton: An internal skeleton, as found in vertebrates.

  • Endosperm: A nutrient-rich tissue (triploid in angiosperms, haploid in gymnosperms) that nourishes the developing embryo in a seed.

  • Endospore (Bacterial): A dormant, highly resistant, non-reproductive structure produced by some bacteria to survive harsh environmental conditions.

  • Endosymbiosis / Endosymbiotic Theory: A theory stating that eukaryotic cells evolved from a symbiotic relationship in which one prokaryotic cell (the endosymbiont) was engulfed by and lived inside another prokaryotic cell (the host). Mitochondria and chloroplasts are believed to have originated this way.

  • Endotherm: An animal that generates its own internal body heat to maintain a relatively constant body temperature ("warm-blooded").

  • Enzyme: A protein (or RNA molecule) that acts as a biological catalyst, speeding up chemical reactions without being consumed.

  • Ergosterol: The primary sterol found in the cell membranes of fungi, analogous to cholesterol in animal cells.

  • Eukaryote: An organism whose cells contain a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Includes protists, fungi, plants, and animals.

  • Evolution: Descent with modification; the change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations.

  • Exoskeleton: A hard, external, supporting covering found in many invertebrates, especially arthropods (made of chitin).

  • Extremophile: An organism (often an archaeon) that thrives in extreme physical or geochemical conditions (e.g., high temperature, high salinity, extreme pH).

  • Eyespot (Stigma): A light-sensitive organelle found in some photosynthetic protists (e.g., Euglena), allowing them to orient towards light.

  • Facultative Anaerobe: See Anaerobe (Facultative).

  • Faunal Succession: The principle that fossil assemblages (groups of fossils) succeed one another in a determinable order through geological time.

  • Feathers: Specialized epidermal outgrowths characteristic of birds, used for flight, insulation, and display.

  • Fermentation: An anaerobic metabolic process that converts sugars to acids, gases, or alcohol, releasing energy.

  • Fertilization: The fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote.

  • Fimbriae (singular: fimbria): Short, bristle-like proteinaceous appendages on the surface of some prokaryotic cells, used for adhesion.

  • Fins: Appendages of aquatic vertebrates (especially fish) used for locomotion, steering, and balance.

  • Fission (Binary): See Binary Fission.

  • Flagellate: A protist that moves using one or more flagella.

  • Flagellin: The protein subunit that makes up bacterial flagella.

  • Flagellum (plural: flagella): A long, whip-like cellular appendage used for locomotion in many prokaryotes and some eukaryotes.

  • Flatworms (Platyhelminthes): A phylum of bilaterally symmetrical, acoelomate flatworms.

  • Flower: The reproductive structure of angiosperms, typically containing sepals, petals, stamens (male), and carpels/pistils (female).

  • Fossil: The preserved remains, impression, or trace of an organism from a past geological age.

  • Fossil Record: The total number of fossils that have been discovered, as well as the information derived from them.

  • Frond: The large, often pinnately compound leaf of a fern.

  • Fruit: In angiosperms, the mature ovary (and sometimes other floral parts) that encloses the seed(s), aiding in protection and dispersal.

  • Fungi (Kingdom Fungi): A kingdom of eukaryotic, heterotrophic organisms with chitinous cell walls, typically reproducing by spores (e.g., yeasts, molds, mushrooms).

  • Gametangium (plural: gametangia): A specialized organ or cell in which gametes are formed in algae, fungi, bryophytes, and pteridophytes.

  • Gamete: A haploid reproductive cell (sperm or egg) that fuses with another gamete during fertilization to form a zygote.

  • Gametophyte: The multicellular haploid (n) phase in the life cycle of organisms undergoing alternation of generations; produces gametes by mitosis.

  • Gemma (plural: gemmae): A small, multicellular asexual reproductive structure in some liverworts, capable of developing into a new plant.

  • Gemma Cup: A small receptacle on the thallus of some liverworts that contains gemmae.

  • Generative Cell: In angiosperm pollen grains, the cell that divides to produce two sperm cells.

  • Genetic Drift: Random changes in allele frequencies in a population from one generation to the next, more pronounced in small populations.

  • Genetic Variation: Differences in DNA sequences among individuals within a population.

  • Gills: Respiratory organs in many aquatic animals (e.g., fish, mollusks, amphibian larvae) that extract dissolved oxygen from water.

  • Glycocalyx: A general term for a layer of polysaccharides or glycoproteins on the outer surface of some cells (e.g., bacterial capsule or slime layer).

  • Golgi Apparatus (Complex): An organelle in eukaryotic cells consisting of stacks of flat membranous sacs (cisternae) that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for secretion or delivery to other organelles.

  • Gram-negative Bacteria: Bacteria that have a thin peptidoglycan cell wall surrounded by an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides; do not retain crystal violet stain in Gram staining (appear pink/red).

  • Gram-positive Bacteria: Bacteria that have a thick peptidoglycan cell wall and no outer membrane; retain crystal violet stain in Gram staining (appear purple).

  • Gymnosperms: A group of seed plants whose seeds are "naked" (not enclosed within a fruit), typically borne on cones (e.g., conifers, cycads).

  • Haploid (n): Having a single set of unpaired chromosomes.

  • Haplo-diplontic Life Cycle: A life cycle (alternation of generations) with significant multicellular haploid (gametophyte) and diploid (sporophyte) phases.

  • Haplontic Life Cycle: A life cycle in which the dominant phase is haploid, and the zygote is the only diploid cell.

  • Haustoria: Specialized hyphal tips of parasitic fungi that penetrate host tissues to absorb nutrients.

  • Heterospory: The production of two distinct types of spores (microspores and megaspores) by a plant, which develop into separate male and female gametophytes, respectively. Found in some pteridophytes and all seed plants.

  • Heterotroph: An organism that cannot produce its own organic compounds and must obtain them by consuming other organisms or their products.

  • Hibernation: A state of inactivity and metabolic depression in endotherms, characterized by low body temperature, slow breathing and heart rate, and low metabolic rate, often during winter.

  • Holomorph: The whole fungus, encompassing all its life stages (both anamorph and teleomorph, if present).

  • Hominid: A member of the taxonomic family Hominidae, which includes modern humans, extinct human species, and their direct ancestors (e.g., Australopithecus, Homo species).

  • Homo erectus: An extinct species of hominid that lived from about 1.9 million years ago to as recently as 110,000 years ago; known for upright posture, larger brain than earlier hominids, and migration out of Africa.

  • Homo habilis: An early species of the genus Homo (about 2.4 to 1.4 million years ago), associated with the first stone tools.

  • Homo neanderthalensis (Neanderthal): An extinct species or subspecies of archaic humans who lived in Eurasia until about 40,000 years ago.

  • Homo sapiens: The species of modern humans.

  • Homology / Homologous Structures: Similarity in characteristics resulting from shared ancestry, even if the structures have different functions in different species (e.g., forelimbs of humans, bats, whales).

  • Homospory: The production of only one type of spore by a plant, which develops into a bisexual gametophyte. Found in most bryophytes and many pteridophytes.

  • Hypha (plural: hyphae): The long, branching, filamentous structural unit of a fungus (mold).

  • Index Fossil: A fossil that is characteristic of a particular geologic time period and is used to identify and date rock layers.

  • Indusium: A protective flap of tissue covering a sorus (cluster of sporangia) on a fern frond.

  • Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics: Lamarck's incorrect idea that traits acquired by an organism during its lifetime could be passed on to its offspring.

  • Integument (Ovule): The outer protective layer(s) of an ovule, which develops into the seed coat.

  • Invertebrate: An animal lacking a backbone (vertebral column).

  • Jawless Fish (Agnatha): A group of primitive, eel-like fish lacking true jaws (e.g., lampreys, hagfish).

  • Karyogamy: The fusion of two haploid nuclei to form a diploid nucleus, occurring during sexual reproduction in fungi.

  • Lamarck, Jean Baptiste: A French naturalist who proposed an early theory of evolution based on use/disuse and inheritance of acquired characteristics.

  • Larva: An immature stage in the life cycle of many animals, morphologically different from the adult.

  • Lichen: A composite organism arising from a symbiotic association between a fungus (mycobiont) and an alga or cyanobacterium (photobiont).

  • Lipopolysaccharide (LPS): A complex molecule found in the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria, acting as an endotoxin.

  • Liverworts (Hepaticopsida): A class of bryophytes with typically thalloid or leafy gametophytes.

  • Lysosome: A membrane-bound organelle in eukaryotic cells containing digestive enzymes.

  • Macronucleus: The larger of two types of nuclei in ciliates, controlling daily metabolic functions.

  • Macrophyll: A large leaf with a complex network of veins, characteristic of ferns and seed plants.

  • Mammals: A class of endothermic vertebrates characterized by hair/fur, mammary glands (producing milk), and three middle ear bones.

  • Mantle (Mollusk): A fold of tissue in mollusks that drapes over the visceral mass and secretes the shell (if present).

  • Medusa: The free-swimming, bell-shaped body form of some cnidarians (e.g., jellyfish).

  • Megaspore: In heterosporous plants, the larger spore that develops into a female gametophyte.

  • Megasporangium: A sporangium that produces megaspores (equivalent to the ovule in seed plants).

  • Megasporocyte: A diploid cell in a megasporangium that undergoes meiosis to produce megaspores.

  • Megasporophyll: A modified leaf or leaf-like structure that bears megasporangia.

  • Meiosis: A type of cell division in sexually reproducing organisms that reduces the number of chromosomes by half, producing haploid gametes or spores from a diploid cell.

  • Mesosome: An invagination of the plasma membrane in some bacteria, possibly involved in cell division, respiration, or cell wall synthesis.

  • Metamorphosis: A profound transformation in an animal's body structure from one developmental stage to another (e.g., tadpole to frog).

  • Methanogen: An archaeon that produces methane (CH4) as a metabolic byproduct in anaerobic conditions.

  • Micro-evolution: Evolutionary change within a species or small group of organisms, especially over a short period.

  • Micronucleus: The smaller of two types of nuclei in ciliates, involved in sexual reproduction (conjugation).

  • Microphyll: A small leaf, typically with a single, unbranched vein, characteristic of lycophytes (club mosses).

  • Microspore: In heterosporous plants, the smaller spore that develops into a male gametophyte (pollen grain in seed plants).

  • Microsporangium: A sporangium that produces microspores (pollen sac in seed plants).

  • Microsporocyte: A diploid cell in a microsporangium that undergoes meiosis to produce microspores.

  • Microsporophyll: A modified leaf or leaf-like structure that bears microsporangia.

  • Mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria): An organelle in eukaryotic cells that is the site of cellular respiration and ATP synthesis ("powerhouse" of the cell).

  • Mitosis: A type of cell division that results in two daughter cells each having the same number and kind of chromosomes as the parent nucleus, typical of ordinary tissue growth.

  • Mixotroph: An organism that can use a mix of different sources of energy and carbon (e.g., both photosynthesis and heterotrophy, like Euglena).

  • Mold: A multicellular, filamentous fungus.

  • Mollusca: A large phylum of soft-bodied invertebrates, many of which have a hard shell (e.g., snails, clams, squids).

  • Monocotyledonae (Monocots): A class of angiosperms characterized by having one cotyledon in the embryo.

  • Motility: The ability of an organism or cell to move independently.

  • Mucus: A slimy, viscous secretion produced by mucous membranes and glands for lubrication and protection.

  • Mutation: A permanent change in the DNA sequence of an organism.

  • Mutualism: A symbiotic relationship in which both participating species benefit.

  • Mycelium (plural: mycelia): The vegetative part of a fungus, consisting of a mass of branching, thread-like hyphae.

  • Mycology: The scientific study of fungi.

  • Mycorrhizae (singular: mycorrhiza): A mutualistic symbiotic association between a fungus and the roots of a vascular plant.

  • Mycosis (plural: mycoses): A fungal infection or disease.

  • Naked Seed: A seed not enclosed within a fruit, characteristic of gymnosperms.

  • Natural Selection: The process whereby organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring, leading to the evolution of populations over time.

  • Nematocyst: The stinging capsule within a cnidocyte of a cnidarian.

  • Nematoda (Roundworms): A phylum of unsegmented, pseudocoelomate roundworms.

  • Nitrogen Fixation: The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen gas (N2) into ammonia (NH3) or other nitrogenous compounds usable by plants, carried out by certain prokaryotes.

  • Non-vascular Plants: Plants lacking a specialized vascular system (xylem and phloem) for transporting water and nutrients (e.g., bryophytes).

  • Notochord: A flexible, rod-shaped supportive structure found in the embryos of all chordates and in the adult stage of some.

  • Nucleoid: The region in a prokaryotic cell where the main circular DNA chromosome is located (not membrane-bound).

  • Nucleoid-Associated Proteins (NAPs): Proteins in prokaryotes that help organize and compact the chromosomal DNA in the nucleoid.

  • Nucleolus: A dense structure within the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, primarily involved in ribosome synthesis.

  • Nucleus (Cellular): A membrane-bound organelle in eukaryotic cells that contains the genetic material (DNA).

  • Numerical Dating: See Absolute Dating.

  • Oldowan Tools: The earliest known stone tool industry, characterized by simple choppers and flakes, associated with Homo habilis.

  • Operculum (Fish): A protective bony flap covering and protecting the gills in bony fish.

  • Oral Groove: A ciliated channel in some protists (e.g., Paramecium) used to direct food particles into the cell mouth (cytostome).

  • Organelle: A specialized, membrane-bound structure within a eukaryotic cell that performs a specific function.

  • Origin of Species, On the: Charles Darwin's seminal book (1859) outlining the theory of evolution by natural selection.

  • Osmoregulation: The control of water balance and solute concentrations in an organism or cell.

  • Osmiotroph: An organism that obtains nutrients by absorbing dissolved organic compounds from its environment (e.g., fungi).

  • Osteichthyes (Bony Fish): A large class of jawed fish with skeletons made primarily of bone.

  • Outer Membrane (Gram-negative bacteria): A lipid bilayer external to the peptidoglycan layer in Gram-negative bacteria, containing lipopolysaccharides (LPS).

  • Ovary (Plant): In angiosperms, the enlarged basal portion of the pistil/carpel where ovules are produced; develops into the fruit after fertilization.

  • Ovule: The structure in seed plants that contains the female gametophyte (embryo sac in angiosperms, female gametophyte with archegonia in gymnosperms) and, after fertilization, develops into a seed.

  • Ovuliferous Scale: In gymnosperm female cones, the scale that bears the ovules.

  • Pangaea: A supercontinent that existed during the late Paleozoic and early Mesozoic eras, incorporating almost all of Earth's landmasses.

  • Paramecium: A genus of unicellular ciliate protozoa.

  • Parasite/Parasitism: A symbiotic relationship in which one organism (the parasite) benefits at the expense of the other organism (the host).

  • Paranthropus: A genus of extinct robust australopithecines, characterized by massive jaws and molars.

  • Pathogen: A disease-causing microorganism or agent.

  • Peat: Partially decayed plant matter (often Sphagnum moss) found in bogs, used as fuel and soil conditioner.

  • Pectoral Fins: Paired fins located on each side of a fish, usually just behind the operculum, used for steering and braking.

  • Pelvic Fins: Paired fins located on the ventral side of a fish, used for stability and maneuvering.

  • Penicillin: An antibiotic produced by Penicillium fungi.

  • Peptidoglycan (Murein): A polymer found in the cell walls of most bacteria, consisting of sugars and amino acids.

  • Periplasmic Space: In Gram-negative bacteria, the space between the inner (plasma) membrane and the outer membrane, containing the thin peptidoglycan layer.

  • Phanerogam: A plant that produces seeds (gymnosperms and angiosperms).

  • Pharyngeal Arches/Slits: Paired structures in the pharyngeal (throat) region of chordate embryos; develop into gills in fish and other structures in terrestrial vertebrates.

  • Phloem: Vascular tissue in plants that conducts sugars and other organic nutrients from where they are made (e.g., leaves) to where they are needed or stored.

  • Photoautotroph: An organism that uses light energy to synthesize organic compounds from inorganic carbon sources (e.g., CO2).

  • Photoheterotroph: An organism that uses light for energy but must obtain carbon from organic compounds.

  • Photosynthesis: The process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose (sugar) and oxygen.

  • Phototaxis: Movement of an organism in response to light.

  • Phylogenetic Tree / Phylogeny: A branching diagram that represents the evolutionary history and relationships among a group of organisms.

  • Phylum (plural: phyla): A major taxonomic rank below kingdom and above class.

  • Phytoplankton: Microscopic, photosynthetic organisms (mostly algae and cyanobacteria) that drift in aquatic environments, forming the base of the food web.

  • Pili (singular: pilus): Hair-like appendages on the surface of many bacteria and archaea. See Fimbriae and Sex Pilus.

  • Pilin: The protein subunit that makes up pili.

  • Placenta: An organ in placental mammals that connects the developing fetus to the uterine wall, allowing nutrient uptake, waste elimination, and gas exchange via the mother's blood supply.

  • Plankton: See Phytoplankton and Zooplankton.

  • Plantae (Kingdom Plantae): The kingdom comprising multicellular, eukaryotic, photosynthetic organisms (plants).

  • Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane): The selectively permeable membrane that encloses the cytoplasm of a cell.

  • Plasmid: A small, circular, extrachromosomal DNA molecule found in bacteria and some other organisms, capable of independent replication. Often carries genes for traits like antibiotic resistance.

  • Plasmogamy: The fusion of the cytoplasm of two parent mycelia or cells during sexual reproduction in fungi, without the fusion of nuclei.

  • Plastid: A group of membrane-bound organelles in plant and algal cells, including chloroplasts, chromoplasts, and leucoplasts.

  • Pollen Grain: In seed plants, the structure containing the male gametophyte, which produces sperm.

  • Pollen Tube: In seed plants, a tube that grows from a pollen grain after germination, conveying the male gametes to the ovule for fertilization.

  • Pollination: The transfer of pollen from the male reproductive structure (anther or male cone) to the female reproductive structure (stigma or ovule).

  • Polyp: The sessile, cylindrical body form of some cnidarians (e.g., sea anemones, corals).

  • Porifera (Sponges): A phylum of simple, multicellular, aquatic animals characterized by a porous body and filter-feeding.

  • Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM): A rare and usually fatal brain infection caused by the amoeba Naegleria fowleri.

  • Prokaryote: A unicellular organism lacking a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles (includes Bacteria and Archaea).

  • Prothallus (Prothallium): The small, free-living, typically heart-shaped gametophyte of ferns and other pteridophytes.

  • Protista (Kingdom Protista): A diverse kingdom of mostly unicellular eukaryotic organisms (e.g., algae, protozoa, slime molds).

  • Protonema: The filamentous, thread-like structure that develops from a germinating moss spore; gives rise to the leafy gametophyte.

  • Protozoa: An informal term for a diverse group of single-celled, heterotrophic, animal-like protists.

  • Pseudocoelomate: An animal whose body cavity (pseudocoelom) is not completely lined by mesoderm.

  • Pseudohypha: A chain of budding yeast cells that have not detached, resembling a hypha.

  • Pseudopeptidoglycan (Pseudomurein): A component of the cell walls of some archaea, structurally similar but chemically different from bacterial peptidoglycan.

  • Pseudopodia ("False Feet"): Temporary cytoplasmic extensions of an amoeboid cell, used for locomotion and engulfing food (phagocytosis).

  • Pteridophyta: A group of seedless vascular plants including ferns, horsetails, and club mosses. Sporophyte generation is dominant.

  • Radial Symmetry: Body symmetry in which body parts are arranged around a central axis, like spokes on a wheel.

  • Radiometric Dating: A method of determining the age of rocks or fossils based on the decay rates of radioactive isotopes.

  • Radula: A tongue-like, toothed organ in many mollusks, used for scraping or cutting food.

  • Recapitulation Theory (Biogenetic Law): The largely discredited theory that an organism's embryonic development (ontogeny) repeats the evolutionary history (phylogeny) of its species.

  • Relative Dating: Determining the relative order of past events (i.e., the age of an object in comparison to another), without necessarily determining their absolute age. Based on principles like superposition.

  • Reptiles: A class of ectothermic vertebrates characterized by scaly skin, lungs, and (typically) laying amniotic eggs.

  • Rhizoid: A root-like filamentous outgrowth in bryophytes and some lower plants/fungi, used for anchorage and some absorption.

  • Rhizome: A horizontal underground stem that can send out roots and shoots from its nodes (e.g., in ferns).

  • Ribosome: A cellular organelle (composed of rRNA and protein) that is the site of protein synthesis. Prokaryotic ribosomes are 70S; eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S.

  • RNA (Ribonucleic Acid): A nucleic acid involved in protein synthesis and gene regulation; typically single-stranded.

  • Root: The part of a vascular plant, typically underground, that anchors the plant, absorbs water and minerals, and stores food.

  • Root Nodule: A swelling on the root of a leguminous plant, containing nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium).

  • S-layer: A regularly structured layer of protein or glycoprotein on the outer surface of the cell wall in many bacteria and archaea.

  • Saprophyte (Saprotroph/Saprobe): An organism (especially a fungus or bacterium) that feeds on dead or decaying organic matter.

  • Scales (Fish): Small, flat, rigid plates that form part of the outer body covering of most fish.

  • Seed: An embryonic plant enclosed in a protective outer covering (seed coat), usually with some stored food. A key adaptation for terrestrial life in gymnosperms and angiosperms.

  • Seed Coat: The protective outer layer of a seed, developed from the integuments of the ovule.

  • Segmentation: The division of an animal's body into a series of similar, repeating units or segments (e.g., in annelids, arthropods).

  • Selective Pressure: Any environmental factor that influences the survival and reproduction of individuals within a population, leading to natural selection.

  • Septate Hyphae: Fungal hyphae that are divided into compartments by cross-walls called septa.

  • Septum (plural: septa): A cross-wall dividing a fungal hypha into cells or compartments; usually has pores.

  • Seta (Bryophyte): The stalk supporting the capsule (sporangium) in the sporophyte of bryophytes.

  • Sex Pilus (Conjugation Pilus): A specialized pilus in some bacteria used to form a bridge for the transfer of genetic material (conjugation).

  • Sexual Reproduction: Reproduction involving the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote, resulting in offspring with genetic material from both parents.

  • Sexual Selection: A form of natural selection in which individuals with certain inherited characteristics are more likely than other individuals to obtain mates.

  • Siphonogamy: A mode of fertilization in seed plants where a pollen tube grows to deliver the male gametes to the ovule, eliminating the need for free water for sperm motility.

  • Slime Layer (Bacterial): A loose, unorganized glycocalyx layer outside the bacterial cell wall.

  • Sorus (plural: sori): A cluster of sporangia on the underside of a fern frond.

  • Species: A group of living organisms consisting of similar individuals capable of exchanging genes or interbreeding to produce fertile offspring.

  • Sperm Cell: The male gamete.

  • Spermatophytes (Seed Plants): Plants that produce seeds (gymnosperms and angiosperms).

  • Sphagnum (Peat Moss): A genus of mosses that forms peat, known for its high water-holding capacity.

  • Spirillum (plural: spirilla): A spiral or corkscrew-shaped prokaryotic cell.

  • Sponges (Porifera): See Porifera.

  • Spontaneous Generation: The outdated hypothesis that living organisms can arise directly from non-living matter.

  • Sporangiophore: A specialized hypha or stalk bearing one or more sporangia.

  • Sporangiospore: An asexual fungal spore produced within a sporangium.

  • Sporangium (plural: sporangia): A sac-like structure in which spores are produced.

  • Spore: A haploid reproductive cell, typically unicellular, capable of developing into a new individual without fusion with another cell. Produced by meiosis in sporophytes (plants) or by mitosis/meiosis in fungi.

  • Sporophyll: A modified leaf or leaf-like structure that bears sporangia.

  • Sporophyte: The multicellular diploid (2n) phase in the life cycle of organisms undergoing alternation of generations; produces haploid spores by meiosis.

  • Sporulation: The process of forming spores, especially endospores in bacteria.

  • Stamen: The male reproductive part of a flower, consisting of an anther (pollen-producing) and a filament (stalk).

  • Stem: The main supporting axis of a plant, typically bearing leaves, buds, and flowers/cones.

  • Stigma (Flower): The receptive tip of a carpel/pistil in a flower, where pollen lands.

  • Stigma (Protist Eyespot): See Eyespot.

  • Stolon (Fungus): A horizontal hypha in some fungi (e.g., Rhizopus) that spreads over the surface and gives rise to rhizoids and sporangiophores.

  • Strata (singular: stratum): Layers of rock, especially sedimentary rock.

  • Strobilus (plural: strobili): See Cone.

  • Superposition (Principle of): In an undisturbed sequence of sedimentary rocks, the oldest layers are at the bottom and the youngest are at the top.

  • Swim Bladder (Gas Bladder): An internal gas-filled sac in many bony fish that helps control buoyancy.

  • Symbiosis: A close and long-term interaction between two or more different biological species. Types include mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.

  • Taproot System: A root system in which one main primary root grows vertically downward, with smaller lateral roots branching off it (common in dicots and gymnosperms).

  • Taxonomy: The science of classifying and naming organisms.

  • Teichoic Acids: Acidic polysaccharides found in the cell walls of Gram-positive bacteria.

  • Teleomorph: The sexual reproductive stage of a fungus.

  • Thallophyta: A historical grouping of organisms with a thallus body (undifferentiated), including algae, fungi, and lichens. In plant context, often refers to algal ancestors.

  • Thallus: A plant body that is not differentiated into true roots, stems, and leaves (e.g., in algae, fungi, liverwort gametophytes).

  • Thermophile: An organism that thrives at high temperatures.

  • Thylakoid: A flattened, sac-like membrane structure inside chloroplasts (and cyanobacteria) that is the site of the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis.

  • Tracheid: A type of water-conducting cell in the xylem of vascular plants; long, tapered cells with lignified walls.

  • Transitional Feature/Fossil: A fossil that exhibits characteristics intermediate between those of an ancestral group and a descendant group, showing an evolutionary transition.

  • Triploid (3n): Having three sets of chromosomes (e.g., endosperm in angiosperms).

  • True/False Questions: A type of exam question requiring identification of a statement as correct or incorrect.

  • Tube Cell: In angiosperm pollen grains, the cell that develops into the pollen tube.

  • Tube Feet: Numerous small, flexible, fluid-filled appendages on the surface of echinoderms, used for locomotion, feeding, and respiration, part of the water vascular system.

  • Tunicates (Sea Squirts): Marine invertebrate chordates whose larvae possess a notochord.

  • Use and Disuse (Principle of): Lamarck's idea that parts of the body that are used extensively become larger and stronger, while those not used deteriorate.

  • Vacuole: A membrane-bound sac within the cytoplasm of a cell, used for storage, waste disposal, maintaining turgor pressure, etc. (Large central vacuole is characteristic of plant cells).

  • Variation (Genetic): Differences in DNA sequences among individuals within a population, providing the raw material for evolution.

  • Vascular Plant: A plant that has a specialized vascular system (xylem and phloem) for transporting water and nutrients.

  • Vascular System/Tissue: Plant tissues (xylem and phloem) that conduct water, minerals, and sugars throughout the plant body.

  • Vegetative Hyphae: Fungal hyphae involved in obtaining nutrients, as opposed to reproduction.

  • Vertebral Column (Backbone): The series of vertebrae that protects the spinal cord and provides support in vertebrate animals.

  • Vertebrate: An animal possessing a vertebral column (backbone).

  • Vesicle: A small, membrane-bound sac within the cytoplasm, used for transport or storage.

  • Vessel (Xylem Vessel Element): A type of water-conducting cell in the xylem of most angiosperms and some gymnosperms; shorter and wider than tracheids, with perforated end walls, forming continuous tubes.

  • Vestigial Structure: A feature that was an adaptation for an organism's ancestor but has lost its original function or become reduced in size/complexity in the descendant (e.g., human appendix, whale pelvic bones).

  • Viviparous: Giving birth to live young that have developed inside the body of the parent.

  • Von Baer, Karl: An embryologist whose work on comparative embryology provided evidence for common descent, later used by Darwin.

  • Wallace, Alfred Russel: A naturalist who independently conceived the theory of evolution by natural selection around the same time as Charles Darwin.

  • Warm-blooded (Endotherm): An animal that generates its own internal body heat to maintain a relatively constant body temperature.

  • Water Vascular System: A unique hydraulic system in echinoderms, used for locomotion, feeding, gas exchange, and waste transport, operating the tube feet.

  • Xylem: Vascular tissue in plants that conducts water and dissolved minerals upward from the roots to the rest of the plant; also provides structural support.

  • Yeast: A unicellular fungus that typically reproduces by budding or fission.

  • Zoosporangium: A sporangium that produces zoospores.

  • Zoospore: A motile, asexual spore that uses a flagellum for locomotion, found in some algae and fungi (e.g., chytrids).

  • Zygomycota (Conjugation Fungi): A phylum of fungi characterized by the formation of zygospores during sexual reproduction.

  • Zygospore: A thick-walled sexual spore formed by the fusion of two compatible gametangia in zygomycete fungi.

  • Zygote: The diploid cell formed by the fusion of male and female gametes (fertilization); the first cell of a new individual.
