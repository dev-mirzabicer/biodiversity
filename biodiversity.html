Slide 3: Early Fossil Discoveries & Additional Clues

  • Early Fossil Discoveries:

    • Scientists have long recognized fossils as evidence of past life.

    • Xenophanes: An ancient Greek philosopher who found fossilized shells on dry land and correctly inferred that the area was once a seabed.

    • Shen Kuo: A Chinese scientist (almost a thousand years ago) who made similar arguments based on fossilized remains of species that could not have lived in the modern environments where they were discovered.

    • Nicholas Steno: In the 17th century, he noted the similarity between shark teeth and "tongue stones" (glossopetrae), arguing that these stones were actually fossilized shark teeth from once-living sharks. This was a significant step in understanding the organic origin of fossils.

    • Paleontologists: Scientists who study fossils.

      • Mary Anning: A notable paleontologist from Victorian England who helped uncover the first correctly identified ichthyosaur fossil, contributing significantly to the understanding of ancient marine reptiles and the diversity of past life.

  • Additional Clues from Fossils:

    • Today, the idea that fossils represent past life is widely accepted (e.g., a T. rex skeleton is clearly from a past organism).

    • However, paleontological research continues to reveal new information from fossils, extending beyond just the anatomy of the preserved organisms.

Slide 6: Transitional Features

  • Core Concept: Transitional Feature

    • A transitional feature is found in a fossil that shows an intermediate state between an ancestral trait (a characteristic of an older, ancestral species) and a derived trait (a characteristic of its later descendants).

    • The fossil record contains many examples of transitional features.

  • Key Takeaway: Transitional features provide strong evidence for evolutionary change over time, showing how species can gradually transform.

  • Transitions in Whale Evolution (Introduction):

    • Pakicetus: An early relative of ancient whales.

    • Pakicetids are known to be closely related to whales and dolphins based on unique specializations of their ear bones.

    • However, Pakicetids lived on land and had nostrils at the front of their skull (similar to modern cows and sheep).

    • The ancestors of whales likely resembled Pakicetus.

    • Question: How did evolution proceed from a Pakicetus-like land animal with frontal nostrils to modern whales with a blowhole (nostrils) at the top of the head?

    • Prediction: If a Pakicetus-like ancestor gave rise to modern whales, we would expect to find intermediate forms in the fossil record, such as one with nostrils in the middle of the skull.

    • Archaeohippus and Parahippus are examples of three-toed horses.

  • Key Takeaway: Fossils like these, bearing transitional features, provide strong evidence illustrating how major evolutionary changes (like the reduction of toes in horses) occurred over time.

Slide 10: Chronology

  • Core Concept: The timing of evolutionary and geological events is determined using two complementary methods:

    1. Relative dating: Determining if one event or rock layer is older or younger than another, without assigning a specific numerical age.

    2. Absolute (numerical or radiometric) dating: Assigning a specific numerical age (e.g., in millions of years) to rocks or fossils.

  • Key Takeaway: This evidence clearly shows that life is very old and allows scientists to place a timescale on the pace of evolutionary change, diversification (the evolution of new species and forms), and extinction.

Slide 11: Radiometric Dating

  • Core Concept: Radiometric Dating

    • Geologists use radiometric dating to estimate how long ago rocks formed and, by inference, the ages of fossils contained within or between those rocks.

  • Radioactive Elements Decay:

    • The universe contains naturally occurring radioactive elements.

    • Radioactive atoms are inherently unstable.

    • Over time, radioactive "parent atoms" decay into stable "daughter atoms" at a predictable, constant rate.

  • Dating Igneous Rocks:

    • When molten rock (magma or lava) cools and solidifies, it forms igneous rocks.

    • Radioactive atoms present in the molten rock become trapped inside the newly formed crystals.

    • These atoms then decay at their characteristic rate.

    • By measuring the ratio of remaining unstable parent atoms to stable daughter atoms in an igneous rock sample, scientists can calculate the time that has passed since the rock formed (crystallized).

  • Bracketing the Fossils:

    • Fossils are generally found in sedimentary rock (formed from accumulated sediments), not igneous rock.

    • Sedimentary rocks can sometimes be dated using radioactive carbon (Carbon-14 dating), but this method is only effective for relatively young materials (younger than about 50,000 years) because carbon decays relatively quickly.

    • To date most older fossils, scientists use a technique called bracketing:

      1. They look for layers of igneous rock (like volcanic ash beds) or intrusions of igneous rock that are above and below the sedimentary layer containing the fossil.

      2. They date these igneous layers using elements with slow decay rates, such as uranium and potassium.

      3. By dating these surrounding igneous layers, they can determine the youngest possible age and oldest possible age for the fossil and the sedimentary layer it's in.

Slide 12: Relative Dating

  • Core Concept: Relative Dating

    • Relative dating places fossils in a temporal sequence (order of occurrence) by noting their positions in layers of rocks, known as strata.

    • Principle of Superposition: In an undisturbed sequence of rock layers, fossils found in lower strata were deposited earlier and are therefore older than fossils in higher strata.

    • Complications: This method can be complicated if:

      • Layers have been up-ended or overturned (e.g., by tectonic forces).

      • Older rock has been pushed up over younger rock (e.g., during mountain formation).

    • In such cases, other lines of evidence and reasoning are used to date fossil-bearing layers relative to one another.

  • Index Fossils:

    • Example: Ammonites (extinct marine cephalopods). In most strata, ammonite fossils are found beneath layers containing dinosaur fossils, indicating ammonites died out before dinosaurs arose.

    • An index fossil is a type of fossil that is restricted to a particular geologic age (it lived for a relatively short period, was geographically widespread, and is easily recognizable). Index fossils are very useful for correlating and figuring out the ages of rocks and other fossils relative to them.

    • If a paleontologist finds ammonites above dinosaur fossils, it would suggest the strata have been overturned.

  • Faunal Succession:

    • This line of evidence and reasoning, based on the consistent vertical ordering of fossil types in strata, is called faunal succession.

    • By studying and comparing strata and their fossils globally, paleontologists have established the geologic timescale, which orders the events of life and Earth history.

  • Limitation: Relative dating establishes the sequence, but numerical dating is needed to calibrate this timescale and determine exact ages.

Slide 13: Numerical Dating

  • Core Concept: Numerical Dating (also called absolute dating or radiometric dating)

    • Relies on the decay of radioactive elements like uranium, potassium, rubidium, and carbon.

    • Radioactive elements decay (convert to a non-radioactive form or a different element) at precisely known and constant rates, which scientists have carefully observed and measured. This rate is often expressed as a half-life (the time it takes for half of the parent atoms in a sample to decay).

  • Process:

    1. Know the decay rate (half-life) of a specific radioactive element.

    2. Measure the amount of the radioactive parent element remaining in a sample and the amount of the stable daughter element produced.

    3. Calculate how old the sample is based on these ratios.

  • Applications and Limitations for Fossils:

    • Direct Dating: Fossils younger than about 50,000 years can sometimes be dated directly using their radioactive carbon (Carbon-14) content (if they contain organic material).

    • Indirect Dating (for most fossils): Most fossils themselves cannot be dated directly using radioactive elements (other than carbon in young fossils) because the fossilization process typically replaces original organic material with minerals, or the elements used for dating (like K, U, Rb) are not incorporated into the fossil itself in a way that reflects its time of death.

    • Instead, the bracketing method (described in Slide 11) is used: dating volcanic ashes or other igneous rocks (which form directly from the crystallization of molten rock) found above and below a fossil-bearing layer to constrain the fossil's age.

  • Example: The fossil trilobites (extinct marine arthropods) in the diagram (referring to the diagram on Slide 10/11) are determined to be "older than 520, but younger than 545 million years" by dating volcanic ash layers above and below them. (Note: The numbers here are slightly different from the diagram, which shows 495, 510, 520, 545 mya. The principle is the same: bracketing. The text might be referring to a general example or a different specific section of the diagram not fully visible.)

  • Key Takeaway: Geologists have assembled the ages for the geological timescale using numerical dating of rocks from around the world.

Slide 14: Biogeography

  • Core Concept: Biogeography

    • Biogeography is the study of the geographic distribution of species on Earth.

    • This distribution provides evidence that informs our understanding of:

      1. The evolution of life.

      2. The movement of continents across the globe via plate tectonics (the theory that Earth's outer shell is divided into several plates that glide over the mantle).

  • Example: Marsupial Mammals

    • Marsupials are mammals characterized by carrying their young in a pouch (e.g., koalas, kangaroos).

    • Distribution: Found in the Americas (e.g., opossums) and in Australia and New Guinea.

    • The Puzzle: How did they end up in such distant locations with no marsupials found in the vast regions in between (e.g., across the Pacific Ocean, Asia, or Africa)?

      • They cannot swim the Pacific Ocean.

      • No fossil evidence of them wandering through Asia or Africa.

      • There appear to be no direct migration routes between the Australian and American populations.

    • Question: How did marsupials get from their place of origin (believed to be North America based on older fossils) to locations half a world away?

  • Image Description:

    • A world map with the heading "marsupial distribution today."

    • Areas where marsupials are found (parts of North and South America, Australia, and New Guinea) are highlighted in orange.

Slide 15: Biogeography (Continued - The Answer)

  • The Answer: The fossil record and the record of past continental positions (continental drift) provide the solution.

    • Pangaea: Evidence suggests that all the continents we know today were once part of a single supercontinent called Pangaea.

    • Fossils of marsupials have been found in Antarctica, as well as in South America and Australia, supporting their presence on these connected landmasses.

    • The provided video link (https://youtu.be/QhldiOaFqpE) likely shows an animation of Pangaea splitting apart, which occurred roughly between 200 to 90 million years ago.

  • Explanation of Marsupial Distribution:

    • Marsupials didn't need a specific migration route across oceans.

    • They were present on parts of Pangaea that later drifted apart. They "rode" these continents to their present positions.

    • Once isolated on these separate continents, they continued to diversify and move around within those landmasses.

  • Later Migration: The fossil record also shows that the North American opossum migrated from South America more recently (perhaps as recently as one million years ago), after the continents of North and South America reconnected.

  • Overall Conclusion: The current distribution of species on Earth, like that of marsupials, makes the most sense when we understand that:

    1. Both life and the Earth itself are very old.

    2. Both life and the Earth's continents have changed significantly over time.

Slide 16: Homologies

  • Core Concept: Homologies

    • If different species share common ancestors, we expect them to share similarities inherited from those ancestors.

    • Homologies are features (anatomical structures, genes, developmental patterns, etc.) inherited from a common ancestor, even if their appearance or function is quite different in the closely related descendant species.

  • Identifying Homologies:

    • Comparing the anatomies (structures) of different living things.

    • Looking at cellular similarities.

    • Studying embryological development (how organisms develop from an embryo).

    • Studying vestigial structures (see Slide 21) within individual organisms.

  • Key Takeaway: Biologists have discovered homologies wherever they have looked in the living world, providing strong evidence of common ancestry.

  • Difficulty in Recognition:

    • Some homologies are easy to recognize (e.g., the forelimbs of cats and dogs share a basic bone structure).

    • Others can be more difficult, especially if the structures have been highly modified.

  • Example: Modified Plant Leaves

    • The slide shows photos of plant leaves that are quite different from each other and from a "normal" leaf, yet are homologous.

Slide 17: Homologies (Plant Leaves Explained)

  • Core Concept: Careful study reveals that the diverse plant structures shown (pitcher, Venus flytrap jaws, poinsettia "petals," cactus spines) are all homologous to one another, meaning they all derive from a common ancestral leaf structure.

  • Evidence for Homology in Poinsettia "Petals":

    • The red "petals" of a poinsettia develop in the same way that its green leaves do.

    • In their mature form, these red structures have the same pattern of veins as the plant's normal green leaves.

  • Evolutionary Modification for Different Functions:

    • Each of these leaf types has been modified through evolution to have a different shape and serve a different function:

      • Pitcher plant and Venus flytrap: Use modified leaves to trap and digest insects (carnivory).

      • Poinsettia: Bright red modified leaves attract pollinators, functioning like flower petals.

      • Cactus: Leaves are modified into small spines, which reduce water loss (crucial in arid environments) and provide protection from herbivores.

Slide 18: Homologies: Anatomical Evidence

  • Core Concept: Organisms that are closely related to one another share many anatomical similarities due to inheritance from a common ancestor. These are anatomical homologies.

  • Obvious Similarities: Sometimes these similarities are conspicuous and easily observed (e.g., the overall body plan of crocodiles and alligators).

  • Less Obvious Similarities: In other cases, more detailed study is needed to appreciate the relationships, particularly when:

    • Lineages have experienced natural selection (the process by which organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring) shaping them in different ways.

    • Examples: The highly modified leaves of different plants (poinsettia "petals," cactus spines) discussed earlier. Their homologous nature as leaves is not immediately obvious without careful study.

Slide 19: Modification of the Tetrapod Skeleton

  • Core Concept: The skeletons of tetrapods, despite vast differences in outward appearance and lifestyle, demonstrate underlying homology.

  • Tetrapods: Vertebrates (animals with backbones) that either have four limbs (legs/wings/arms) or had ancestors with four limbs (e.g., snakes, whose ancestors were legged reptiles).

  • Example: Whales and Hummingbirds

    • Appear very different on the surface.

    • Nonetheless, both are tetrapods and both possess tetrapod skeletons inherited from a common tetrapod ancestor.

  • Homologous Bones:

    • Apart from a few bones that may have been lost over evolutionary time in one lineage or another, nearly every bone in a whale's skeleton corresponds to an equivalent (homologous) bone in a hummingbird's skeleton.

    • However, the sizes and shapes of these homologous bones have been greatly modified over millions of years.

    • This modification occurred as the processes of variation (the natural differences between individuals in a population) and natural selection adapted these species to their very different lifestyles (e.g., swimming in whales, flying in hummingbirds).

      • hind limb (greatly reduced in the whale)

      • ribs

      • radius, ulna, humerus (bones of the forelimb/wing/flipper)

Slide 20: Tetrapod Forelimb

  • Core Concept: The tetrapod forelimb is a classic example of homology, illustrating how a basic ancestral structure has been modified for diverse functions in different descendant lineages.

  • Shared Bone Structure:

    • Organisms like hummingbirds, whales, frogs, and rabbits all have differently shaped forelimbs, reflecting their different lifestyles (flying, swimming, jumping, running).

    • Despite these differences, their forelimbs all share the same fundamental set of bones, arranged in the same pattern:

      • Starting from the shoulder: one bone (the humerus).

      • Followed by two bones (the radius and the ulna).

      • Followed by wrist bones (carpals), hand bones (metacarpals), and finger bones (phalanges) – though these are often highly modified.

  • Evolutionary Significance:

    • These same arm bones are seen in modern tetrapods (including humans).

    • Crucially, they are also found in fossils of very early tetrapods and even in their fish ancestors, such as the extinct fish Eusthenopteron (which had fleshy, lobed fins with a bone structure pre-adapting them for limb development).

  • Key Takeaway: This type of evidence (shared underlying structure despite different functions) strongly demonstrates the common ancestry of all tetrapods.

Slide 21: Homologies: Vestigial Structures

  • Core Concept: Vestigial Structure

    • A vestigial structure is a feature (e.g., organ, bone) that a species inherited from an ancestor but is now less elaborate and/or less functional (or non-functional) than it was in that ancestor.

  • Formation of Vestigial Structures:

    • Usually formed when a lineage (a line of descent) experiences a different set of selective circumstances (environmental pressures) than its ancestors.

    • As a result, selection to maintain the original elaboration and function of the feature either ends or is greatly reduced. Over time, the structure may shrink or lose its original purpose.

  • Relationship to Homology:

    • Vestigial structures are homologous to fully functioning structures that were inherited by related lineages (where the structure remained useful).

  • Significance:

    • They provide strong evidence of common ancestry.

    • They can help trace the evolutionary origin of the species possessing the vestigial structures, offering clues about their ancestors' characteristics and lifestyle.

Slide 22: Examples of Vestigial Structures

  • Dewclaws:

    • Found in several animals, including pigs, cattle, deer, and dogs.

    • Description: Reduced, often nonfunctional digits (toes) that typically don't touch the ground.

    • Example (Pig): The foot of a pig has:

      • Lost digit 1 completely.

      • Digits 2 and 5 (the dewclaws) are greatly reduced.

      • Only digits 3 and 4 are large and support the body.

    • Evolutionary Explanation: Dewclaws are vestigial features inherited from ancestors that had a larger number of functional digits. Evolution best explains their existence.

  • Claws on Bird Wings:

    • Hoatzin chicks: Have claws on their wings (used for climbing among branches).

    • Some adult chickens and ostriches also retain small claws on their wings.

    • Evolutionary Explanation: These vestigial features reflect the fact that the ancestors of all living birds had clawed hands.

    • Fossil Evidence: Illustrated by the 150 million-year-old Jurassic bird, Archaeopteryx, which had well-developed claws on its wing "fingers."

Slide 23: Homologies: Cellular/Molecular Evidence

  • Core Concept: At the cellular and molecular levels, all living things are fundamentally alike.

  • Key Takeaway: These basic similarities are most easily explained by evolution: life shares a common ancestor from which these fundamental traits were inherited.

  • The Cellular Level:

    • All organisms are made of cells.

    • Cells consist of water-filled membranes that contain:

      • Genetic material (DNA or RNA)

      • Proteins (complex molecules that perform most of the work in cells)

      • Lipids (fats and oils, important for membranes and energy storage)

      • Carbohydrates (sugars and starches, for energy and structure)

      • Salts and other substances.

    • The slide points to a comparison of typical animal and plant cells (shown on the next slide), noting that many structures are shared.

    • Shared Structures: Cell membrane, mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria - for energy production), ribosomes (for protein synthesis), endoplasmic reticulum (network for protein and lipid synthesis), nuclear membrane, nucleus (contains genetic material), nucleolus (inside nucleus, for ribosome production), chromosome (structures of DNA), vacuole (storage sac), Golgi apparatus (modifies and packages proteins), flagellum (whip-like tail for movement, present in many animal cells and plant reproductive cells), cytoplasm (jelly-like substance filling the cell).

    • Plant Cell Only: Cell wall (rigid outer layer), chloroplast (for photosynthesis).

    • Animal Cell Only (typically): Centriole (involved in cell division).

  • Key Inference: The fact that most cellular structures occur in both types of cells suggests that these structures were inherited from a common ancestor that also possessed them. The unique structures represent later evolutionary specializations in plant and animal lineages.

Slide 25: The Molecular Level

  • Core Concept: Different species share genetic homologies (similarities in their DNA sequences and genes) as well as anatomical ones.

  • Examples:

    • Humans and Chimpanzees: Their genomes (the complete set of genetic material) differ by only a few percent (the exact percentage depends on what differences are counted). This indicates a very recent common ancestor.

    • Beyond Close Relatives: Genetic homologies extend far beyond closely related "twigs" on the tree of life.

      • For example, approximately 70% of human genes are homologous (share a common ancestral gene) to genes found in acorn worms. Acorn worms are eyeless marine worms that filter food from water or mud – seemingly very different from humans.

  • Key Takeaway: These genes are slightly different in each species (due to accumulated mutations over time), but their striking similarities nevertheless reveal our shared ancestry with creatures that might seem quite different from us.

  • Image Description:

    • Three photos, left to right: a chimpanzee, humans (students in a lab), and an acorn worm.

    • Credits: Chimpanzee (Chi King, Wikipedia), Students (Elena Zhukova, UC Regents), Acorn worm (Casey Dunn, Springer Nature 2015).

Slide 26: The Genetic Code as Homology

  • Core Concept: The genetic code itself is a fundamental homology that links all life on Earth to a common ancestor.

    • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) and RNA (Ribonucleic Acid) possess a simple four-base code (using nucleotide bases A, T/U, C, G) that provides the "instruction manual" for the growth, function, and replication of all living things.

    • This code is (with very minor exceptions) universal across all known life forms.

  • Implication of a Universal Code:

    • If genetic material (e.g., a gene) is transferred from the cell of one organism to the cell of another species, the recipient cell can often "understand" and follow the new instructions as if they were its own.

    • Example: If one of the genes that tells a mouse where to develop an eye is transferred into a fruit fly embryo, the fruit fly embryo understands the genetic instructions perfectly and develops eyes (though they will be fruit fly eyes, not mouse eyes) where the mouse gene was injected. This is a famous experiment demonstrating the deep homology of developmental genes (like Pax6).

  • Key Takeaway:

    • These characteristics of life (universal genetic code, shared molecular machinery) demonstrate the fundamental sameness of all living things on Earth.

    • This shared foundation serves as the basis for modern genetic engineering, where genes from one organism can be inserted and function in another.

Slide 27: Developmental Biology

  • Core Concept: During some stages of embryological development (the process by which an embryo forms and develops), some organisms exhibit ancestral features that were present in their distant ancestors, either in whole or in incomplete form.

  • Evolutionary Explanation:

    • Evolutionary theory and common ancestry explain why we observe these patterns.

    • Lineages change over time, and present-day species have inherited these developmental features (and the genes controlling them) from their ancestors, even if the features are modified or lost in the adult form.

  • Example: Limb-buds in Snake Embryos

    • Some species of living snakes have hind limb-buds (small, undeveloped leg structures) as early embryos.

    • These buds are rapidly lost during development, and the snakes develop into legless adults.

    • The study of these developmental stages in snakes, combined with fossil evidence of ancient snakes that did have hind limbs, supports the hypothesis that snakes evolved from a limbed ancestor.

  • Fossil Evidence:

    • The fossil of the Cretaceous snake Pachyrhachis problematicus (from ~95 million years ago) clearly had small hindlimbs.

    • A reconstruction of the pelvis and hindlimb of Pachyrhachis shows that it has exactly the same bones (ilium, pubis, ischium, femur, tibia, fibula, calcaneum, astragalus – typical leg bones) seen in all terrestrial vertebrates with hindlimbs.

  • Image Description:

    • Left: A fossil of Pachyrhachis problematicus, with a yellow circle highlighting the small hindlimb region. A 1 cm scale bar is present.

    • Right: A line drawing reconstructing the pelvis and hindlimb bones of Pachyrhachis, with individual bones labeled.

Slide 28: Developmental Biology (Continued)

  • Example: Teeth in Baleen Whale Fetuses

    • Toothed Whales (e.g., dolphins, orcas): Have full sets of teeth throughout their lives.

    • Baleen Whales (e.g., humpback, blue whales): As adults, they lack teeth and instead have baleen plates for filter-feeding.

    • However, baleen whales possess teeth in their early fetal stage of development. These teeth are lost before birth.

  • Key Takeaway: The possession of teeth in fetal baleen whales provides evidence of common ancestry with toothed whales (and indeed, all other mammals, which are ancestrally toothed). The genes for tooth development are still present and briefly active, reflecting this ancestry, even though adult baleen whales do not use teeth.

  • Image Description:

Slide 29: Nested Hierarchies

  • Core Concept: Nested Hierarchies

    • If different species share common ancestors, we would expect living things to be related to one another in a pattern that scientists refer to as nested hierarchies – rather like a set of nested boxes, where smaller groups are contained within larger groups, which are within still larger groups.

  • Observation: This is indeed what we observe in the living world and in the fossil record.

    • When we study their traits (characteristics), species naturally cluster into groups based on suites of similar, inherited traits (i.e., homologies).

    • These groups then cluster into larger groups, and so on.

  • Evolutionary Explanation: This observation makes the most sense if we understand that, over time:

    • Lineages (lines of descent) have split and formed new species.

    • These new species then split and formed even more species, and so on.

    • This process of splitting and diversification, with traits being passed down (and modified), generates the branching structure that is the Tree of Life.

Slide 30: Nested Hierarchies (Example)

  • Image Description: An evolutionary tree diagram (a cladogram) illustrating nested hierarchies. Different colored boxes enclose groups within groups.

    • Innermost Groups (Yellow Boxes):

      • "sunflower" and "orchid" are grouped together (they share many homologies, including flowers, making them flowering plants).

      • "pine tree" and "cypress tree" are grouped together (they share many homologies, including cones, making them cone-bearing plants).

    • Next Level (Blue Box): These two groups (flowering plants and cone-bearing plants) nest together because they all share homologies such as seeds (making them seed plants).

    • Next Level (Light Green Box): Seed-bearing plants nest with "fern" because they all share homologies like stomata (pores on leaves/stems for gas exchange). This makes them all vascular plants.

    • Outermost Group (Red Box): This larger group (vascular plants) would then nest with "green algae" (their closest relatives) based on other shared homologies, as part of the larger group of green plants (Viridiplantae).

  • Key Takeaway: This pattern of groups-within-groups continues "across the whole Tree of Life."

Slide 31: Nested Hierarchies (Significance)

  • Alternative Possibility (Not Observed): It could be that each species has a random assortment of traits, with no obvious way to group them. But this is not what we observe.

  • Observed Reality: It's fairly easy to group most organisms into a nested hierarchy based on shared characteristics.

  • Historical Recognition: This pattern was recognized by scientists like Carolus Linnaeus (the father of modern taxonomy) long before Charles Darwin. Linnaeus developed a hierarchical system of classification (kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, species) based on shared similarities, though he did not propose an evolutionary explanation.

  • Darwin's Explanation: Darwin proposed the explanation for this pattern: organisms on the Tree of Life are descended with modification from common ancestors.

  • Key Takeaway: Common ancestry is conspicuous (clearly visible) when we examine the distribution of traits (homologies) in living things. The nested hierarchical pattern is a strong piece of evidence for evolution.

Slide 32: Observations of Evolution in the Wild

  • Core Concept: As predicted by evolutionary theory, populations evolve (change in their heritable characteristics over generations) in response to their surroundings (environment and selective pressures).

  • Challenges in Observation:

    • Much of this evolution happened long ago and was not directly observed by humans.

    • Consequently, we often have to investigate many lines of indirect evidence (fossils, homologies, biogeography, etc.) to reconstruct this history.

  • Opportunities for Direct Observation:

    • However, in some cases, evolution has occurred in the wild over timescales and in places where humans can make direct observations.

    • This means we know what a species was like at one point in time, and then later observe that it has changed in ways that can only be attributed to evolution.

Slide 33: House Sparrows in North America - An Example of Observed Evolution

  • Background: House sparrows were introduced to North America from Europe in the 19th century (1800s).

  • Evolutionary Process: Since their introduction:

    • Genetic variation (pre-existing differences in genes within the sparrow population)

    • Combined with different selective pressures (environmental challenges, e.g., climate) present in different habitats across the continent,

    • Have allowed them to adapt to different parts of North America.

  • Observed Pattern of Adaptation:

    • Modern house sparrows in the north are generally larger and darker colored than those in the south.

    • Adaptive Advantages in Cold Climates (North):

      • Darker colors: Absorb sunlight better than light colors, aiding in warmth.

      • Larger size: A larger body has a smaller surface area to volume ratio. This reduces relative heat loss, which is advantageous in a cold climate (this is related to Bergmann's Rule).

  • Concept Illustrated: This is an example of natural selection acting upon different populations, producing micro-evolution (small-scale evolutionary changes within a species) on a continental scale.

  • Human Observation: This is an evolutionary change that humans have been around to observe firsthand. We know when they were introduced, and we can directly observe the differences in sparrows from various parts of the continent today.

The map generally shows smaller sizes in the south (e.g., Florida, Mexico) and larger sizes in the north (e.g., Canada, northern US).

Slide 34: Experiments - Guppies and Observable Evolution

  • Core Concept: Experiments can also show that populations can evolve, often rapidly.

  • Example: John Endler's Experiments with Guppies in Trinidad

    • These experiments clearly show selection at work.

    • Background Observations in the Wild:

      • Female guppies prefer colorful males as mates (sexual selection).

      • However, colorful males are also more likely to be eaten by predators because they are easier to spot (natural selection due to predation).

      • In parts of streams with less dangerous predators, males are generally more colorful.

      • In locations with more dangerous predators, males tend to be less colorful (duller).

    • Likely Explanation for Natural Pattern: The guppy population in each area has evolved in response to the competing selective pressures: female preference for colorfulness versus predator preference for conspicuous prey.

    • Endler's Experimental Approach: Endler didn't just stop with these observations; he performed experiments on guppy populations in the wild to test this.

Slide 35: Guppy Experiment (Endler's Design and Results)

  • Title: Guppies evolve bright coloration in males when predation threats decrease.

  • Experimental Design:

    • Endler transferred guppies from an area with dangerous predatory fish (these guppies had dull-colored males) to a new region (a different part of a stream) with less dangerous predators.

    • He left them there for about two years, which is approximately 15 guppy generations.

    • He then returned to observe if they had evolved.

  • Results:

    • In the new, low-predation region, without hungry predators preferentially eating the bright fish, sexual selection (female preference for colorful males) became the dominant selective force.

    • The population rapidly evolved: males became significantly more brightly colored.

  • Key Takeaway: This experiment demonstrates that:

    • Genetic variation (for color, in this case) within a population provides the raw material for evolution.

    • Populations can evolve rapidly when environmental conditions (and thus selective pressures) change.

  • Image Description: A diagram illustrating the experiment in three panels (Regions):

    • Region 1: "dangerous predators, dull guppies." Shows a large predatory fish (Crenicichla alta) and dull-colored guppies. An arrow indicates "15 generations of selection" leading to the same dull state.

    • Region 2: "dull guppies introduced to new region with less dangerous predators." Shows dull guppies being "introduced" into an area with a less dangerous predator (Rivulus hartii). An arrow indicates "15 generations of selection." The guppies at the end of this period are shown as brightly colored.

Slide 36: Evolution in the Lab (Atlantic Silversides)

  • Core Concept: This section describes an experiment designed to test the hypothesis that fish populations evolve different body sizes when they are harvested (fished) based on size.

  • Researchers: David O. Conover and his colleague Stephan Munch.

  • Experimental Organism: Atlantic silversides (a type of fish).

  • Experimental Setup:

    • A large number of fish: 6,000 Atlantic silversides in total.

    • Six experimental populations were established, each with 1,000 fish.

    • The experiment was conducted over four generations of fish.

    • The "Twist" (Simulating Harvesting): In each generation, before the fish were allowed to spawn (reproduce), the researchers removed 900 fish from each 1,000-fish tank. This removal simulated what happens in wild fisheries when fish are harvested.

  • Treatment Groups: The 6 populations were divided into three treatment groups, with two replicate populations for each treatment:

    1. Populations A1 and A2 (Remove Biggest): Researchers removed the 900 biggest fish from these tanks. This meant that only the 100 smallest fish in these populations were left to reproduce and contribute to the next generation.

    2. Populations C1 and C2 (Remove Smallest): Researchers removed the 900 smallest fish from these tanks. This meant that only the 100 largest fish in these populations were left to reproduce.

    3. Populations B1 and B2 (Random Removal): Researchers randomly selected and removed 900 fish, without any consideration of their body size. The remaining 100 fish (of random sizes) were left to reproduce.

  • Rationale for Duplicates: [Instructor Emphasis] Running duplicate populations (A2 identical to A1, B2 to B1, C2 to C1) was a crucial safeguard. If an accident or illness had affected one population, the entire four-year-long experiment could have been ruined. The instructor highlighted this as a "really amazing effort."

  • Outcome of Setup: Luckily, none of the fish populations experienced any unusual problems during the four-year experiment.

Summary & Review Points (Overall Lecture)

  • The fossil record provides direct evidence of past life, showing that life is ancient and has changed over time (evolution).

  • Fossils can reveal not only anatomy but also ecological relationships, physiology, and behavior of ancient organisms.

  • Transitional features in fossils (e.g., in whale and horse evolution) show intermediate states between ancestral and descendant forms, demonstrating evolutionary pathways.

  • Chronology (timing of events) is established by relative dating (superposition, faunal succession, index fossils) and absolute/numerical dating (radiometric methods like carbon dating for young fossils, and bracketing with igneous rock dating for older fossils).

  • Biogeography (distribution of species, e.g., marsupials) can be explained by evolution in conjunction with continental drift (plate tectonics).

  • Homologies are similarities inherited from a common ancestor and are strong evidence for evolution. They can be:

    • Anatomical (e.g., tetrapod forelimb structure modified for different functions; modified plant leaves).

    • Vestigial structures (e.g., dewclaws, snake limb-buds, whale fetal teeth) are remnants of ancestral features that are no longer fully functional.

    • Cellular (e.g., shared organelles in animal and plant cells).

    • Molecular (e.g., shared genes between distant relatives like humans and acorn worms; the universal genetic code).

  • Developmental biology reveals ancestral traits during embryological stages (e.g., snake limb-buds, baleen whale fetal teeth).

  • Life is organized into nested hierarchies of groups within groups, a pattern best explained by descent with modification from common ancestors (the Tree of Life).

  • Evolution can be observed in the wild (e.g., adaptation of house sparrows in North America due to natural selection).

  • Experiments can demonstrate evolutionary change:

    • Endler's guppy experiment showed rapid evolution of coloration due to shifts in predation pressure and sexual selection.

    • Conover and Munch's silverside experiment showed that size-selective harvesting leads to predictable evolutionary changes in fish body size over generations. This has important implications for fisheries management.

II. Classifying Prokaryotes

Slides 6-7: General Characteristics & Classification

  • Core Concept: Prokaryotes are unicellular microorganisms that are the smallest and most abundant life forms, covering nearly every square centimeter of Earth.

  • Key Defining Features:

    • Unicellular: Composed of a single cell.

    • No Nucleus: Their DNA is not enclosed within a membrane-bound nucleus.

    • DNA in Cytoplasm: Genetic material (usually a circular DNA molecule) is located in the cytoplasm.

  • Historical Context: For many years, all prokaryotes were simply called "bacteria." Modern classification is more complex.

  • The Two Prokaryotic Domains:

    • Domain Bacteria: Corresponds to the kingdom Eubacteria.

    • Domain Archaea: Corresponds to the kingdom Archaebacteria.

  • Relationship to Eukaryotes: [Instructor Emphasis] The domains Bacteria and Archaea are as different from each other as both are from Eukaryotes. In fact, Archaea share more similarities with Eukaryotes than they do with Bacteria in certain aspects (e.g., DNA sequences of key genes).

  • Image Description (Slide 6): A micrograph of a prokaryotic cell, likely undergoing binary fission (dividing). It appears rod-shaped.

Slide 8: Domain Bacteria

  • Overview: Bacteria constitute the larger of the two prokaryotic domains. They include a vast range of organisms with diverse lifestyles. Biologists sometimes disagree on the exact number of phyla needed for their classification.

  • Habitat: Found almost everywhere: freshwater, saltwater, on land, and within the bodies of other organisms (including humans and other eukaryotes).

  • Example: Escherichia coli (E. coli)

    • A typical bacterium residing in human intestines.

    • The slide shows an illustration of E. coli, highlighting its typical prokaryotic structures.

  • Typical Bacterial Structures (Illustrated with E. coli):

    • DNA: Located in the cytoplasm (nucleoid region, not membrane-bound).

    • Ribosomes: Sites of protein synthesis (shown as black dots).

    • Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane): Inner membrane enclosing the cytoplasm.

    • Cell Wall: Surrounds the cell membrane.

      • Composed primarily of peptidoglycan (a polymer of sugars and amino acids).

      • Functions: Protects the cell from injury and determines its shape.

    • Outer Membrane: Some bacteria, like E. coli, possess a second membrane outside the peptidoglycan cell wall. This makes the cell particularly resistant to damage.

    • Pili (singular: Pilus): Short, hair-like appendages on the surface.

      • Function: Mainly for anchoring the bacterium to surfaces or to other bacteria (e.g., during colony formation or conjugation).

    • Flagellum (plural: Flagella): Long, whip-like tail.

      • Function: Used for movement (motility) in fluid environments.

Slides 9-10: Archaea vs. Bacteria - Key Differences

  • Superficial Similarities: Under a microscope, Archaea and Bacteria can look very similar: both are small, lack nuclei, and typically have cell walls.

  • Important Differences:

    1. Cell Walls:

      • Bacterial cell walls contain peptidoglycan.

      • Archaeal cell walls lack peptidoglycan. Their composition varies (discussed later).

    2. Cell Membranes:

      • Archaeal membranes contain different types of lipids than bacterial membranes.

    3. DNA Sequences:

      • [Instructor Emphasis] DNA sequences of key archaeal genes (involved in processes like transcription and translation) are more similar to those of Eukaryotes than to those of Bacteria. This suggests a closer evolutionary relationship between Archaea and Eukaryotes.

    4. Environments:

      • Archaea are often extremophiles, thriving in harsh environments where most other life cannot survive. Examples:

        • Methanogens: Produce methane gas; live in oxygen-poor environments (e.g., thick mud, digestive tracts of animals).

        • Halophiles: Live in extremely salty environments (e.g., Utah's Great Salt Lake).

        • Thermophiles: Live in very hot environments (e.g., hot springs, volcanic vents, with temperatures approaching boiling point).

  • Evolutionary Implication: Based on these differences, scientists conclude that Archaea and Eukaryotes are more closely related to each other than either is to Bacteria.

    • If drawing an archaean: It would lack a nucleus and have a cell wall (like a bacterium). However, the cell wall would be labeled differently (no peptidoglycan).

III. Prokaryotic Structures in Detail

Slide 12: Overview of Prokaryotic Structures (Cell Wall, Capsule, Appendages, Plasmids)

  • Cell Wall Composition:

    • Differs significantly between Bacteria and Archaea.

    • Also differs from eukaryotic cell walls (plants: cellulose; fungi/insects: chitin).

    • Function: Protective layer, responsible for organism's shape.

  • Capsule:

    • An outer layer found outside the cell wall in some bacteria (e.g., E. coli had an outer membrane, a capsule is yet another layer some might have).

    • Functions:

      • Enables attachment to surfaces.

      • Protects from dehydration.

      • Protects from attack by phagocytic cells (e.g., host immune cells).

      • Makes pathogens more resistant to host immune responses.

  • Other Structures (Variable Presence):

    • Flagella (singular: flagellum): Used for locomotion (motility).

    • Pili (singular: pilus): Used for attachment to surfaces.

  • Plasmids:

    • Small, circular, extra-chromosomal DNA molecules (not part of the main bacterial/archaeal genome).

    • Present in many species of Bacteria and Archaea.

    • Can carry a few genes, often conferring advantages like antibiotic resistance or new metabolic capabilities.

    • [Instructor Note] Used extensively in molecular biology for genetic engineering (introducing genes of interest into bacteria for amplification).

  • Phylum Proteobacteria: Mentioned as one of up to 52 bacterial phyla, further subdivided into five classes (Alpha through Epsilon).

Slide 13: The Plasma Membrane - Differences in Lipids

  • General Prokaryotic Plasma Membrane:

    • A thin lipid bilayer (6-8 nanometers thick).

    • Completely surrounds the cell, separating internal contents from the external environment.

    • Selectively permeable: Controls passage of substances, keeping ions, proteins, etc., inside while allowing others to move through.

  • Recall: General cell membrane structure is a phospholipid bilayer.

  • Key Differences in Archaeal Cell Membranes (Figure 2):

    1. Lipid Chains:

      • Bacteria & Eukarya: Use fatty acids (typically linear chains) linked to glycerol.

      • Archaea: Use isoprene (phytanyl) chains linked to glycerol. These phytanyl chains are branched.

    2. Linkage to Glycerol:

      • Bacteria & Eukarya: Fatty acids are linked to glycerol via an ester bond.

      • Archaea: Phytanyl chains are linked to glycerol via an ether bond.

    3. Membrane Structure:

      • Bacteria & Eukarya: Membranes are lipid bilayers.

      • Archaea: Some archaeal membranes are lipid monolayers instead of bilayers. This can provide greater stability in extreme environments.

  • Image Description (Figure 2):

    • Top: "Phospholipid from Archaea" showing a branched phytanyl sidechain, an O-P-O (phosphate group), and the C-O-C ether linkage to glycerol.

    • Middle: "Phospholipid from Bacteria and Eukarya" showing a linear fatty acid chain, an O-P-O (phosphate group), and the C-O-C=O ester linkage to glycerol.

    • Bottom: Diagram comparing a "Phospholipid bilayer from Bacteria and Eukarya" (two layers of lipids) with a "Phospholipid bilayer from Archaea" (which can also be a monolayer, though depicted here as a bilayer of their unique lipids, potentially implying some can form bilayers too, or it's a simplified representation before mentioning monolayers). The text clarifies some are monolayers.

Slide 14: The Cell Wall - Function and Composition

  • Osmotic Pressure: Prokaryotic cytoplasm has a high concentration of dissolved solutes, leading to relatively high internal osmotic pressure.

  • Cell Wall Functions:

    • Protective layer.

    • Gives shape and rigidity.

    • Located outside the cell membrane.

    • Prevents osmotic lysis (bursting due to excessive water uptake and increased internal volume).

  • Chemical Composition Variability:

    • Varies between Archaea and Bacteria.

    • Also varies between different bacterial species.

  • Bacterial Cell Walls:

    • Contain peptidoglycan.

    • Peptidoglycan structure: Polysaccharide chains cross-linked by unusual peptides.

      • These peptides contain both L-amino acids and D-amino acids (e.g., D-glutamic acid, D-alanine).

      • [Foundational Knowledge Refresh] Proteins in most organisms (including eukaryotes) normally contain only L-amino acids.

      • Significance: The presence of D-amino acids is a target for some antibiotics, which can mimic D-amino acids and interfere with bacterial cell wall synthesis.

    • Over 100 different forms of peptidoglycan exist.

  • S-layer (Surface Layer) Proteins:

    • Found on the outside of cell walls in both Archaea and Bacteria.

Slides 15-16: Gram Staining and Bacterial Cell Wall Types

  • Two Major Groups of Bacteria (based on Gram stain):

    1. Gram-positive bacteria: Stain positive (retain the crystal violet stain, appearing purple).

    2. Gram-negative bacteria: Stain negative (do not retain crystal violet, are counterstained pink/red).

  • Gram Staining Method: Named after its inventor, Danish scientist Hans Christian Gram (1853-1938).

  • Basis of Differential Staining: Differences in cell wall structure.

  • Gram-Positive Bacteria (Figure 3, Left):

    • Typically lack an outer membrane.

    • Have a thick cell wall composed of up to 90% peptidoglycan.

    • Contain teichoic acids (acidic substances in the cell wall).

      • Lipoteichoic acids: Teichoic acids covalently linked to lipids in the plasma membrane; they anchor the cell wall to the cell membrane.

    • All Gram-positive bacteria belong to one phylum (as stated on slide 15, though slide 11 lists "Gram-Positive bacteria" as a major group, implying a phylum or super-phylum level).

  • Gram-Negative Bacteria (Figure 3, Right):

    • Have a relatively thin cell wall with only a few layers of peptidoglycan (about 10% of the total cell wall).

    • Possess an outer membrane (outer envelope) surrounding the peptidoglycan layer.

      • This outer membrane contains lipopolysaccharides (LPS) and lipoproteins.

      • LPS is a key feature of the Gram-negative outer membrane and can act as an endotoxin.

      • The outer membrane is sometimes referred to as a second lipid bilayer, but its chemistry is very different from the plasma membrane.

    • Periplasmic space: The region between the inner (plasma) membrane and the outer membrane, containing the thin peptidoglycan layer.

    • Porins: Proteins embedded in the outer membrane that form channels, allowing the passage of certain substances.

    • Bacteria in other phyla (e.g., Proteobacteria, Chlamydias, Spirochetes, Cyanobacteria) are Gram-negative.

  • Image Description (Figure 3):

    • Side-by-side comparison of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacterial cell wall structures.

    • Gram-positive: Cytoplasm -> Plasma membrane -> Thick peptidoglycan layer (with lipoteichoic acid anchoring it).

    • Gram-negative: Cytoplasm -> Inner (plasma) membrane -> Periplasmic space (containing thin peptidoglycan layer) -> Outer membrane (with lipopolysaccharide, lipoprotein, porins).

Slide 17: Archaean Cell Walls

  • Key Feature: Archaean cell walls do NOT contain peptidoglycan.

  • Four Different Types of Archaean Cell Walls:

    1. Pseudopeptidoglycan (or pseudomurein): Similar in morphology (structure) to peptidoglycan but contains different sugars in its polysaccharide chains (e.g., N-acetyltalosaminuronic acid instead of N-acetylmuramic acid) and different peptide cross-links.

    2. Polysaccharides: Composed of complex sugars.

    3. Glycoproteins: Composed of proteins with attached carbohydrate chains (often forming S-layers).

    4. Pure Protein: Cell wall made primarily of protein (S-layers).

  • Other Differences (referencing Table 4): Features related to DNA replication, transcription, and translation in Archaea show similarities to those processes in Eukaryotes.

Slide 18: IN SUMMARY: ARCHAEA VS. BACTERIA (Table 4)

Slide 19: Recap of Structural Differences (Membranes & Walls)

  • Lipid Composition: Bacteria and Archaea differ in the lipid composition of their cell membranes.

    • Archaeal membranes: phytanyl units (branched isoprenoids) instead of fatty acids, linked to glycerol by ether bonds. Some are lipid monolayers.

  • Cell Wall Location & Function: Located outside the cell membrane, prevents osmotic lysis. Chemical composition varies.

  • Peptidoglycan:

    • Bacterial cell walls contain peptidoglycan.

    • Archaean cell walls do NOT contain peptidoglycan (may have pseudopeptidoglycan, polysaccharides, glycoproteins, or protein-based walls).

IV. Prokaryotic Diversity: Size, Shape, Movement, Nutrition, Metabolism, and Reproduction

Slide 21: Prokaryotes: Size, Shape, and Movement

  • Size: Range from 1 to 5 micrometers (µm). This makes them much smaller than most eukaryotic cells.

  • Shape (Three Basic Shapes Illustrated):

    • Bacilli (singular: bacillus): Rod-shaped. (Image: micrograph of rod-shaped bacteria).

    • Cocci (singular: coccus): Spherical. (Image: micrograph of spherical bacteria, likely staphylococci or streptococci).

    • Spirilla (singular: spirillum): Spiral or corkscrew-shaped. (Image: micrograph of spiral-shaped bacteria).

  • Movement:

    • None: Some prokaryotes are non-motile.

    • Propelled by flagella: Many use flagella for swimming.

    • Glide on secretions: Some can glide slowly along surfaces using slime-like material they secrete.

Slide 22: Prokaryotes: Nutrition and Metabolism (Energy Capture)

  • Energy Needs: Prokaryotes need a supply of chemical energy, often stored as fuel molecules like sugars. They obtain energy in diverse ways. Some can switch methods depending on environmental conditions.

  • Modes of Nutrition (How Energy is Captured):

    • Heterotroph ("other feeder"):

      • How Energy is Captured: Take in organic molecules from the environment or other organisms to use as both an energy source and a carbon supply.

      • Habitat: Wide range of environments.

    • Photoheterotroph ("light and other feeder"):

      • How Energy is Captured: Like basic heterotrophs (take in organic molecules), but also use light energy.

      • Habitat: Where light is plentiful.

    • Photoautotroph ("light self-feeder"):

      • How Energy is Captured: Use light energy to convert inorganic carbon (CO2) into organic carbon compounds (carbon fixation).

      • Habitat: Where light is plentiful.

      • [Instructor Q&A in transcript confirms this definition for a prokaryote using only light as an energy source.]

    • Chemoautotroph ("chemical self-feeder"):

      • How Energy is Captured: Use energy released by chemical reactions involving inorganic substances (e.g., ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, iron).

      • Habitat: Chemically harsh and/or dark environments (e.g., deep in the ocean, in thick mud, in digestive tracts of animals, in boiling hot springs).

Slide 23: Prokaryotes: Nutrition and Metabolism (Energy Release)

  • Energy Release Mechanisms: Energy is released from fuel molecules during cellular respiration, fermentation, or both.

  • Modes of Metabolism (How Energy is Released):

    • Obligate Aerobe ("requiring oxygen"):

      • How Energy is Released: Cellular respiration; must have a ready supply of O2 to release fuel energy.

      • Habitat: Oxygen-rich environments (e.g., near water surface, in animal lungs).

    • Obligate Anaerobe ("requiring a lack of oxygen"):

      • How Energy is Released: Fermentation; die in the presence of oxygen.

      • Habitat: Environments lacking O2 (e.g., deep soil, animal intestines, airtight containers). (Image: canned food, an airtight container).

    • Facultative Anaerobe ("surviving without oxygen when necessary"):

      • How Energy is Released: Can use either cellular respiration (if O2 is present) or fermentation (if O2 is absent) as necessary.

      • Habitat: Oxygen-rich or oxygen-poor environments.

    • [Instructor Q&A in transcript asks which modes use fermentation: Obligate anaerobes and facultative anaerobes.]

Slides 24-25: Prokaryotes: Reproduction and Change

  • Asexual Reproduction: Binary Fission

    • When a prokaryote has grown (nearly doubled in size) and replicated its DNA, it divides in half, producing two genetically identical daughter cells.

    • This process is called binary fission.

    • It does not involve the exchange or recombination of genetic information.

    • Under favorable conditions, prokaryotes can grow and divide at astonishing rates (e.g., some divide as often as every 20 minutes).

  • Mechanisms for Evolutionary Change (Genetic Variation):

    • Since prokaryotes reproduce asexually, how do their populations evolve?

      1. Mutation:

        • Random changes in DNA sequence.

        • Mutations are one of the main ways prokaryotes evolve.

        • Mutations are inherited by daughter cells produced by binary fission.

      2. Conjugation (Horizontal Gene Transfer):

        • Many prokaryotes exchange genetic information through conjugation.

        • A hollow bridge (pilus) forms between two bacterial cells.

        • Genetic material, usually in the form of a plasmid, moves from one cell (donor) to the other (recipient).

        • Many plasmids carry genes that enable bacteria to survive in new environments or resist antibiotics.

        • This transfer of genetic information increases genetic diversity in prokaryotic populations.

        • Image (Slide 24, left): Micrograph showing conjugation between bacterial cells.

    • Important Note: Mutation and conjugation are not forms of reproduction; they do not result in the formation of new daughter cells but rather alter the genetic makeup of existing cells or their descendants.

  • Survival Strategy: Endospores

    • When growth conditions become unfavorable, many prokaryotic cells (primarily certain types of bacteria like Bacillus and Clostridium) form an endospore.

    • An endospore is a thick internal wall that encloses the DNA and a portion of the cytoplasm.

    • Endospores are highly resistant to harsh conditions (heat, desiccation, radiation, chemicals).

    • They can remain dormant for months or even years.

    • The ability to form endospores allows some prokaryotes to survive very harsh conditions and germinate when conditions become favorable again.

    • Image (Slide 24, right): Micrograph showing an endospore within a bacterial cell.

V. The Role of Prokaryotes in the Living World

Slides 26-27: Decomposers

  • Core Role: Prokaryotes (especially bacteria and some archaea) are vital decomposers in ecosystems.

    • They assist in breaking down dead organic matter (dead organisms, waste products).

    • By doing so, they recycle matter and supply raw materials (nutrients) back into the environment, making them available for other organisms.

  • Industrial Applications:

    • Bacterial decomposers are essential in industrial sewage treatment, helping to produce purified water and chemicals that can be used as fertilizers.

  • Examples of Decomposing/Recycling Prokaryotes (Images on Slide 26):

    • Bacteria of the genus Rhizobium: (Also a nitrogen fixer, see below). Often involved in decomposition in soil.

    • Cyanobacteria of the genus Anabaena: While primarily producers, they also contribute to nutrient cycling upon death and decomposition. Anabaena form filamentous chains in ponds and other aquatic environments where they perform photosynthesis.

    • Bacteria called Actinomycetes: Abundant in soil and in rotting plant material (e.g., fallen logs). They decompose complex organic molecules into simpler molecules.

  • Ecological Disruption (Negative Impact):

    • The growth of organisms like bacteria can also disrupt ecosystem health.

    • Example: When an oversupply of nutrients (e.g., from fertilizer runoff) enters freshwater lakes, it can cause an explosive growth of algae and other producers (an algal bloom).

    • As these organisms die off, bacterial decomposers flourish, consuming large amounts of organic matter.

    • If this happens to excess, the bacteria can use up virtually all the dissolved oxygen in the water.

    • This oxygen depletion can lead to massive fish kills and further disrupt the ecosystem (a process called eutrophication).

Slide 28: Producers

  • Core Role: Prokaryotes, particularly photosynthetic prokaryotes, are among the most important producers on the planet. They form the base of many food webs.

    • Producers convert inorganic substances (like CO2) into organic compounds (food), often using light energy.

  • Example: Prochlorococcus

    • A tiny cyanobacterium.

    • May account for more than half of the primary production (production of organic compounds from CO2) in the open ocean.

  • Significance: Food chains everywhere are dependent upon prokaryotes as producers of food and biomass.

  • Image: A serene pond scene, representing an ecosystem reliant on producers.

Slides 29-30: Nitrogen Fixers

  • The Nitrogen Problem: Nitrogen gas (N2) makes up about 80% of Earth's atmosphere, but most organisms cannot use N2 directly.

  • Prokaryotic Solution: Nitrogen Fixation

    • Only a few organisms, all of them prokaryotes (certain bacteria and archaea), can convert atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into useful forms like ammonia (NH3). This process is called nitrogen fixation.

    • Ammonia can then be converted to nitrates (NO3-), which plants can absorb and use to make proteins and nucleic acids.

  • Quantitative Importance: Nitrogen-fixing bacteria and archaea provide approximately 90% of the nitrogen used by other organisms in ecosystems.

  • Other Nitrogen Sources: The remaining 10% comes from nitrogen-containing compounds from weathering rocks and from atmospheric processes like lightning (which can combine oxygen and nitrogen).

  • Symbiotic Nitrogen Fixation:

    • Some plants (especially legumes like clover, soybeans, peas) have symbiotic relationships with nitrogen-fixing prokaryotes.

    • Example: Bacteria of the genus Rhizobium grow in nodules (knobs) on the roots of legume plants.

    • Inside these nodules, Rhizobium bacteria convert N2 from the air into nitrogen compounds essential for plant growth.

    • [Instructor Analogy] In effect, these plants have "fertilizer factories in their roots."

  • Human/Animal Nitrogen Acquisition: Humans and other animals get the nitrogen they need to make proteins by eating plants or by eating animals that have eaten plants.

  • Image (Slide 29): A plant root system with prominent root nodules (circled in red), which house nitrogen-fixing bacteria.

VI. It Takes Teamwork: How Endosymbiosis Changed Life on Earth (Case Study)

(This section is based on slides 33-47, which appear to be derived from an external article by the "Understanding Evolution team." The instructor presented this as part of the lecture.)

Slide 33-34: Kwang Jeon's Amoebae and the X-bacteria: An Accidental Experiment

  • The Event (1966): Microbiologist Kwang Jeon was studying amoebae when they were infected by X-bacteria.

    • Thousands of X-bacteria invaded each amoeba cell, making them sick. Only a few amoebae survived.

  • Unexpected Outcome: Several months later, surviving amoebae and their descendants were healthy, even though the X-bacteria were still thriving inside them. The bacteria no longer made the amoebae sick.

  • A Deeper Dependence:

    • When Jeon used antibiotics to kill the X-bacteria inside an amoeba, the host amoeba also died.

    • Discovery: The X-bacteria were producing a protein that the amoebae needed to survive.

  • Shift in Relationship: The relationship transformed from attack/defense (parasitism) to cooperation (mutualism).

  • Key Questions from this Case Study:

    1. What is endosymbiosis?

    2. What role did endosymbiosis play in the evolution of eukaryotes?

    3. How does endosymbiosis change our view of the branching pattern on the tree of life?

Slide 35: Defining Symbiosis

  • Symbiosis: A close and long-term interaction between two or more different biological species.

  • Three Kinds of Symbiosis:

    1. Mutualism: A symbiosis in which both organisms benefit. (e.g., Jeon's amoeba and X-bacteria eventually).

    2. Commensalism: A symbiosis in which one organism benefits without helping or harming the other.

    3. Parasitism: A symbiosis in which one organism (the parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (the host). (e.g., initial X-bacteria infection).

Slide 36: When Two Become One - The Path to Integration

  • Jeon's amoebae and X-bacteria worked together for mutual benefit but remained separate organisms (dividing independently, using their own genes mostly).

  • Hypothesis: Over many years of evolving together, such closely associated cells could become a single integrated organism with a common set of genes and proteins. A future scientist might not guess such an "amoebacterium" was once two distinct organisms.

  • Natural "Merging": Evidence suggests that the merging of two simple organisms has happened naturally in evolutionary history.

    • Two cells formed a symbiotic team.

    • Over millions of years, they evolved into a single organism.

    • The result of such a union was the first eukaryotic cell.

    • [Key Statement] "We humans owe our existence to two bacteria that teamed up in a symbiotic relationship over a billion years ago!"

Slide 37: From Prokaryotes to Eukaryotes - The Domains of Life

  • Recap of Domains:

    • Archaea & Bacteria (Prokaryotes): Small, relatively simple cells, membrane, cell wall, circular DNA.

    • Eukaryota: More complex cells, DNA is linear and in a nucleus.

  • Mitochondria: Eukaryotic cells have "personal power plants" called mitochondria.

    • These organelles produce chemical energy.

    • They also hold the key to understanding the evolution of the eukaryotic cell.

  • Image: "Typical prokaryote cell" (strand of DNA) vs. "Typical eukaryote cell" (nucleus containing DNA, mitochondria). Tree showing EUKARYOTA, BACTERIA, ARCHAEA.

Slide 38: Endosymbiosis in a Nutshell - The Origin of Eukaryotic Complexity

  • The complex eukaryotic cell allowed for the evolution of multicellular organisms.

  • How did the eukaryotic cell evolve? The answer seems to be symbiosis (teamwork).

  • Endosymbiotic Theory: Eukaryotic cells are descendants of separate prokaryotic cells that joined in a symbiotic union.

    • Mitochondrion: Believed to be the descendant ("great-great...granddaughter") of a free-living bacterium that was engulfed by another (host) cell (perhaps as food, or as an invader) and became a permanent resident.

      • Host cell benefited: From the chemical energy (ATP) produced by the mitochondrion.

      • Mitochondrion benefited: From the protected, nutrient-rich environment inside the host.

  • Definition: Endosymbiosis

    • A type of "internal" symbiosis where one organism takes up permanent residence inside another.

    • Eventually, this arrangement evolves into a single, new lineage or organism.

  • Diagram: "Endosymbiosis in a nutshell"

    1. Start with two independent bacteria.

    2. One bacterium engulfs the other.

    3. One bacterium now lives inside the other.

    4. Both bacteria benefit from the arrangement.

    5. The internal bacteria are passed on from generation to generation (when the host cell divides).

Slides 39-40: Evidence for Endosymbiosis (Mitochondria & Chloroplasts)

  • Lynn Margulis: First proposed the case for endosymbiosis in the 1960s; faced initial skepticism.

  • Evidence (Striking Similarities between Prokaryotes like Bacteria and Mitochondria):

    1. Membranes: Mitochondria have their own cell membranes (typically two: an outer membrane and a highly folded inner membrane), similar in composition to prokaryotic membranes.

    2. DNA: Each mitochondrion has its own circular DNA genome, like a bacterium's genome (though much smaller).

      • This mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) is separate from the host cell's nuclear DNA.

      • mtDNA is passed from a mitochondrion to its offspring mitochondria.

    3. Reproduction: Mitochondria multiply by pinching in half (binary fission), the same process used by bacteria.

      • Every new mitochondrion must be produced from a parent mitochondrion.

      • If a cell's mitochondria are removed, it cannot build new ones from scratch (the nuclear genes don't code for a complete, de novo synthesis pathway).

  • Conclusion: Endosymbiosis is the best explanation for the evolution of the eukaryotic cell.

  • Chloroplasts: The evidence for endosymbiosis also applies to chloroplasts (organelles in plant and algal cells responsible for photosynthesis).

    • Chloroplasts are like "tiny green factories" converting sunlight into sugars.

    • They share many similarities with mitochondria (e.g., own DNA, ribosomes, double membrane, reproduce by fission).

    • Evidence suggests chloroplasts were also once free-living photosynthetic bacteria (likely cyanobacteria).

Slide 41: Timing of Endosymbiotic Events and Genome Integration

  • Mitochondrial Endosymbiosis: Must have happened early in the history of eukaryotes, because all eukaryotes (or their ancestors) have or had mitochondria.

  • Chloroplast Endosymbiosis: Happened later, in some eukaryotic cells, creating the lineage that led to algae and plants.

  • Diagram: A phylogenetic tree showing:

    • Prokaryotes (archaea, bacteria) branching off.

    • An early eukaryotic lineage acquiring mitochondria.

    • A subsequent branch (leading to plants and some algae) acquiring chloroplasts.

    • Animals and fungi branch off after mitochondria acquisition but before (or independently of) chloroplast acquisition.

  • Loss of Independence: Mitochondria and chloroplasts are no longer free-living bacteria.

    • The first eukaryotic cell evolved over a billion years ago.

    • These organelles have become completely dependent on their host cells.

    • Many key proteins needed by the mitochondrion/chloroplast are encoded by nuclear genes and imported from the host cell's cytoplasm.

  • Gene Transfer: Over their long-standing relationship, many genes originally in the organelle's genome were transferred to the host's nuclear genome.

  • Irreversible Step: Scientists consider this mixing of genomes (gene transfer and co-dependence) to be the irreversible step at which the two independent organisms became a single, integrated individual.

Slide 42: WEIRD FACT - Paramecium bursaria Packs a Lunch

  • Paramecium bursaria: A single-celled eukaryote (a ciliate) found in pond water.

  • It may not have its own chloroplasts, but it "borrows" them by swallowing photosynthetic green algae and storing them instead of digesting them.

  • The Paramecium can become green due to the packed algae.

  • Mutualism:

    • When P. bursaria swims into light, the algae photosynthesize sugar, and both cells share the "lunch."

    • P. bursaria doesn't just exploit the algae; it actively "chauffeurs" them to well-lit areas.

    • It also shares food it finds with its algae if they are forced to live in the dark.

  • Image: A micrograph of P. bursaria appearing green due to ingested algae.

Slide 43: Finding Our Roots - Unanswered Questions and Genetic Clues

  • Unanswered Questions about Endosymbiosis:

    • How did the first endosymbiont get inside its host?

    • Was the host trying to eat it? If so, why wasn't it digested?

    • Or was the endosymbiont trying to infect/exploit the host (like the X-bacteria initially)?

  • Genetic Clues: Knowing which species of bacteria (or archaea) came together could help answer these questions.

    • Biologists compare gene sequences of different organisms.

    • Principle: The more similar two organisms' genes are, the more recently their lineages split from a common ancestor.

    • Distant common ancestors = more accumulated differences in gene sequences.

    • This allows reconstruction of "family trees" (phylogenies).

  • Diagram: A simple phylogenetic tree showing organisms A, B, C, D.

    • C & D: DNA 95% similar (most closely related).

    • B & D: DNA 90% similar.

    • A & D: DNA 80% similar (most distantly related of these comparisons to D).

Slide 44: Mitochondrial Ancestry and the Archaeal Host

  • Mitochondrial DNA Studies:

    1. All mitochondria are descended from one original mitochondrion (i.e., the endosymbiotic event leading to mitochondria happened only once in evolutionary history).

    2. Mitochondria are most closely related to a group of bacteria called Alpha-proteobacteria, specifically bacteria like Rickettsia prowazekii.

      • Rickettsia prowazekii is a parasitic bacterium that causes epidemic typhus and is transmitted by fleas.

  • The Host Cell's Ancestry:

    • Genomic studies reveal that eukaryotic "host" cells have much in common (genomically) with the Domain Archaea.

    • Archaea were undiscovered until 1977 due to their extremophilic nature (living in Antarctica, Dead Sea, deep-sea vents, hot springs, sewage sludge).

    • It's hypothesized that an archaeon (a member of Archaea) may have been the first endosymbiotic host that engulfed the bacterium that became the mitochondrion. This archaeon would be our (eukaryotic) ancestor.

Slide 45: WEIRD FACT - Hot in Here: Archaea Like it Extreme

  • Extremophiles: Organisms that thrive in extreme conditions. Archaea are prime examples.

  • Examples of Archaeal Extremophily:

    • Solutions more acidic than vinegar.

    • Environments as basic as ammonia.

    • Incredible heat tolerance: many species live at temperatures above the boiling point of water (100°C).

  • How do they survive such conditions (when their cells are made of membranes, proteins, DNA, like ours)?

    • Archaea and other extremophiles make specialized salts and proteins.

    • These compounds act like "antifreeze," keeping the cell's metabolism from freezing up or breaking down under hostile conditions.

  • Implications:

    • Archaea demonstrate that life could have existed on early Earth when it was volcanically active and boiling hot.

    • If life is ever found on other planets, it might resemble extremophiles like Archaea.

Slide 46: How Important is Endosymbiosis? Beyond Mitochondria and Chloroplasts

  • Endosymbiosis clearly explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts.

  • Could it explain other eukaryotic features? Maybe.

    • Endosymbiotic origins have been suggested for:

      • Flagella (e.g., sperm tail-like structures)

      • Cilia (hair-like structures for locomotion or moving fluid)

      • Even the Nucleus (the cell's command center)

    • However, these are still actively debated, and the "jury is out" pending more evidence.

  • Lynn Margulis's Broader View:

    • Emphasized that symbiosis has played a role in life's history not just once or twice, but over and over again.

    • Proposed that the Tree of Life is not just about branches separating (diverging) but also about branches coming together again (converging) many times as different species form symbiotic relationships and create new organisms.

    • This forms an interconnected tree of life where organisms can have multiple ancestors, even from different domains.

    • Eukaryotic Ancestry: As eukaryotes, our ancestors include both the bacterium that became mitochondria and the archaeobacterium that was the host cell.

  • Image: Diagrams of a flagellum, cilia, and a nucleus.

Slide 47: Why Cooperate? The Evolutionary Rationale for Symbiosis

  • Why have endosymbiosis and symbiosis been so important? Why cooperate at all?

    • The answer points to a basic process of evolution: natural selection.

  • Natural Selection (Darwin): Organisms fit enough to succeed in survival have a good chance of passing their genes to the next generation.

    • Any survival or reproductive advantage can help a species out-compete others or avoid extinction.

  • The Eukaryotic Advantage:

    • It seems likely that the first eukaryotic cells (the host + mitochondrion) gained a slight edge over their neighbors because the mitochondrion provided a rich source of energy.

  • Mutual Dependence and Success:

    • Like Kwang Jeon's X-bacteria and amoebae, the mitochondria and their host cells relied more and more on each other to survive.

    • Eventually, neither could succeed alone.

    • But as a team, they produced millions of descendants, establishing a whole new domain of life (Eukarya).

  • Image: "The tree of life grows by branching... ...and by converging." Shows a stylized tree with branches that also merge.

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 2: Prokaryotes)

  • Prokaryotes are unicellular organisms lacking a nucleus, with DNA in the cytoplasm. They comprise two domains: Bacteria and Archaea.

  • Bacteria vs. Archaea - Key Differences:

    • Cell Wall: Bacteria have peptidoglycan; Archaea do not.

    • Cell Membrane Lipids: Bacteria have ester-linked fatty acids; Archaea have ether-linked phytanyl chains (can form monolayers).

    • Genetics: Archaeal DNA replication, transcription, and translation mechanisms show more similarity to Eukaryotes.

    • Habitat: Archaea are often extremophiles.

  • Bacterial Cell Walls: Can be Gram-positive (thick peptidoglycan, teichoic acids) or Gram-negative (thin peptidoglycan, outer membrane with LPS).

  • Prokaryotic Diversity: Vary in size (1-5 µm), shape (bacilli, cocci, spirilla), movement (flagella, gliding, non-motile), and metabolism.

  • Nutrition & Metabolism:

    • Energy Capture: Heterotrophs, photoheterotrophs, photoautotrophs, chemoautotrophs.

    • Energy Release: Obligate aerobes (respiration), obligate anaerobes (fermentation), facultative anaerobes (either).

  • Reproduction & Genetic Change:

    • Binary Fission: Asexual reproduction.

    • Evolutionary Mechanisms: Mutation, Conjugation (horizontal gene transfer via plasmids).

    • Endospores: Dormant, resistant structures for survival in harsh conditions.

  • Ecological Roles of Prokaryotes:

    • Decomposers: Recycle nutrients from dead organic matter.

    • Producers: Photosynthetic prokaryotes (e.g., cyanobacteria) form the base of food webs.

    • Nitrogen Fixers: Convert atmospheric N2 into usable forms (e.g., Rhizobium).

  • Endosymbiotic Theory: Explains the origin of eukaryotic cells.

    • Mitochondria and chloroplasts are descendants of free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by an ancestral host cell (likely an archaeon for mitochondria).

    • Evidence: Organelles have their own DNA (circular), ribosomes, membranes, and reproduce by fission.

    • Gene transfer from organelle to host nucleus solidified the symbiotic relationship.

    • Symbiosis (cooperation) provided evolutionary advantages, leading to the Eukarya domain.

I. Recap: Prokaryotic Cell Structure (Slides 2-32)

This section serves as a review and detailed elaboration of prokaryotic cell characteristics, building upon concepts likely introduced in the previous lecture.

Slide 2: Definition of Prokaryote

  • Core Concept: Prokaryote

    • A prokaryote is typically a unicellular microorganism that lacks a distinct nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.

    • Historically classified under Kingdom Prokaryotae (synonym: Monera).

    • Modern classification places prokaryotes into two distinct domains: Domain Bacteria and Domain Archaea.

  • Key Distinction:

    • Prokaryotic cells lack internal membrane-bound cellular bodies (organelles).

    • Eukaryotic cells possess these organelles.

  • Examples:

    • Prokaryotes: Bacteria and Archaea.

    • Eukaryotes: Protists, Fungi, Plants, and Animals (everything except prokaryotes).

Slide 3: What are examples of prokaryotes?

  • Domains: Prokaryotes are split into two domains: Archaea and Bacteria.

  • Cyanobacteria:

    • Sometimes historically considered algae, but they are actually bacteria (prokaryotic).

    • The term "algae" is now generally reserved for eukaryotic organisms (which will be discussed later as protists).

    • Cyanobacteria derive their energy through photosynthesis.

    • Crucially, they lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles like chloroplasts. Photosynthesis occurs using pigments and specialized membrane infoldings within the cytoplasm, not in discrete chloroplasts.

  • Image Description: A diagram of a eukaryotic plant cell is shown on the slide, likely for contrast or to highlight the chloroplasts (circled in red) which cyanobacteria (as prokaryotes) lack, even though they photosynthesize. The diagram labels typical eukaryotic plant cell features: Cell wall, Membrane, Ribosomes, Nucleus, Nucleolus, Vacuole, Chloroplast, Mitochondrion, Endoplasmic reticulum, Cytoplasm.

Slide 4: Components of a prokaryotic cell

  • Four Common Components Shared by All Cells (including prokaryotes):

    1. A plasma membrane: An outer covering that separates the cell's interior from its surrounding environment.

    2. Cytoplasm: A jelly-like cytosol within the cell where other cellular components are found.

    3. DNA: The genetic material of the cell.

    4. Ribosomes: Sites where protein synthesis occurs.

  • Some Other Components or Parts Found in Many Prokaryotes:

    • Pili (singular: pilus): Hair-like appendages.

    • Flagella (singular: flagellum): Whip-like tails for motility.

    • Fimbriae (singular: fimbria): Shorter, bristle-like fibers.

    • Capsule: An outer protective layer found in some prokaryotes.

    • Cell wall: A rigid layer outside the plasma membrane.

    • Mesosome: Infoldings of the plasma membrane.

    • Chromatophore: Pigment-containing structures for photosynthesis in some prokaryotes.

  • Instructor Elaboration (from transcript): Flagella function in motility; pili allow clinging to surfaces. The cell typically has three layers: inner plasma membrane, cell wall, and sometimes an outer capsule.

Slide 6: The Nucleoid

  • Core Concept: The Nucleoid

    • The nucleoid (meaning "nucleus-like") is an irregularly shaped region within the prokaryotic cell that contains all or most of the genetic material.

    • Most prokaryotes, like E. coli, contain a single circular DNA molecule that constitutes their entire genome.

    • Key Difference from Eukaryotic Nucleus: The nucleoid is not surrounded by a nuclear membrane (envelope).

  • DNA Organization:

    • Prokaryotic DNA and DNA-associated proteins are concentrated within the nucleoid region.

    • Prokaryotic DNA interacts with Nucleoid-Associated Proteins (NAPs), which assist in the organization and packaging of the chromosome.

    • NAPs Function: Similar to histones in eukaryotic cells, NAPs act as DNA-organizing proteins, helping to compact the DNA.

      • [Foundational Knowledge Refresh from transcript]: Histones in eukaryotes provide a scaffold around which DNA wraps, allowing it to occupy a more compacted form and less space within the nucleus.

Slide 8: Plasmids

  • Core Concept: Plasmids

    • Prokaryotic cells may contain extrachromosomal DNA (DNA that is not part of the main chromosome).

    • This extrachromosomal DNA is found in plasmids, which are small, circular, double-stranded DNA molecules.

  • Occurrence:

    • More commonly found in bacteria.

    • Also found in archaea and even some eukaryotic organisms (e.g., yeast, as mentioned in transcript).

  • Function & Importance:

    • Plasmids often carry genes that confer advantageous traits, such as antibiotic resistance.

    • Thus, they are important for the survival of the organism in certain environments.

  • Abundance: Cells that have plasmids can often contain hundreds of them within a single cell.

Slide 9: Ribosomes (General)

  • Core Concept: Ribosomes

    • Structures responsible for protein synthesis in all cellular life.

    • Location in Prokaryotes: Found in the cytoplasm.

    • Size Classification:

      • Prokaryotic ribosomes are called 70S ribosomes.

      • Eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S ribosomes.

      • S (Svedberg unit): A unit of size based on sedimentation rate in an ultracentrifuge, which depends on the molecule's size, shape, and surface qualities. It's a way to describe the "weight" or size of very large macromolecules.

        • [Instructor Explanation from transcript]: When cell lysates are centrifuged in a sucrose gradient, macromolecules separate based on their sedimentation properties. Larger/heavier molecules sediment in denser (lower) sucrose layers, lighter ones in less dense (upper) layers. The Svedberg unit reflects this.

  • Structural Differences: Ribosomes in each of the three domains (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya) are structurally different, though their fundamental function is conserved.

  • Essential for Life: [Instructor Emphasis] No living cell can survive without ribosomes. Even viruses rely on host cell ribosomes to replicate.

    2. Cellular Organization: (General cell difference) Prokaryotes lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; eukaryotes have both.

  • Ribosomes are also classified based on their location:

    • Bacterial Ribosomes: 70S, in bacterial cells.

    • Mitoribosomes: Found in mitochondria.

    • Nuclear Ribosome: Ribonucleoprotein (RNP) particles found in the nucleus.

    • Nucleolar Ribosomes: RNP particles found in the nucleolus.

    • Cytoplasmic Ribosomes: Large particles found in the cytoplasm (referring to the main 80S eukaryotic or 70S prokaryotic ones).

  • Core Concept: Endospores

    • Some genera of bacteria can transform from vegetative cells (actively growing and metabolizing) into endospores.

    • Endospores are structures that protect the bacterial genome in a dormant state when environmental conditions are unfavorable.

    • [Instructor Analogy from transcript]: Similar to spores or seeds in plants, containing enough material to give rise to a new individual when conditions improve.

Slide 15: Endospores (Sporulation Process)

  • Core Concept: Sporulation

    • The process by which vegetative cells transform into endospores.

    • Generally begins when nutrients become depleted or environmental conditions become otherwise unfavorable.

  • Process of Sporulation (Diagram & Text):

    1. DNA replicates. (Ensures genetic material for future cell).

    2. Membranes form around the DNA. (Asymmetric cell division begins, as per transcript).

    6. Spore is released. (Upon disintegration of the mother cell).

Slide 16: Plasma Membrane (General)

  • Cell Envelope: Structures that enclose the cytoplasm and internal structures of the cell are collectively known as the cell envelope.

  • Universality: All cells (prokaryotic and eukaryotic) have a plasma membrane (also called cytoplasmic membrane or cell membrane).

  • Selective Permeability: Exhibits selective permeability, allowing some molecules to enter/leave while restricting others.

  • Fluid Mosaic Model: The structure is often described by the fluid mosaic model.

  • Composition (Bacteria & Eukaryotes): The plasma membrane of most bacterial and eukaryotic cell types is a bilayer composed mainly of phospholipids formed with ester linkages and proteins. (Archaea differ, as discussed in previous lecture).

Slide 17: Plasma Membrane (Fluidity & Figure)

  • Mobility: Phospholipids and proteins within the membrane can move laterally (side-to-side) within the plane of the membrane and also between the two phospholipid layers (though trans-layer movement, or flip-flop, is much rarer for lipids and very rare for proteins without assistance).

Slide 18: Plasma Membrane (Glycoproteins/lipids & PLFA)

  • Glycoprotein and Glycolipid Complexes:

    • Extend out from the cell surface.

    • Allow the cell to interact with the external environment.

    • Vary considerably in chemical composition among archaea, bacteria, and eukaryotes, allowing scientists to use them to characterize unique species.

  • Phospholipid-Derived Fatty Acid Analysis (PLFA):

    • Plasma membranes contain unique phospholipids with specific fatty acids.

    • PLFA profiles (patterns of these fatty acids) can be used to identify unique types of cells.

    • Archaea, bacteria, and eukaryotes each have a unique PFLA profile.

Slide 19: Photosynthetic Membrane Structures [cell membrane modification]

  • Nomenclature:

    • In cyanobacteria, these membrane structures are called thylakoids.

    • In other photosynthetic bacteria, they are called chromatophores, lamellae, or chlorosomes.

  • Context: [Instructor Emphasis] Since prokaryotes lack membrane-bound organelles, they don't have chloroplasts. Photosynthesis occurs in these specialized membrane regions.

Slide 21: MESOSOMES [CELL MEMBRANE MODIFICATION]

  • Core Concept: Mesosomes

    • Mesosomes (or chondrioids) are folded invaginations (inward foldings) of the plasma membrane in bacteria.

    • They are formed by an extension of the plasma membrane into the cell.

    • These extensions can be in the form of vesicles, tubules, or lamellae.

  • Functions:

    1. Help in the synthesis of the cell wall and replication of DNA. Also aid in the equal distribution of chromosomes into daughter cells during division.

    2. Increase the surface area of the plasma membrane for various enzymatic activities.

    3. Help in secretion processes and bacterial respiration.

Slide 22: TYPES OF MESOSOMES

  • Two Main Types:

    1. Septal or Central Mesosome:

      • Helps in DNA replication and transfer of replicated DNA to daughter cells.

      • Helps in the formation of septa (cross-walls) during binary fission (the wall separating daughter cells).

    2. Peripheral or Lateral Mesosome (also called Chondroid):

      • Considered analogous to the mitochondria of eukaryotic cells.

      • Serves as a site for respiration in bacteria.

      • Contains enzymes for the electron transport chain (ETC).

      • Provides a large surface area for enzyme activity.

Slide 26: S-layer and Capsule Pathogenicity

  • S-layer:

    • Another type of cell envelope structure.

    • Composed of a mixture of structural proteins and glycoproteins.

    • In bacteria, S-layers are found outside the cell wall.

    • In some archaea, the S-layer serves as the cell wall itself.

    • Exact function is not entirely understood but may help the cell withstand osmotic pressure and, for certain pathogens, interact with the host immune system.

  • Capsule and Pathogenicity:

    • The ability to produce a capsule can contribute to a microbe's pathogenicity (ability to cause disease).

    • The capsule can make it more difficult for phagocytic cells (like white blood cells) to engulf and kill the microorganism.

    • Example: Streptococcus pneumoniae (causes pneumonia) produces a capsule that aids its pathogenicity.

Slide 27: FLAGELLA: Introduction

  • Core Concept: Flagella

    • Complex, filamentous cytoplasmic structures protruding through the cell wall.

    • Unbranched, long, thread-like structures.

    • Mostly composed of the protein flagellin.

    • Intricately embedded in the cell envelope.

  • Dimensions: About 12-30 nm in diameter and 5-16 µm in length.

  • Function: Responsible for bacterial motility (movement). Motility plays an important role in survival and the ability of certain bacteria to cause disease.

  • Core Concept: Fimbriae

    • Fimbria (Latin for 'fringe'; plural: fimbriae), also referred to as an "attachment pilus."

    • Type of appendage found on many Gram-negative and some Gram-positive bacteria.

    • Thinner and shorter than a flagellum.

  • Function: Used by bacteria to adhere to one another, to animal cells, and to some inanimate objects.

  • Characteristics:

    • A bacterium can have as many as 1,000 fimbriae.

    • Only visible with an electron microscope.

    • May be straight or flexible.

    • Made up of fimbrillin protein.

    • No role in bacterial motility and conjugation.

Slide 31: Pili

  • Pilin: A class of fibrous proteins found in pilus structures in bacteria. (Both fimbriae and other pili are made of types of pilin).

  • Bacterial Pili (General Term):

    • Longer pili (Sex Pili): Used in the exchange of genetic material during bacterial conjugation.

    • Shorter appendages (Fimbriae): Also made of pilin, used as a cell adhesion mechanism.

    • Not all bacteria have pili or fimbriae.

  • Characteristics of (Conjugation) Pili:

    • Typically longer and fewer in number than fimbriae.

    • Found in virtually all Gram-negative bacteria but not in many Gram-positive bacteria.

  • Structure: Fimbriae and pili have a shaft composed of a protein called pilin. At the end of the shaft is an adhesive tip structure that corresponds to specific glycoprotein or glycolipid receptors on a host cell.

  • Two Basic Types of Pili (Functional Classification):

    1. Short attachment pili (i.e., fimbriae)

    2. Long conjugation pili (i.e., sex pili)

  • Image Description: Diagram showing a bacterial pilus with an "adhesive tip" binding to a "receptor" on a "host cell."

Slide 32: Long Conjugation Pili / Sex Pili

  • Long conjugation pili (also called "F" pili or sex pili):

    • Are longer and very few in number compared to common pili (fimbriae/short attachment pili).

  • Function: The conjugation pilus enables conjugation, which is the transfer of DNA from one bacterium to another by cell-to-cell contact.

  • Mechanism in Gram-negative bacteria: Typically involves the transfer of DNA (often a plasmid) from a donor or "male bacterium" (with a sex pilus) to a recipient or "female bacterium."

  • Image Description:

    • Left: Diagram showing "Conjugation (Sex) Pilus" connecting a "male (genetic donor)" bacterium to a "female (genetic recipient)" bacterium.

    • Right: "Scanning electron micrograph of E.coli bacteria exchanging genes" via a long pilus.

Slide 39: Eukaryotic Organelles - Definitions

  • Nucleolus: Found inside of the nucleus; the part of eukaryotic cells where ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is produced and ribosomes are assembled (as per transcript).

  • Plasma membrane: A phospholipid bilayer that surrounds the entire cell and encompasses the organelles within.

  • Cytoskeleton: A system of protein fibers (e.g., microtubules, actin filaments as per transcript) and other molecules that gives shape to the cell, aiding in the correct positioning of organelles and cell movement.

  • Cell wall: Only found in certain eukaryotes (e.g., plant cells, fungi). A rigid covering that provides structural support and protection.

  • Ribosomes: Responsible for protein synthesis.

  • Mitochondria: Also known as the "powerhouses of the cell"; responsible for energy production (ATP through cellular respiration).

  • Cytoplasmic space: The region of the cell between the nuclear envelope and the plasma membrane.

  • Cytoplasm: Defined as the total inner-cellular volume, with the exception of the nucleus; includes the cytosol and all organelles.

  • Cytosol: The gel-like substance that accounts for all the material in the cytoplasm, excluding the contents of the various membrane-bound organelles.

  • Endoplasmic reticulum (ER): An organelle dedicated to protein maturation (folding, modification) and transportation.

  • Vesicles and vacuoles: Membrane-bound sacs involved in transportation and storage.

  • Other common organelles (found in many, but not all, eukaryotes): Golgi apparatus (complex), chloroplasts, and lysosomes.

V. Kingdom Protista (Slides 46-65)

Slide 46: KINGDOM PROTISTA - General Characteristics

  • Definition: Protists are eukaryotic microscopic organisms belonging to Kingdom Protista.

  • Cellular Organization:

    • Most are single-celled.

    • Some are multi-celled (but often lack complex tissue/organ organization found in plants/animals/fungi).

    • Some are colonial (clusters of cells).

  • Metabolism:

    • Some are more related to plant metabolism (autotrophic, e.g., algae).

    • Others are more related to heterotrophs (heterotrophic, like fungi/animals, obtaining food from external sources).

  • Reproduction: Primarily asexual reproduction through mitosis.

  • Transport: Occurs through the cell membrane.

  • Diversity: [Instructor Emphasis] Kingdom Protista contains the most diverse organisms of all kingdoms. It's often called the "miscellaneous drawer" of the tree of life because its members don't fit neatly into the other eukaryotic kingdoms (Plants, Animals, Fungi).

    • They may share some characteristics with plants, animals, or fungi, but always possess some property that excludes them from those kingdoms.

  • Size: Members can be microscopic or very large (e.g., kelp).

  • No "Typical" Protist: There is no single organism that represents a "typical" protist due to their immense diversity.

  • Shared Characteristic: [Instructor Emphasis] The one characteristic shared by ALL protists is that, unlike bacteria, they are all eukaryotes (i.e., their cells contain a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles).

Slide 47: Types of protists

  • Protists are broadly categorized based on their general characteristics, often resembling other kingdoms:

    1. Protozoa (Animal-like protists):

      • Resemble animals in the way they get food (heterotrophic, often ingestive).

      • All are unicellular.

    2. Algae (Plant-like protists):

      • Perform photosynthesis to make their food (autotrophic).

    3. Fungus-like protists:

      • Decompose dead organisms (saprophytic).

      • Unlike true fungi:

        • Fungus-like protists are able to move at some point in their life cycle.

        • They do not have chitin in their cell walls (true fungi do).

      • Examples: Slime molds, water molds, and downy mildews.

Slide 48: Protists (Impact)

  • Harmful Protists: Some protists cause significant diseases, such as malaria and sleeping sickness, resulting in millions of human deaths worldwide each year.

  • Beneficial Protists: Unicellular algae (a type of protist, often phytoplankton) produce a large portion of the Earth's atmospheric oxygen and form the basis of aquatic food chains.

Slide 49: What is a protozoan?

  • Definition: All protozoans are unicellular heterotrophs that feed on other organisms or dead organic matter.

  • Reproduction: They usually reproduce asexually, but some can also reproduce sexually (often dependent on environmental conditions, as per transcript).

  • Four Main Groups of Protozoans:

    1. The amoebas

    2. Flagellates

    3. Ciliates

    4. Sporozoans

Slide 50: The amoebas: shapeless protists

  • Characteristics of Amoebas:

    • Have no cell wall.

    • Form pseudopodia ("false feet" - extensions of cytoplasm) to move and engulf food. The shape of the cell changes as pseudopodia form.

    • Habitat: Most live in salt water; some freshwater species live in the ooze of ponds.

    • Reproduction: Most commonly reproduce by asexual reproduction.

    • Survival: When environmental conditions become unfavorable, some types of amoebas form cysts (dormant, resistant structures) that can survive extreme conditions.

  • Energy Acquisition (Heterotrophic):

    • Predator: Eats small organisms.

    • Surrounds food with pseudopodia (phagocytosis).

    • Digests food with lysosomes (after forming a food vacuole).

  • Osmoregulation: Possess contractile vacuoles to remove excess water from the cell, preventing lysis.

  • Image Description: Diagram of an Amoeba showing: Pseudopod, Contractile Vacuole, Food Vacuole, Nucleus, Membrane, Endoplasm (inner cytoplasm), Ectoplasm (outer cytoplasm).

    • In aquatic habitats

Slide 52: Naegleria fowleri

  • Organism: Naegleria fowleri, also known as the "brain-eating amoeba."

  • Habitat: Typically found in warm freshwater bodies (lakes, rivers, hot springs), warm water discharge from industrial/power plants, geothermal well water, poorly maintained/minimally chlorinated swimming pools (chlorine < 0.5 g/m³), water heaters, soil, and pipes connected to tap water.

  • Life Stages: Can exist in an amoeboid form or a temporary flagellate stage.

  • Definition: Flagellates are protozoans that have one or more flagella (whip-like appendages) to help them move.

  • Lifestyles:

    • Some are parasites that cause disease in animals (e.g., African sleeping sickness in humans, caused by Trypanosoma).

Slide 54: Ciliates: protozoans with cilia

  • Definition: Ciliates are protozoans that use cilia (short, hair-like projections) that cover their bodies to move.

  • Habitat: Live in every kind of aquatic habitat – from ponds and streams to oceans and sulfur springs.

  • Reproduction: Usually reproduce asexually by dividing and separating into two daughter cells (binary fission).

Slide 55: PROTIST EXAMPLES - Paramecium

  • Characteristics:

    • Unicellular

    • Heterotrophs

Slide 56: Paramecium (Energy & Reproduction)

  • Energy Acquisition (Eating):

Slide 61: Sporozoans: parasitic protozoans (Malaria Example)

  • Definition: Sporozoans

    • A group of parasitic protozoans.

    • They live as internal parasites in one or more hosts and have complex life cycles.

  • Example: Plasmodium

    • The genus of sporozoan protists that causes the disease malaria in humans, other mammals, and birds.

  • Energy/Lifestyle (Malaria):

    • Infects humans through a mosquito bite (mosquito is a vector).

Slide 62: Malaria Life Cycle (Detailed Diagram)

  • Image Description: A more detailed diagram of the Plasmodium life cycle.

    1. In the gut of Anopheles mosquito: Female gametes fuse, zygote undergoes meiosis. Cell divisions produce many sporozoites, which migrate to the salivary glands.

    2. Mosquito bites human: Sporozoites pass from mosquito salivary glands into the bloodstream and then to the liver of the host.

    3. Asexual spores (merozoites) produced in the liver enter the bloodstream and then infect red blood cells (RBCs).

    4. RBCs rupture: When RBCs rupture, spores (merozoites) invade and reproduce asexually inside new RBCs.

    5. Symptoms: Spores and toxins pour into the bloodstream when RBCs rupture, causing recurrent chills and fever cycle.

    6. Gametocyte formation: Some spores become male and female gametocytes, which enter the bloodstream. If taken up by a mosquito during a blood meal, they become gametes in the mosquito gut, continuing the cycle.

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 3: Eukaryotes and Protists)

  • Prokaryotic Recap: Detailed review of prokaryotic structures including nucleoid (with NAPs), plasmids, 70S ribosomes (vs. 80S eukaryotic), endospores (for survival), plasma membrane modifications (photosynthetic structures, mesosomes), cell wall (peptidoglycan in Bacteria, pseudopeptidoglycan or none in Archaea), glycocalyx (capsule, slime layer), S-layers, flagella, fimbriae, and pili (for adhesion and conjugation).

  • Eukaryotic Cells: Appeared ~2.7 billion years ago, likely via endosymbiosis. Characterized by a membrane-bound nucleus and numerous membrane-bound organelles (mitochondria, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, etc.). DNA is linear and in the nucleus. Can be unicellular or multicellular.

  • Key Eukaryotic Organelles: Nucleolus (rRNA synthesis, ribosome assembly), plasma membrane, cytoskeleton, cell wall (in plants, fungi), ribosomes (80S), mitochondria (energy), ER (protein processing), Golgi (modification, packaging), lysosomes (digestion), centrioles (animal cell division), chloroplasts (plant/algae photosynthesis).

  • Animal vs. Plant Cells: Plants have cell walls, chloroplasts, large central vacuoles, plasmodesmata; animals typically have centrioles and lack these plant-specific features.

  • Kingdom Protista: A highly diverse group of eukaryotes; the "miscellaneous drawer." Mostly unicellular, some multicellular/colonial. Can be autotrophic or heterotrophic.

    • Protozoa (Animal-like): Unicellular heterotrophs (e.g., Amoeba, Paramecium, Naegleria fowleri). Move by pseudopodia, flagella, or cilia.

    • Algae (Plant-like): Photosynthetic (e.g., Euglena, Volvox, phytoplankton, multicellular seaweeds). Produce significant atmospheric oxygen.

    • Fungus-like Protists: Decomposers, but differ from true fungi (motile stage, no chitin in cell walls). (e.g., Slime molds, water molds, downy mildews).

  • Impact of Protists: Some are pathogenic (malaria, sleeping sickness, PAM), while others are crucial ecosystem components (phytoplankton).

II. General Properties and Characteristics of Fungi

Slide 8: General properties of fungi

  • Eukaryotic Cells: Contain membrane-bound organelles (nuclei, mitochondria, Golgi, ER, lysosomes, etc.).

  • Cell Wall: Have a rigid cell wall made up of chitin.

    • [Foundational Knowledge Refresh]: Chitin is a polysaccharide also found in the exoskeletons of arthropods.

  • Cell Membrane: Composed of ergosterols, not primarily phospholipids like in many other eukaryotes.

    • Transcript Elaboration: The instructor mentions ergosterol is a "multicyclic aromatic compound." This distinguishes fungal membranes.

  • Nutrition (Chemoheterotrophs):

    • Lack chlorophyll and are therefore chemoheterotrophs (require organic compounds for both carbon and energy sources); they are not autotrophic.

    • Osmiotrophic: Obtain nutrients by absorption from the environment.

    • Obtain nutrients as:

      • Saprophytes (or saprobes): Live off of decaying organic matter.

      • Parasites: Live off of living matter.

    • [Instructor Emphasis] Ecological Role as Recyclers: "The fact that fungi live off of decaying matter, they're basically the recyclers of this ecosystem on Earth. Okay, if fungi didn't exist, okay, there would be no recycling of organic material to support new forms of life... they complete the circle of recycling... major recycling agents."

  • Reproduction: Typically reproduce asexually and/or sexually by producing spores.

  • Growth:

    • Reproductively by budding (common in yeasts).

    • Non-reproductively by hyphal tip elongation (in filamentous fungi).

  • Food Storage: Generally in the form of lipids and glycogen.

    • [Foundational Knowledge Refresh]: Glycogen is also a primary storage polysaccharide in animals.

Slide 9: General Characteristics (Molds, Mycelium, etc.)

  • Mycelium: The total hyphal mass of a fungus is called a mycelium.

  • Hyphal Structure:

    • Hyphae may be septate (divided by cross-walls called septa) or aseptate (lacking septa, also called coenocytic, meaning multinucleate without internal divisions).

  • Dimorphism: Many fungi are dimorphic, meaning they can change from a yeast form to a mold form (or vice versa) depending on environmental conditions.

  • Cellular Features: Consist of typical eukaryotic nuclei and membrane-bound organelles.

  • Ribosomes: Are of the 80S type (characteristic of eukaryotes).

  • Nutritional Mode: Most fungi are saprophytes, securing nutrients from dead organic material.

  • Optimal Growth Conditions:

    • pH: Grow best at an optimum pH of 5.5 (acidic environment).

    • Temperature: Optimum temperature of 20-35°C.

  • Oxygen Requirements:

    • Most fungi are aerobic (require oxygen).

    • Some yeasts are facultatively anaerobic (can survive with or without oxygen) and carry out fermentation. (Transcript: "That's why those types of yeast strains are the backbone of the baking and wine industry.")

Slide 12: FUNGI (Ecological/Economic Roles, Distinctions from Other Kingdoms)

  • Environmental Role: Fungi play a vital role in the environment (e.g., decomposition).

  • Economic Importance: Economically important as a food source (e.g., mushrooms).

  • Cellular Similarity: Cells of fungi are quite similar to those of plants, protists, and animals (all are eukaryotes).

Slide 20: Structure - Hyphae (Septate vs. Coenocytic)

  • Septate Hyphae:

    • Hyphae of most molds contain cross-walls called septa, which divide them into distinct, uninucleate (one-nucleus) cell-like units. Fungi with such hyphae are called septate fungi.

  • Coenocytic Hyphae:

    • Hyphae of a few classes of fungi contain no septa and appear as long, continuous cells with many nuclei (multinucleate).

    • Cytoplasm passes through and among cells of the hypha uninterrupted by cross-walls. Fungi with such hyphae are called coenocytic fungi.

  • Pores in Septa (Reiteration): Even in fungi with septate hyphae, there are usually openings (pores) in the septa that make the cytoplasm of adjacent "cells" continuous.

Slide 21: Structure - Hyphae (Growth, Types) & Mycelium

  • Types of Hyphae based on Function:

    • Vegetative Hyphae: The portion of a hypha that obtains nutrients.

  • Structure - Mycelium:

    • When environmental conditions are suitable, the hyphae grow to form a filamentous, tangled mass called a mycelium, which is visible to the unaided eye.

Slide 29: FUNGI - NUTRITION (Chemoheterotrophs, Saprophytes, Parasites)

  • Chemoheterotrophs: All fungi are chemoheterotrophs, requiring organic compounds for energy and carbon.

  • Saprophytes: Mostly saprophytes, obtaining their nutrients from dead organic matter.

  • Ecological Role: Play an important role in the environment by decomposing and recycling organic matter.

  • Parasitic Fungi:

    • Few are parasitic on plants, causing major losses of crops.

    • Others find their way into humans or other animals and cause serious infections.

Slide 30: Nutrition of Fungi (Enzymes, Absorption, Lifestyles)

  • Enzymes: The nutritional needs of a fungus are facilitated by enzymes such as cellulase (breaks down cellulose) and/or chitinase (breaks down chitin). (Transcript: "enzymes that we don't have").

  • Nutrient Acquisition: Acquire nutrients by absorption.

  • Lifestyles:

    • Most are saprobes (saprophytes).

    • Some trap and kill microscopic soil-dwelling nematodes (predatory fungi). (See image on Slide 76).

    • Haustoria: Specialized hyphal structures that allow some fungi to derive nutrients from living plants and animals (parasitic).

  • Oxygen Requirements:

    • Most are aerobic.

    • Some are anaerobic.

    • Many yeasts are facultative anaerobes.

Slide 31: NUTRITIONAL ADAPTATIONS (vs. Bacteria - pH, Aerobicity)

  • Hostile Environments: Fungi are generally adapted to environments that would be hostile to bacteria.

  • Absorption: Fungi are chemoheterotrophs and, like bacteria, they absorb nutrients rather than ingesting them as animals do.

  • Differences from Bacteria (Environmental/Nutritional Requirements):

    • pH: Fungi usually grow better in an environment with a pH of about 5 (acidic), which is too acidic for the growth of most common bacteria.

    • Oxygen: Almost all molds are aerobic. Most yeasts are facultative anaerobes.

Slide 32: NUTRITIONAL ADAPTATIONS (Osmotic Pressure, Moisture, Nitrogen, Complex Carbs)

  • Osmotic Pressure: Most fungi are more resistant to osmotic pressure than bacteria; most can therefore grow in relatively high sugar or salt concentrations. (Transcript: "That's why, for example, in certain fruit jam recipes, if you keep sugar concentration low... mold will grow in it definitely.")

  • Moisture Content: Fungi can grow on substances with a very low moisture content, generally too low to support the growth of bacteria.

  • Nitrogen Requirement: Fungi require somewhat less nitrogen than bacteria for an equivalent amount of growth.

  • Metabolism of Complex Carbohydrates: Fungi are often capable of metabolizing complex carbohydrates, such as lignin (a component of wood), that most bacteria cannot use for nutrients.

  • Transcript Summary: "if you look at these four bullet points, it shows you how sturdy the fungal species can be."

Slide 37: Physiological Requirements (Oxygen, pH, Media for Molds)

Slide 39: REPRODUCTION (Overview)

  • Fungi reproduce by the production of spores.

  • Fungal spores can be either asexual or sexual.

  • Asexual Reproduction by Fragmentation: Filamentous fungi can reproduce asexually by fragmentation of their hyphae (each fragment can grow into a new mycelium).

  • Molds reproduce using both sexual and asexual reproduction methods.

  • Molds reproduce by producing very large numbers of small spores, which may contain a single nucleus or be multinucleate.

  • Spores are formed from aerial hyphae (reproductive hyphae).

  • Mold spores can be:

    • Asexual: The products of mitosis.

    • Sexual: The products of meiosis.

  • Many species can produce both types of spores.

  • Spores are of considerable importance in the identification of fungi.

  • At the end of the hyphae are the reproductive structures that produce spores.

  • Spores are produced on different structures depending on the species.

Slide 40: Asexual and Sexual Reproduction in fungi (Terminology)

  • Teleomorphs: The sexual fungi (the "perfect" stage, involving meiosis). They produce sexual spores.

  • Anamorph: The asexual fungi (the "imperfect" stage, involving mitosis). They produce asexual spores.

    • Example: Penicillium is an anamorph that arose from a mutation in a teleomorph.

  • Holomorph: The whole fungus, including anamorphs and teleomorph (i.e., all its life stages).

  • Many fungi can have both sexual and asexual stages, especially Ascomycota. Most have either one or the other as their predominant mode.

Slide 41: Asexual Spores (Conidia)

  • Conidia (singular: conidium): Asexual spores are often called conidia.

  • Germination: When these spores germinate, they become organisms that are genetically identical to the parent cell.

  • Production: Asexual spores are produced by the hyphae of an individual fungus through mitosis and subsequent cell division.

  • No Nuclear Fusion: There is no fusion of the nuclei of cells in asexual reproduction.

  • Image Description: Diagram showing Conidia (chains of spores) being produced on a specialized hyphal structure called a Conidiophore, which arises from a Hyphae.

Slide 42: Asexual Spores (Types)

  • Conidiospore/Conidium (plural: conidia):

    • Unicellular or multicellular spores produced in a chain at the end of a conidiophore.

    • Produced by Aspergillus.

    • Conidia are not enclosed in a sac.

  • Blastospores (Blastoconidia):

    • Produced in a cluster by budding from a parent cell (which itself can be a conidium or yeast cell).

    • Consist of buds coming off the parent cell.

    • Found in yeasts, such as Candida albicans and Cryptococcus.

  • Sporangiospores:

    • Spores formed within a sporangium (sac) at the end of an aerial hypha called a sporangiophore.

    • The sporangium can contain hundreds of sporangiospores.

    • Produced by Rhizopus (bread mold).

Slide 43: Asexual Reproduction (Diagram)

  • Image Description: A diagram illustrating the asexual life cycle involving conidia.

    • Hyphae (subsurface and aerial) give rise to a Conidiophore.

    • The conidiophore produces chains of Conidia (spores).

    • Conidia are released and, upon Germination in a suitable environment, develop into new hyphae, forming a new mycelium.

Slide 44: Sexual reproduction

  • Process:

    1. During sexual reproduction, compatible nuclei unite within the mycelium and form sexual spores.

    2. Sexually opposite cells (different mating types, often designated + and -) may unite within a single mycelium or different mycelia.

    3. When the cells unite, the nuclei fuse (karyogamy) and form a diploid nucleus (zygote).

    4. Several divisions (typically meiosis) follow, and the haploid state is reestablished in the resulting sexual spores.

  • Transcript Elaboration on Advantage: [Instructor Emphasis] The advantage of sexual reproduction is genetic variation. "Because having a sexual mode of reproduction for fungi once in a while will ensure that the genes will be shuffled and they will have increased variation, allowing the fungus to adapt to new environmental conditions."

Slide 45: Sexual spores

  • Origin: Sexual spores result from the fusion of nuclei from two opposite mating strains of the same species of fungus.

  • Frequency: Fungi produce sexual spores less frequently than asexual spores. (Transcript: "this is not their preference they mostly form their spores through asexual reproduction").

  • Genetic Outcome: Organisms that grow from sexual spores will have genetic characteristics of both parental strains, leading to increased genetic diversity.

Slide 46: Sexual Spores (Three Phases of Sexual Reproduction)

  • Fungal sexual reproduction consists of three phases:

    1. Plasmogamy (Cell fusion): A haploid nucleus of a donor cell (+) penetrates the cytoplasm of a recipient cell (-). The cytoplasms fuse, but the nuclei may remain separate for a time (dikaryotic stage in some fungi).

    2. Karyogamy (Nuclear fusion): The (+) and (-) nuclei fuse to form a diploid zygote nucleus.

    3. Meiosis: The diploid zygote nucleus undergoes meiosis to give rise to haploid nuclei (sexual spores), some of which may be genetic recombinants.

Slide 47: Oospores, Ascospores (Types of Sexual Spores)

  • Oospores:

    • Produced when male gametes (reproductive nuclei) enter a large spherical cell (the oogonium) and fertilize the eggs within.

  • Ascospores:

    • Produced within spherical or sac-like cells called asci (singular: ascus).

    • Most often in groups of 4 or 8 ascospores per ascus.

    • Usually, the asci are produced within some kind of enclosing structure (fruiting body, e.g., ascocarp) and thus are not found exposed on the hyphae.

Slide 48: Zygospores (Type of Sexual Spore)

  • Characteristics:

    • Do not occur inside any kind of enclosing structure.

    • Produced by the direct fusion of two hyphal protrusions (gametangia) from neighboring filaments (compatible mating types).

    • Usually recognized as large, nearly spherical, often dark brown or black, rough-walled spores with two connecting hyphae, representing the two mating gametangia.

  • Image Description: A micrograph showing a dark, spherical Zygospore formed between two hyphae.

Slide 49: Basidiospores (Type of Sexual Spore)

  • Characteristics:

    • Produced externally on a structure called a basidium (plural: basidia).

    • Basidia come in a variety of forms, but those commonly encountered on molds will be club-shaped.

    • They bear four or eight spores on sharp projections (called sterigmata, singular: sterigma) at the apex.

  • Image Description: Diagram of a club-shaped Basidium with four Basidiospores attached via Sterigmata.

Slide 50: Common Molds (List of Genera)

  • A list of common mold genera: Absidia, Alternaria, Ascomycetes (a phylum/class, not a genus), Aspergillus, Basidiomycetes (a phylum/class), Cladosporium, Curvularia, Fusarium, Mucor, Penicillium, Pithomyces, Stachybotrys, Trichoderma, Ulocladium.

    • (Note: Ascomycetes and Basidiomycetes are major fungal groups, not specific mold genera in the same way the others are listed.)

Slide 51: Fungal spores - Bacterial endospores (Comparison)

  • Key Differences:

    • Fungal spores are quite different from bacterial endospores.

    • Bacterial Endospores:

      • Allow a bacterial cell to survive adverse environmental conditions.

      • A single vegetative bacterial cell forms one endospore, which germinates to produce a single vegetative bacterial cell.

      • This process is not reproduction because it does not increase the total number of bacterial cells.

    • Fungal Spores:

      • After a mold forms a spore, the spore detaches from the parent and germinates into a new mold.

      • This is a true reproductive spore; a second organism grows from the spore.

    • Tolerance: Although fungal spores can survive for extended periods in dry or hot environments, most do not exhibit the extreme tolerance and longevity of bacterial endospores.

VIII. Yeasts: Unicellular Fungi

Slide 52: Yeast (General Characteristics & Budding)

  • Definition: Yeasts are microscopic, unicellular fungi with a single nucleus and eukaryotic organelles.

  • Shape: They are oval or round in shape.

  • Asexual Reproduction (Budding): They reproduce asexually by a process of budding.

    • In this process, a new cell forms at the surface of the original cell, enlarges, and then breaks free to live independently.

  • Image Description:

    • Right: Diagram titled "Yeast is a unicellular fungus" showing a single yeast cell with labeled cell wall, nucleus, cytoplasm, vacuole. Slide 53: Yeasts (Detailed Characteristics)

  • Cellular Nature: Yeasts are single-celled eukaryotic microorganisms.

  • Size: Yeasts are 5 to 10 µm in diameter. They are larger than bacteria.

  • Form: Yeasts are non-filamentous unicellular fungi.

  • Shape: Typically spherical or oval in shape.

  • Organelles: Yeasts consist of a single nucleus and eukaryotic organelles.

  • Classification: Classified in the Kingdom Fungi, with 1,500 species currently described.

  • Abundance: Estimated to be 1% of all fungal species.

Slide 54: Yeast (Economic Importance)

  • Yeasts are very important economically:

    • Responsible for fermentation of beer and bread (e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae).

    • Ethanol production.

    • Wastewater treatment: A mixed culture of yeasts (Candida lipolytica, Candida tropicalis, and Yarrowia lipolytica) grown on hydrocarbons or gas oil.

    • Transcript Addition: Used in biopharma technology for the production of therapeutic antibodies.

Slide 55: Yeast Cell Diagram (Detailed)

  • Image Description: A detailed diagram of a yeast cell showing various organelles: Cell wall, Periplasm, Plasma membrane, Cytosol, Nucleus, ER (Endoplasmic reticulum), Golgi, Vacuole, Mitochondrion, Peroxisome, Secretory vesicles (S), Bud scar, Invagination.

Slide 56: Structure of Yeast Cell (Model Organism, Ultrastructure)

  • Model Organism: Yeast cells share most of the structural and functional features of higher Eukaryotes — which has rendered yeast an ideal model for Eukaryotic cell biology. [Instructor Emphasis]

  • Ultrastructural Features Observed: Cell wall, periplasm, Plasma membrane, bud scar, cytoplasm, nucleus, mitochondria, Endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi Apparatus, Secretory vesicles, vacuole, peroxisomes.

Slide 57: Structure of Yeast Cell (Peculiarities, Bud/Birth Scars)

  • Contrast to Mammalian Cells: Peculiarities of yeast cells are that they are surrounded by a rigid cell wall and develop birth scars during cell division. The vacuole in yeast corresponds to lysosomes in higher cells.

  • Bud Scars: Specialized, ring-shaped convex protrusions at the cell surface which remain on the mother cells (of budding yeasts) after cell division and birth of daughter cells.

  • Birth Scars: The concave indentations remaining on the surface of the daughter cell after budding are called birth scars.

Slide 58: Structure of Yeast Cell (Cytoplasm, Cytoskeleton, Ribosomes, Nucleus, DNA, ER)

  • Cytoplasm: Yeast cytoplasm is an acidic (pH 5.25) colloidal fluid.

  • Cytoskeleton: Comprises microtubules and microfilaments.

  • Ribosomes: Freely suspended 80S ribosomes.

  • Nucleus: Yeast nucleus is a round, lobate organelle, 1.5 µm in diameter.

  • Nuclear DNA: Yeast nuclear DNA can be isolated as linear molecules ranging in size from 10 µm - 50 µm.

  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): The site of biosynthesis and modifications of proteins that are to be exported.

Slide 59: Structure of Yeast Cell (Protein Trafficking, Peroxisomes, Mitochondria)

  • Protein Trafficking (ER to Golgi): From ER, proteins are directed to the Golgi Apparatus by vesicles, which fuse at the Cis side and are exported from the Golgi Apparatus at the trans side. In Golgi, further modifications of the protein by carbohydrate side chains may take place.

  • Protein Trafficking (Golgi to Destinations): Proteins delivered from the Golgi are directed to different destinations within the cell or to the exterior via different secretory vesicles to the vacuole, the bud region during mitosis, the plasma membrane, or the periplasm.

  • Peroxisomes: Membrane-enclosed organelles that contain enzymes involved in a variety of metabolic reactions, including several aspects of energy metabolism. They perform a variety of metabolic functions in Eukaryotic cells.

  • Mitochondria: Under aerobic conditions, yeast mitochondria are involved in ATP synthesis coupled to oxidative phosphorylation.

Slide 60: Cytological methods (for studying yeast)

  • Light Microscopy: Unstained yeast cells can hardly be visualized by light microscopy. At 1000-fold magnification, it may be possible to see the yeast vacuole and cytosolic inclusion bodies.

  • Phase Contrast Microscopy: By phase contrast microscopy, together with an appropriate staining technique, several cellular structures can be distinguished.

  • Fluorescent Protein Tagging: A very convenient tool to localize and even to follow the movement of particular proteins within yeast cells is the use of green fluorescent protein (GFP) from the jellyfish (Aequorea victoria) as a reporter molecule.

  • Electron Microscopy (EM): Organelle ultrastructure and macromolecular architecture can only be obtained with the aid of EM.

  • Transcript Note: Yeast cells are smaller than mammalian cells but bigger than bacterial cells, requiring high-resolution microscopy.

Slide 61: Yeasts (Distribution, Reproduction - Budding/Fission)

  • Distribution: Like molds, yeasts are widely distributed in nature; they are frequently found as a white powdery coating on fruits and leaves (e.g., grapes, plums).

  • Reproduction (Asexual):

    • Most yeasts reproduce asexually.

    • Yeasts grow as single cells producing daughter cells either by:

      1. An asymmetric division process called Budding - the budding yeasts (e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae).

      2. Binary fission (splitting in two) - the fission yeasts (e.g., Schizosaccharomyces pombe).

    • Budding (Process): A small bud cell forms on the cell, which gradually enlarges and separates from the mother cells.

    • Most of the yeasts reproduce by budding.

Slide 62: Budding Diagram

  • Image Description: A diagram illustrating the stages of yeast budding:

    • Yeast cell -> Developing bud -> New bud -> Chain of buds (if they don't separate, forming pseudohyphae).

Slide 63: Budding (Detailed Process)

  • Process:

    1. In budding, the parent cell forms a protuberance (bud) on its outer surface.

    2. As the bud elongates, the parent cell's nucleus divides, and one nucleus migrates into the bud.

    3. Cell wall material is then laid down between the bud and parent cell.

    4. The bud eventually breaks away.

  • Rapidity: Yeasts reproduce rapidly. One yeast cell can in time produce up to 24 daughter cells by budding.

  • Division Type: Budding yeasts divide unevenly (e.g., Saccharomyces).

Slide 65: Pseudohypha

  • Definition: Some yeasts produce buds that fail to detach themselves; these buds form a short chain of cells called a pseudohypha (plural: pseudohyphae).

  • Pathogenic Example: Candida albicans attaches to human epithelial cells as a yeast but usually requires pseudohypha to invade deeper tissues. (Transcript: "this is a pathogenic yeast, Candida albicana. And they used the pseudohypha form of reproduction when they want to infect human epithelial cells.")

  • Image Description: Diagram showing a single yeast cell (nucleus, cytoplasm, cell wall) and a chain of budding yeast cells forming a pseudohypha.

Slide 66: Fission (Yeast Reproduction)

  • Fission: Similar to budding in outcome (daughter cells) but different in process.

  • Process:

    1. During fission, the parent cell elongates and grows to a certain size.

    2. Its nucleus divides.

    3. Two equal daughter cells are produced (symmetric division).

  • Occurrence: Only a few yeast species reproduce by fission (e.g., Schizosaccharomyces).

  • Division Type: Fission yeasts divide evenly to produce two new cells.

  • Image Description: Fluorescence micrograph of fission yeast cells (likely Schizosaccharomyces pombe) undergoing cell division, with nuclei stained blue and possibly spindle pole bodies red. A clear division septum is forming.

Slide 67: Yeast respiration (Facultative Anaerobes)

  • Oxygen Requirement: Yeasts are facultative anaerobes, which allows them to grow in a variety of environments.

    • Aerobic Respiration: When oxygen is available, they carry out aerobic respiration (metabolize carbohydrate to carbon dioxide and water).

    • Fermentation: When oxygen is not available (denied oxygen), they ferment carbohydrates to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide.

  • Final Electron Acceptor: Yeasts can use oxygen or an organic compound as the final electron acceptor; this is a valuable attribute because it allows these fungi to survive in various environments.

  • Industrial Applications of Fermentation: This fermentation is used in the brewing, wine-making, and baking industries. Saccharomyces species produce ethanol in brewed beverages and CO2 for leavening bread dough.

    • Transcript Elaboration on Baking: "during baking, all this carbon dioxide released as a result of fermentation forms air bubbles within the dough. And these bubbles are captured in the dough as it is baked. And therefore, that's how the dough is actually leaven, rise basically, as a result of yeast fermentation."

Slide 68: Morphology (Yeast Size, Shape, Pseudohyphae)

  • Size: Yeast size can vary greatly depending on the species, typically measuring 4-12 µm long and 3-4 µm in diameter, although some yeasts can reach over 40 µm in diameter.

  • Shape: Spherical or oval in shape.

  • Multicellularity (Pseudohyphae): Some species of yeast forms may become multicellular through the formation of strings of connected budding cells known as pseudohyphae, as seen in most molds (referring to the filamentous appearance, though molds are truly multicellular via hyphae).

Slide 69: Nutrition and growth (Yeast)

  • Energy Source: Yeasts are chemoorganotrophs, as they use organic compounds as a source of energy and do not require sunlight to grow.

  • Carbon Sources: Carbon is obtained mostly from hexose sugars (e.g., glucose and fructose) or disaccharides (e.g., sucrose and maltose).

  • Other Metabolizable Compounds:

    • Some species can metabolize pentose sugars (e.g., ribose, Xylose).

    • Some species can metabolize alcohols and organic acids.

  • Oxygen Requirement (Reiteration): Yeast species require oxygen for aerobic cellular respiration (obligate aerobes) or are anaerobic (fermentative yeast), or facultative anaerobes.

Slide 70: Cultural Characteristics (Yeast Growth Media)

  • Laboratory Growth: Yeasts are grown in the laboratory on solid growth media or in liquid broths.

  • Preferred Conditions: They grow especially well in substances containing sugar.

  • Colony Appearance: An increase in the number of yeast cells on a solid medium produces a colony similar to a bacterial colony.

  • Common Media for Yeast Cultivation:

    • Potato dextrose agar or potato dextrose broth

    • Wallerstein Laboratories nutrient agar

    • Yeast peptone dextrose agar

Slide 71: Cultural Characteristics (Yeast Temperature, pH, Water Activity)

  • Temperature:

    • Yeasts vary in the temperature range they grow best.

    • Optimum = 25 to 30°C.

    • Maximum about 35 to 47°C.

    • Some grow at 0°C or less.

  • pH:

    • Optimal pH: 4 to 4.5.

    • Yeasts grow best in a neutral or slightly acidic pH.

    • Will not grow well in alkaline medium unless adapted to it.

  • Water activity/moisture: 0.7 to 0.8.

    • Osmophilic yeasts can grow at even low Aw.

    • Require less moisture than the majority of bacteria and more moisture than molds.

    • Can grow in the presence of high concentrations of solutes (sugar/salt).

Slide 72: Cultural Characteristics (Yeast vs. Molds, Dimorphism, Colony Appearance)

  • Distinction from Molds: Yeasts differ from most fungi, which grow as thread-like hyphae.

  • Dimorphism: This distinction is not fundamental because some fungi can alternate between a yeast phase and a hyphal phase, depending on environmental conditions. Such fungi are termed dimorphic.

  • Identification Challenges:

    • The appearance of massed yeast growth is not usually useful for identification, although growth as a film on the surface of liquid media suggests an oxidative or film yeast.

    • Some produce pigments (e.g., carotenoid pigment indicates the genus Rhodotorula - causes colored spots on foods).

    • It is difficult to tell yeast colonies from bacterial ones on agar plates; the only certain way is by microscopic examination.

  • Colony Morphology:

    • Most young yeast colonies are moist and somewhat slimy but may appear mealy.

    • Most colonies change little with age and become dry and wrinkled (Transcript: "probably through the accumulation of lots of butt scars").

  • Growth in Liquid:

    • Yeasts are oxidative, fermentative, or both.

    • Oxidative yeasts may grow as a film, pellicle, or scum on the surface of a liquid (termed film yeasts).

    • Fermentative yeasts usually grow throughout the liquid and produce carbon dioxide.

Slide 73: Fungi - dimorphic

  • Dimorphism: Some fungi, most notably pathogenic species, exhibit dimorphism—two forms of growth. Such fungi can grow either as a mold or as a yeast.

  • Phase Shifting: They have the ability to shift from the yeast form to the mold form and vice versa.

  • Pathogens: Many fungal pathogens exist in the body in the yeast form but revert to the mold form in the laboratory when cultivated.

  • Structural Correlates:

    • Mold-like forms produce vegetative and aerial hyphae.

    • Yeast-like forms reproduce by budding.

  • Environmental Control of Dimorphism:

    • Temperature-dependent: In pathogenic fungi, at 37°C (body temperature), the fungus is yeast-like, and at 25°C (room temperature), it is mold-like.

    • CO2 Concentration: The appearance of the dimorphic fungus also changes with CO2 concentration.

X. Classification of Fungi

Slide 81: Classification of fungi.

  • The kingdom fungi is classified into 4 divisions (Phyla):

    1. Ascomycetes

    2. Basidiomycetes

    3. Zygomycetes

    4. Deuteromycetes (Fungi Imperfecti)

Slide 82: Ascomycetes (Sac Fungi)

  • Sexual Spore: Produced within a sac-like structure called an ascus.

  • Sexual Spore Name: Called ascospore.

  • Asexual Reproduction: Occurs by conidia.

  • Common Name: Ascomycetes are also known as sac mycetes (sac fungi).

  • Hyphae: Generally septated.

  • Examples: Saccharomyces (a yeast), Arthroderma.

  • Image Description: Micrograph and diagram showing an ascus containing multiple ascospores.

Slide 83: Basidiomycetes (Club Fungi)

  • Sexual Spore: Produced externally on a basidium.

  • Sexual Spore Name: Known as basidiospore.

  • Asexual Reproduction: Occurs by budding, fragmentation, or conidia formation.

  • Common Name: Commonly called as mushroom group (includes mushrooms, puffballs, rusts, smuts).

  • Hyphae: Generally septated.

  • Examples: Amanita (a genus of mushrooms, some toxic), Agaricus (a genus of mushrooms, some edible).

  • Image Description: Electron micrograph and diagram showing basidiospores on basidia.

Slide 84: Zygomycetes (Conjugation Fungi)

  • Sexual Spore: Known as Zygospore.

  • Zygospore Formation: Formed by the fusion of two similar cells (gametangia).

  • Asexual Reproduction: Occurs by sporangiospore.

  • Hyphae: Generally aseptated (coenocytic).

  • Examples: Rhizopus (bread mold), Mucor.

  • Image Description: Diagram illustrating the formation of a zygospore from the fusion of hyphae of different mating types, showing gametangia and the diploid zygote developing into a zygospore. A micrograph of a mature zygospore is also shown.

Slide 85: Deuteromycetes (Fungi Imperfecti)

  • Sexual Stage: No sexual stage is present (or has not been observed).

  • Common Name: Deuteromycetes are also known as fungi imperfecti.

    • (Note: This is an artificial grouping for fungi where sexual reproduction is unknown. Many have since been reclassified into other phyla, especially Ascomycota, once their sexual stages were discovered.)

  • Asexual Reproduction: Occurs by means of conidia.

  • Pathogens: Most of the human and animal pathogens are present in this class (historically).

  • Examples: Candida, Cryptococcus, Trichophyton, Epidermophyton, Histoplasma.

  • Image Description: Various diagrams showing conidia formation in different Deuteromycetes (e.g., Cladosporium, Helminthosporium, Penicillium, Geotrichum candidum).

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 4: Kingdom of Fungi)

  • General Characteristics: Fungi are eukaryotic, heterotrophic (saprophytes or parasites, osmiotrophic), mostly unicellular (yeasts) or filamentous (molds), spore-bearing, with cell walls of chitin and cell membranes of ergosterols. They are vital decomposers/recyclers.

  • Structure:

    • Hyphae: Basic structural units of molds; can be septate (with cross-walls) or coenocytic (aseptate, multinucleate).

    • Mycelium: A tangled mass of hyphae.

    • Growth: Hyphae grow at tips; require organic matter and moisture.

  • Nutrition: Heterotrophic by absorption (extracellular digestion via enzymes like cellulase, chitinase). Adapted to acidic pH, high osmotic pressure, low moisture.

  • Reproduction:

    • Asexual: Spores (conidia, blastospores, sporangiospores), fragmentation, budding (yeasts), binary fission (some yeasts).

    • Sexual: Involves plasmogamy, karyogamy, and meiosis to produce sexual spores (oospores, ascospores, zygospores, basidiospores). Provides genetic variation.

    • Teleomorph (sexual), Anamorph (asexual), Holomorph (whole fungus).

  • Yeasts: Unicellular fungi, reproduce by budding or fission. Economically important (Saccharomyces cerevisiae for baking/brewing). Some form pseudohyphae. Facultative anaerobes. Model organisms for eukaryotic cell biology.

  • Molds: Multicellular, filamentous fungi forming mycelia.

  • Dimorphism: Ability of some fungi (esp. pathogens) to switch between yeast and mold forms based on environmental conditions (e.g., temperature, CO2).

  • Significance:

    • Ecological: Decomposers, mycorrhizae (symbiosis with plants).

    • Economic/Human Use: Food (mushrooms), food production (cheese, bread), antibiotics (penicillin), drugs (cyclosporin), industrial fermentation (ethanol, citric acid), research models.

    • Detrimental: Plant and animal diseases (mycoses), food spoilage.

  • Classification (4 Divisions):

    • Ascomycetes (Sac Fungi): Ascospores in asci. (e.g., Saccharomyces).

    • Basidiomycetes (Club Fungi): Basidiospores on basidia. (e.g., mushrooms).

    • Zygomycetes (Conjugation Fungi): Zygospores. (e.g., Rhizopus).

    • Deuteromycetes (Fungi Imperfecti): No known sexual stage. (e.g., many human pathogens like Candida).

B. Bryophyta (Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts)

Slide 11: Bryophytes (Brief Overview)

  • Body Differentiation: Have a differentiated plant body with structures that resemble stems and leaves, but lack true roots, stems, and leaves with vascular tissue.

  • Vascular System: Lack a vascular system for the transportation of substances across the plant body.

  • Habitat: Found in both land and aquatic habitats, often requiring moist environments. Hence, they are known as "amphibians of the plant kingdom."

  • Examples: Mosses and Marchantia (a liverwort).

Slide 13: BRYOPHYTA (Detailed Characteristics & Images)

  • Habitat Dependence: Mostly terrestrial plants which depend on external water for fertilization and completion of their life cycle. This is why they are called amphibian plants.

  • Growth Environment: Grow in shady and moist places (e.g., moist walls, damp rocks, moist soil, decaying logs).

  • Plant Body: Show a thalloid plant body which is not differentiated into true roots, stems, and leaves.

  • Anchorage: True roots are absent, but rhizoids are present.

    • Rhizoids: Filamentous outgrowths that anchor the plant and absorb some water and minerals.

      • Unicellular in liverworts.

      • Multicellular in mosses.

    • Function: Absorb water and minerals, and help in fixation of the thallus to the substratum.

  • Images:

    • A liverwort (flat, lobed thallus).

    • Moss plants (small, leafy appearance).

    • Diagram of a moss showing: Capsule (sporangium), Seta (stalk), Leaves, Gametophyte (main plant body), Rhizoids.

Slide 14: BRYOPHYTA (Life Cycle - Alternation of Generations)

  • Core Concept: Alternation of Generations: Bryophytes exhibit an alternation between a multicellular haploid (n) phase and a multicellular diploid (2n) phase.

  • Diagram Explanation (Synthesized from slide and transcript):

    • Haploid Gametophyte Generation (Dominant Phase - Blue shaded area in diagram):

      1. Spores (1n): Haploid spores are released from the sporophyte.

      2. Germination: Spores germinate, typically forming a filamentous structure called a protonema (especially in mosses).

      3. Gametophyte Development: The protonema develops into the mature gametophyte (the main, leafy or thalloid plant body).

      4. Sex Organs: The gametophyte produces multicellular sex organs:

        • Antheridium (male): Produces motile, biflagellate sperm (antherozoids).

        • Archegonium (female): Flask-shaped, produces a single egg.

    • Fertilization (Requires Water):

      1. Sperm swim through water to reach the archegonium and fertilize the egg.

      2. Zygote (2n): The fertilized egg is the diploid zygote.

    • Diploid Sporophyte Generation (Dependent Phase - Pink shaded area in diagram):

      1. Embryo Development: The zygote develops into a multicellular embryo within the archegonium.

      2. Sporophyte (2n): The embryo grows into the sporophyte, which remains attached to and dependent on the gametophyte for nutrition. The sporophyte typically consists of a foot, seta (stalk), and capsule (sporangium).

      3. Sporangium: Within the capsule (sporangium), spore mother cells undergo MEIOSIS to produce haploid spores (1n).

      4. Release of spores starts the cycle anew.

And then these gametes unite in the archegonium. And after the fertilization stage, the 2N genomic content, young sporophyte, undergoes meiosis and forms spores with 1N content at the end."

Slide 15: GAMETOPHYTE vs. SPOROPHYTE in Bryophytes

  • GAMETOPHYTE (Haploid - n):

    • The main plant body (thallus) of the bryophyte is haploid.

    • It produces gametes (sperm and egg), hence it is called a gametophyte.

    • Sex organs are multicellular:

      • Antheridium (male): Produces biflagellate antherozoids (sperm).

      • Archegonium (female): Flask-shaped, produces a single egg.

    • Antherozoids are released into water and swim to the archegonium to fertilize the egg, producing a zygote (2n).

    • The gametophyte is the DOMINANT, green (photosynthetic), haploid, and independent phase in the bryophyte life cycle.

  • SPOROPHYTE (Diploid - 2n):

    • The zygote develops into a multicellular body called a sporophyte.

    • The sporophyte is NOT free-living; it is attached to the photosynthetic gametophyte and derives nourishment from it.

    • Some cells of the sporophyte undergo meiosis (reduction division) to produce haploid spores (n).

    • These spores germinate to produce a new gametophyte.

    • Thus, the sporophyte is diploid, recessive (less conspicuous), and partially dependent on the gametophyte.

Slide 16: Economic Importance of Bryophytes

  1. Food Source: Some mosses provide food for herbaceous mammals, birds, and other animals.

  2. Sphagnum (Peat Moss):

    • Provides peat, which has long been used as fuel.

    • Its high water-holding capacity makes it useful as packing material for the trans-shipment of living material (e.g., nursery plants).

  3. Ecological Pioneers: Mosses, along with lichens, are often the first organisms to colonize bare rocks. They decompose rocks, creating a substrate suitable for the growth of higher plants.

  4. Soil Erosion Prevention: Since mosses form dense mats on the soil, they reduce the impact of falling rain and help prevent soil erosion.

Slides 17-18: Subgroups of Bryophyta

  • Bryophyta is divided into three main classes:

    1. Hepaticopsida (Liverworts)

    2. Anthoceratopsida (Hornworts) (Briefly mentioned, not detailed on slides)

    3. Bryopsida (Mosses)

  • Hepaticopsida (Liverworts) - Slide 17:

    • Plant Body: The plant body of a liverwort is thalloid (flat, undifferentiated), e.g., Marchantia.

    • The thallus is dorsiventral (has distinct upper and lower surfaces) and closely appressed (pressed flat) to the substrate.

    • Leafy members have tiny leaf-like appendages in two rows on stem-like structures.

    • Asexual Reproduction:

      • By fragmentation of thalli.

      • By formation of specialized structures called gemmae (singular: gemma). Gemmae are green, multicellular, asexual buds that develop in small receptacles called gemma cups on the thalli. Gemmae detach and germinate to form new individuals.

    • Sexual Reproduction:

      • Male and female sex organs are produced either on the same thallus or on different thalli.

      • The sporophyte is differentiated into a foot, seta, and capsule.

      • After meiosis, spores are produced within the capsule. These spores germinate to form free-living gametophytes.

  • Bryopsida (Mosses) - Slide 18:

    • Gametophyte Stages: The gametophyte consists of two stages:

      1. Protonema stage: Develops directly from a spore; it's a filamentous, branched structure.

      2. Leafy stage: Develops from the secondary protonema as a lateral bud. Consists of upright, slender axes bearing spirally arranged "leaves." Attached to the soil through multicellular, branched rhizoids. This stage bears the sex organs.

    • Vegetative Reproduction: In mosses, occurs by fragmentation and budding in the secondary protonema.

    • Sexual Reproduction:

      • Sex organs (antheridia and archegonia) are produced at the apex (tip) of the leafy shoots.

      • After fertilization, the zygote develops into a sporophyte, consisting of a foot, seta, and capsule.

    • Sporophyte Complexity: The sporophyte in mosses is more elaborate (more complex in structure) than that in liverworts.

    • Spore Production & Dispersal: The capsule contains spores, formed after meiosis. Mosses often have an elaborate mechanism for spore dispersal.

    • Common Examples: Funaria, Polytrichum, Sphagnum.

    • Image: Photograph of moss plants with visible sporophytes (capsules on setas).

C. Pteridophyta (Ferns and Allies)

Slide 11: Pteridophytes (Brief Overview)

  • Body Differentiation: Have well-differentiated structures such as true stems, roots, and leaves.

  • Vascular System: Possess a vascular system (xylem and phloem).

Slide 19: PTERIDOPHYTA (General Characteristics & Images)

  • Evolutionary Significance:

    • The first vascular plants.

    • The first successful terrestrial plants with true roots, stem, and leaves.

    • However, they do not produce flowers, fruits, or seeds.

  • Common Name: Called VASCULAR CRYPTOGAMS.

  • Groups Included: Pteridophytes include horsetails and ferns.

  • Habitat & Growth:

    • Terrestrial, small, either annual or perennial.

    • Grow luxuriantly in cool, moist, and shady places (e.g., ferns in deep forests).

    • Some may be:

      • Aquatic: e.g., Azolla, Marsilea.

      • Xerophytic (adapted to dry conditions): e.g., Equisetum.

      • Epiphytic (growing on other plants, like tree trunks): e.g., Lycopodium.

  • Images: Various pteridophytes: Selaginella, Pteris (a fern), Dryopteris (a fern), Adiantum (maidenhair fern), Equisetum (horsetail).

Slide 20: PTERIDOPHYTA (Plant Body)

  • Roots: The primary root is short-lived and is soon replaced by adventitious roots (roots that arise from non-root tissue, like stems).

  • Stem: May be aerial or underground (e.g., rhizome in ferns).

  • Leaves:

    • May be scaly (e.g., Equisetum).

    • May be simple and sessile (without a stalk) (e.g., Lycopodium).

    • May be large and pinnately compound (divided into leaflets) (e.g., Ferns - these large leaves are called fronds).

  • Leaf Size Classification:

    • Microphylls: Small leaves, often with a single, unbranched vein (e.g., in Selaginella, lycophytes).

    • Macrophylls: Large leaves with a complex system of branching veins (e.g., in Ferns).

  • Vascular Tissue Composition:

    • Xylem: Consists of only tracheids (water-conducting cells). (Vessel elements, found in angiosperms, are generally absent).

    • Phloem: Consists of sieve cells only. (Companion cells, found in angiosperms, are absent).

  • Secondary Growth: Secondary growth (increase in girth/thickness, typical of woody plants) is not seen in Pteridophytes due to the absence of cambium (a lateral meristem responsible for secondary growth).

  • Image: Diagram of a fern showing frond, pinnae (leaflets), stipe (leaf stalk), stolon (horizontal stem), caudex (upright stem/rhizome), and adventitious roots.

Slide 21: PTERIDOPHYTA (Life Cycle - Heteromorphic Alternation of Generation)

  • Core Concept: Pteridophytes exhibit a heteromorphic alternation of generations, meaning the sporophyte and gametophyte generations are morphologically distinct. The sporophyte is the dominant generation.

  • Diagram Explanation (Synthesized from slide and transcript):

    1. Sporophyte (2n, Dominant): The familiar fern plant with fronds (leaves), rhizome (underground stem), and roots.

    2. Sori (singular: Sorus): On the underside of fronds, clusters of sporangia (spore-producing structures) form, called sori. Sori are often covered by a protective flap called an indusium.

    3. Sporangium: Each sporangium has a ring of thickened cells called an annulus, which helps in spore dispersal by causing the sporangium to burst open.

    4. Meiosis: Inside the sporangium, spore mother cells undergo MEIOSIS to produce haploid spores (1n).

    5. Spore Germination: Spores are released and germinate in moist soil, developing into a small, heart-shaped, photosynthetic structure called a prothallus (gametophyte, 1n). The prothallus has rhizoids for anchorage.

    6. Gametophyte (Prothallus, 1n): The prothallus bears both male sex organs (antheridia, producing sperm) and female sex organs (archegonia, producing an egg).

    7. Fertilization (Requires Water): Sperm swim through a film of water to reach the archegonium and fertilize the egg, forming a zygote (2n).

    8. Young Sporophyte Development: The zygote develops into a young sporophyte while still attached to the gametophyte. The young sporophyte grows, with its first leaves often appearing as coiled fiddleheads, and eventually becomes the independent, dominant fern plant. The gametophyte then degenerates.

  • Transcript Elaboration: "The fern actually forms spores that are deposited on the leaves as yellow sacks... sporangium bursts out open, releasing spores... each spore germinates in the soil to form rhizoids... two types of gametophytes form, female and male [the prothallus typically bears both]... unification through fertilization forms the new zygote... that grows into a full bona fide fern."

Slides 22-23: PTERIDOPHYTA (Reproduction - Detailed Text)

  • Sporophyte Phase (2n):

    • Shows asexual reproduction by producing spores via meiosis.

    • The sporophyte is the dominant phase of the pteridophytes.

  • Gametophyte Phase (1n):

    • Develops from a spore.

    • Is haploid, recessive (less conspicuous), but independent (free-living and photosynthetic).

    • Reproduces sexually, producing gametes.

    • The product of sexual reproduction (zygote) develops into the diploid sporophyte.

  • Spore Production:

    • Spores are produced in special multicellular structures called sporangia.

    • Sporangia are borne on leaf-like appendages called sporophylls.

    • In some pteridophytes (e.g., Selaginella, Equisetum), sporophylls may form distinct compact structures called strobili or cones.

    • Sporangia produce spores by meiosis in spore mother cells.

  • Gametophyte (Prothallus):

    • Spores germinate to give rise to inconspicuous, small but multicellular, free-living, mostly photosynthetic thalloid gametophytes called the prothallus.

    • These gametophytes require cool, damp, shady places to grow.

    • The gametophytes bear male sex organs (antheridia) and female sex organs (archegonia).

    • Water is required for the transfer of antherozoids (male gametes released from antheridia) to the mouth of the archegonium.

    • Fusion of the male gamete with the egg in the archegonium results in the formation of the zygote (2n).

  • Homospory vs. Heterospory:

    • Homosporous: In the majority of pteridophytes, all spores are of similar kinds (i.e., they produce only one type of spore, which develops into a bisexual gametophyte bearing both antheridia and archegonia).

    • Heterosporous: Genera like Selaginella and Salvinia produce two kinds of spores:

      • Megaspores: Large spores that germinate to give rise to female gametophytes.

      • Microspores: Small spores that germinate to give rise to male gametophytes.

    • In heterosporous plants, the female gametophytes are often retained on the parent sporophyte for variable periods. The development of zygotes into young embryos takes place within the female gametophytes. This is considered an important step towards seed evolution.

Slide 24: PTERIDOPHYTA (Alternative Reproduction & Classification)

  • Alternative Reproduction Methods:

    1. Apogamy: The development of the sporophyte directly from the gametophyte without the fusion of male and female gametes. The resulting sporophyte is haploid.

    2. Apospory: The development of the gametophyte from any cell of the sporophyte other than the haploid spores. Such a gametophyte is diploid in nature.

  • Classification (Four Main Groups - Instructor noted not to memorize these specific names for the exam):

    • Psilopsida: e.g., Psilotum

    • Lycopsida: e.g., Selaginella, Lycopodium (club mosses)

    • Sphenopsida: e.g., Equisetum (horsetails)

    • Pteropsida: e.g., Pteris, Dryopteris, Adiantum (true ferns)

1. Angiosperms (Flowering Plants)

Slide 25: ANGIOSPERMS (General Characteristics & Diagram)

  • Evolutionary Status: The most advanced division of the flowering plants; highly evolved.

  • Habitat: Primarily adapted to terrestrial habitats.

  • Size Range:

    • Wolffia is the smallest angiosperm (1 mm in size).

    • Eucalyptus can grow to over 100 meters.

  • Plant Body: Differentiated into root, stem, and leaves.

  • Key Features: Possesses flowers, fruits, and seeds.

    • Flowers: Reproductive structures.

    • Fruits: Develop from the ovary (part of the flower) and enclose the seeds, aiding in seed dispersal.

    • Seeds: Contain the embryo and nutritive tissue, protected by a seed coat.

  • Vascular Tissues: Well-developed.

    • Xylem: Shows vessels (tracheae) in addition to tracheids (more efficient water transport).

    • Phloem: Has sieve tubes and companion cells (more efficient sugar transport).

  • Diagram: A typical angiosperm plant showing:

    • Root system: Primary root, secondary roots.

    • Shoot system: Stem, leaf (with node, internode), bud, flower, fruit.

Slide 26: ANGIOSPERMS (Heteromorphic Alternation of Generation - Diagram)

  • Life Cycle Overview:

    • SPOROPHYTIC (2n) GENERATION (Diploid, Autotrophic, Dominant): This is the main plant body (e.g., tree, herb).

      • Produces flowers.

      • Within the flower:

        • Anther (male part): Contains microsporangia where microspores (pollen grains) are produced.

        • Ovary (female part): Contains ovules (megasporangia) where the megaspore (leading to the embryo sac) is produced.

    • GAMETOPHYTIC (n) GENERATION (Haploid, Dependent, Recessive):

      • Male gametophyte: The pollen grain, which contains sperm nuclei.

      • Female gametophyte: The embryo sac, located within the ovule, containing the egg cell.

    • Pollination & Fertilization: Pollen is transferred to the stigma, a pollen tube grows, and double fertilization occurs.

    • Zygote (2n): Develops into the embryo.

    • Seed & Fruit: The ovule develops into a seed (containing the embryo), and the ovary develops into a fruit.

    • The seed germinates to form a new sporophyte.

  • Transcript Elaboration: "Stigma and anther are the female and male sex organ names in flowering plants... undergoing spore formation allows microspore pollen grain formation. And the male gametophyte and female gametophyte unites, forming the zygote, giving rise to the embryo."

Slide 27: ANGIOSPERMS (Male & Female Gametophyte Development)

  • Development of a Male Gametophyte (in Pollen Grain):

    1. Microsporangium (pollen sac) in the anther contains microsporocytes (2n).

    2. Microsporocyte undergoes MEIOSIS to produce four microspores (n).

    3. Each microspore undergoes MITOSIS to develop into a pollen grain (male gametophyte).

    4. A mature pollen grain typically contains two cells: a generative cell (n) (which will later divide to form two sperm cells) and a tube cell (n) (whose nucleus is the tube cell nucleus).

    • Image: Ragweed pollen grain.

  • Development of a Female Gametophyte (Embryo Sac):

    1. Within the ovule (megasporangium), a megasporocyte (2n) is present. The ovule is covered by integuments (2n) and has an opening called the micropyle.

    2. Megasporocyte undergoes MEIOSIS to produce four megaspores (n), but typically only one survives.

    3. The surviving megaspore (n) undergoes three rounds of MITOSIS (without cytokinesis initially, then cell walls form) to develop into the embryo sac (female gametophyte).

    4. A mature embryo sac typically contains:

      • 3 antipodal cells (n) (at the end opposite the micropyle).

      • 2 polar nuclei (n + n) (in the large central cell).

      • 1 egg cell (n) (near the micropyle).

      • 2 synergids (n) (flanking the egg cell, help guide the pollen tube).

Slides 28-29: ANGIOSPERMS (Double Fertilization)

  • Core Concept: Double Fertilization is a unique characteristic of angiosperms.

  • Process (Illustrated in diagrams):

    1. Pollination: A pollen grain lands on the stigma of a flower.

    2. Pollen Tube Growth: The pollen grain germinates, and the tube cell forms a pollen tube that grows down through the style towards the ovary and ovule. The generative cell divides by mitosis within the pollen tube to form two sperm nuclei (n).

    3. Entry into Ovule: The pollen tube enters the ovule (usually through the micropyle) and penetrates one of the synergids. The synergid breaks down.

    4. Discharge of Sperm: The two sperm nuclei are discharged into the embryo sac.

    5. Fertilization Events:

      • First Fertilization: One sperm nucleus (n) fuses with the egg cell nucleus (n) to form the zygote (2n). The zygote will develop into the embryo.

      • Second Fertilization: The other sperm nucleus (n) fuses with the two polar nuclei (n + n) in the central cell to form the primary endosperm nucleus (3n). This triploid nucleus will develop into the endosperm, a nutritive tissue that nourishes the developing embryo.

  • Outcome:

    • The ovule develops into a seed, containing the diploid embryo and triploid endosperm, enclosed by a seed coat (derived from integuments).

    • The ovary (and sometimes other floral parts) develops into a fruit, which encloses the seed(s).

  • [Instructor Emphasis on "Double Fertilization"]: The instructor noted that plants can undergo "two consecutive fertilization events," which refers to these two distinct fusion events occurring within the embryo sac.

2. Gymnosperms (Conifers and Allies)

Slide 31: GYMNOSPERMS (General Characteristics & Diagram)

  • General Features: Most Gymnosperms are evergreen, perennial woody trees or shrubs (e.g., pine, fir, spruce, cycads).

  • Ovules & Seeds:

    • The term "gymnosperm" means "naked seed."

    • The ovules are not enclosed by any ovary wall and remain exposed, both before and after fertilization.

    • They are non-flowering plants that produce naked seeds (i.e., seeds are not enclosed within a fruit).

  • Vascular Tissues:

    • Xylem: Has tracheids (for water conduction). (Vessels are generally absent, except in Gnetophytes).

    • Phloem: Has sieve cells (for sugar transport). (Companion cells are absent).

  • Diagram: Compares a gymnosperm ovule (exposed on a scale/sporophyll) with an angiosperm ovule (enclosed within an ovary).

Slide 32: GYMNOSPERMS (Plant Body: Roots, Stem, Leaves)

  • The plant body (sporophyte) is differentiated into root, stem, and leaves.

  • ROOTS:

    • Often a taproot system.

    • Specialized roots:

      • Coralloid roots of Cycas show association with N2-fixing blue-green algae (cyanobacteria).

      • Roots of Pinus show association with endophytic fungi called mycorrhizae (symbiosis that aids nutrient absorption).

  • STEM:

    • The gymnospermic stem is mostly erect, aerial, solid, and cylindrical.

    • In Cycas, it is unbranched.

    • In Pinus, Cedrus, and other conifers, it is branched.

  • LEAVES:

    • Often dimorphic (two types of leaves):

      • Foliage leaves: Photosynthetic leaves; can be simple, needle-like (e.g., pines), or pinnately compound (e.g., cycads).

      • Scale leaves: Small, membranous, and brown, often protective.

Slides 33-34: GYMNOSPERMS (Reproduction: Heterospory, Cones, Gametophytes)

  • Heterospory: Gymnosperms are heterosporous; they produce two types of haploid spores:

    • Microspores (male)

    • Megaspores (female)

  • Spore Production: The two kinds of spores are produced within sporangia that are borne on leafy structures called sporophylls. Sporophylls are typically arranged spirally along an axis to form compact structures called strobili or cones.

  • Male Cones (Male Strobili) - Slide 33:

    • Bear microsporophylls and microsporangia.

    • Microspores develop within the microsporangia.

    • Each microspore develops into a male gametophytic generation which is reduced and confined to only a limited number of cells. This reduced male gametophyte is called a pollen grain.

    • The development of pollen grains takes place within the microsporangia.

    • Image: Male cone of a cycad.

  • Female Cones (Female Strobili) - Slide 34:

    • Bear megasporophylls with ovules.

    • Male or female strobili may be borne on the same tree (monoecious, e.g., Pinus) or on different trees (dioecious, e.g., Cycas).

    • Within the ovule, the megaspore mother cell (located in the nucellus, the nutritive tissue of the ovule) undergoes meiosis to produce megaspores.

    • Image: Female cone of a cycad.

Slides 37-38: GYMNOSPERMS (Life Cycle - Heteromorphic Alternation of Generation - Diagrams)

  • Life Cycle Overview (Synthesized from diagrams and text):

    • SPOROPHYTE (2n, Diploid, Dominant, Independent): The mature tree (e.g., pine tree).

    • Cone Production: Produces separate male (pollen) cones and female (seed/ovulate) cones.

    • Male Cone Development:

      • Microsporophylls bear microsporangia.

      • Microsporocytes (2n) in microsporangia undergo MEIOSIS to produce microspores (n).

      • Microspores develop into pollen grains (male gametophytes, n).

    • Female Cone Development:

      • Megasporophylls (ovuliferous scales) bear ovules.

      • Each ovule contains a megasporangium (nucellus) with a megasporocyte (2n).

      • Megasporocyte undergoes MEIOSIS to produce four megaspores (n); typically, only one survives.

      • The surviving megaspore develops into the female gametophyte (n), which contains one or more archegonia, each with an egg cell (n). The female gametophyte is retained within the ovule.

    • Pollination: Pollen grains are dispersed by wind and land near the micropyle (opening) of an ovule.

    • Pollen Tube Growth & Fertilization: The pollen grain germinates, forming a pollen tube that grows towards an archegonium. Sperm cells are released from the pollen tube, and one fertilizes the egg, forming a zygote (2n).

    • Seed Development: The zygote develops into an embryo (2n). The ovule matures into a seed, which consists of the embryo, stored food (haploid female gametophyte tissue), and a seed coat (derived from the integument of the ovule).

    • Seed Dispersal & Germination: The naked seed is dispersed and, under favorable conditions, germinates to grow into a new sporophyte tree.

    • GAMETOPHYTE (n, Haploid, Recessive, Dependent): The male (pollen grain) and female (within ovule) gametophytes are highly reduced and develop within, and are dependent on, the sporophyte.

IV. Plant Life Cycles

Slide 42: LIFE CYCLES (Haplontic, Haplo-diplontic, Diplontic)

  • This slide summarizes the three main types of life cycles found in organisms, particularly relevant to algae and plants, focusing on the relative prominence of the haploid (n) and diploid (2n) phases.

  • 1. Haplontic Life Cycle:

    • The dominant, photosynthetic phase in such plants (or algae) is the free-living haploid gametophyte (n).

    • The diploid phase is restricted to the zygote (2n), which typically undergoes meiosis immediately to produce haploid spores or cells.

    • Examples: Many algae such as Volvox, Spirogyra.

    • Diagram: Shows a large haploid (n) phase and a very small diploid (2n) phase (zygote only).

  • 2. Haplo-diplontic Life Cycle (Alternation of Generations):

    • Exhibits an intermediate condition where both the haploid gametophyte (n) phase and the diploid sporophyte (2n) phase are multicellular.

    • These two phases alternate.

    • They differ in their dominant phases:

      • Bryophytes: The gametophyte (n) is dominant, photosynthetic, and independent. The sporophyte (2n) is smaller and dependent on the gametophyte.

      • Pteridophytes: The sporophyte (2n) is dominant, photosynthetic, and independent. The gametophyte (n) is smaller but also free-living and photosynthetic (though less conspicuous).

    • Diagram: Shows significant multicellular haploid and diploid phases.

  • 3. Diplontic Life Cycle:

    • The diploid sporophyte (2n) is the dominant, photosynthetic, independent phase of the plant.

    • The haploid gametophyte (n) phase is highly reduced and typically dependent on the sporophyte (e.g., pollen grain and embryo sac in seed plants).

    • Meiosis produces haploid gametes directly (in animals) or spores that develop into very reduced gametophytes which then produce gametes (in seed plants).

    • Examples: Gymnosperms and Angiosperms (also animals, including humans).

    • Diagram: Shows a large diploid (2n) phase and a very reduced haploid (n) phase.

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 5: Kingdom of Plantae)

  • Kingdom Plantae: Multicellular, eukaryotic, autotrophic (photosynthetic via chlorophyll in chloroplasts), non-motile, with cell walls (cellulose).

  • Classification Criteria: Plant body differentiation, presence/absence of vascular system, presence/absence of seeds/flowers/fruits.

  • Major Plant Groups & Evolutionary Trends:

    • Cryptogams (Non-flowering, spore-bearing):

      • Thallophyta (Algae as ancestors): Simple thallus body, aquatic, no vascular tissue.

      • Bryophyta (Mosses, Liverworts): "Amphibians of plant kingdom," non-vascular, gametophyte dominant, sporophyte dependent, require water for fertilization.

      • Pteridophyta (Ferns): First vascular plants, true roots/stems/leaves, sporophyte dominant, free-living gametophyte, require water for fertilization, no seeds. Homosporous or heterosporous.

    • Phanerogams (Seed-bearing):

      • Gymnosperms (Conifers, Cycads): "Naked seeds" (not enclosed in fruit), non-flowering (cones), vascular, sporophyte dominant, wind-pollinated, siphonogamy.

      • Angiosperms (Flowering Plants): Most advanced, seeds enclosed in fruits, flowers, vascular, sporophyte dominant, double fertilization (forms 2n zygote and 3n endosperm). Divided into Monocots and Dicots.

  • Alternation of Generations: All plants exhibit this, but the relative dominance of gametophyte (n) vs. sporophyte (2n) changes:

    • Bryophytes: Gametophyte dominant.

    • Pteridophytes: Sporophyte dominant, but gametophyte is free-living.

    • Seed Plants (Gymnosperms & Angiosperms): Sporophyte dominant, gametophyte highly reduced and dependent.

  • Life Cycle Types:

    • Haplontic: Dominant haploid gametophyte (e.g., some algae).

    • Haplo-diplontic: Multicellular haploid and diploid phases (e.g., Bryophytes, Pteridophytes).

    • Diplontic: Dominant diploid sporophyte (e.g., Gymnosperms, Angiosperms, Animals).

  • Key Reproductive Structures/Events: Spores, gametes, antheridia, archegonia, cones (strobili), flowers, pollen, ovules, seeds, fruits, double fertilization, siphonogamy.

IV. Early Concepts of Evolution

(This section discusses historical figures and ideas that preceded or contributed to Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection.)

A. Jean Baptiste Lamarck (Slides 33-37)

Slide 33: Early Concepts of Evolution: Jean Baptiste Lamarck

  • Historical Context:

    • Darwin was not the first naturalist to propose that species changed over time (evolved).

    • In the 18th century, Buffon and others began to suggest life might not be fixed since creation.

    • By the end of the 1700s, paleontologists had extensive fossil collections showing a changing natural world.

    • In 1801, French naturalist Jean Baptiste Lamarck proposed a full-blown theory of evolution.

  • Lamarck's Background:

    • Started as a botanist.

    • Became a professor and expert on invertebrates at the Musee National d'Histoire Naturelle in 1793.

    • His work on classifying invertebrates (worms, spiders, mollusks) was ahead of its time.

  • Image: Portrait of Jean Baptiste Lamarck.

Slide 34: Lamarck's Theory: Change Through Use and Disuse

  • Observation: Lamarck was struck by similarities among animals and the fossil record, leading him to argue that life was not fixed.

  • Mechanism 1: Use and Disuse:

    • When environments changed, organisms had to change their behavior to survive.

    • If an organ was used more frequently, it would increase in size or strength during an organism's lifetime.

      • Example: If a giraffe stretched its neck for higher leaves, a "nervous fluid" would flow into its neck, making it longer.

    • If an organ was not used, it would shrink or atrophy.

  • Mechanism 2: Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics:

    • Changes acquired during an organism's lifetime (like the giraffe's stretched neck) would be inherited by its offspring.

    • Continued stretching over generations would lead to progressively longer necks.

  • Image: Three giraffes with progressively longer necks over "TIME," reaching for higher leaves. Caption: "Lamarck believed that the long necks of giraffes evolved as generations of giraffes reached for ever higher leaves."

  • Transcript Elaboration: "If they began to use an organ more than they had in the past, it would increase in its lifetime... Its offspring would inherit the longer neck and continued stretching would make it longer still over several generations. Meanwhile, organs that organisms stopped using would shrink."

Slide 35: Lamarck's Theory: Organisms Driven to Greater Complexity

  • Second Proposed Mechanism: Besides adaptation through use/disuse, Lamarck believed nature drove organisms inexorably upward from simple forms to increasingly complex ones.

  • Spontaneous Generation: Like Buffon, Lamarck believed life had begun through spontaneous generation.

  • Continuous Origin of Life: He claimed new primitive life forms sprang up throughout history; today's microbes were "the new kids on the block."

  • Image: An arrow pointing from "Simple forms" to "Complex forms." Caption: "Lamarck also proposed that organisms were driven from simple to increasingly more complex forms."

Slide 36: Lamarck's Theory: Evolution by Natural Processes

  • Core Idea: Lamarck proposed that life took on its current form through natural processes, not miraculous interventions.

  • Reaction from British Naturalists:

    • This was appalling to British naturalists steeped in natural theology (believing nature reflected God's benevolent design).

    • They saw Lamarck's theory as attributing change to "blind primal forces."

  • Lamarck's Fate: Rejected on religious grounds and shunned by scientists like Cuvier for lack of deductive rigor, Lamarck died in 1829 in poverty and obscurity.

  • Legacy of Evolutionary Thought:

    • The notion of evolution persisted.

    • Geoffroy St. Hilaire (1820s) championed another version of evolutionary change.

    • Robert Chambers (1844) authored Vestiges of a Natural Creation, a best-selling argument for evolution.

    • Charles Darwin (1859) published The Origin of Species.

    • Lamarck, St. Hilaire, Chambers, and Darwin had different ideas, but only Darwin's has scientific currency today.

Slide 37: Lamarck vs. Darwin - A Comparison

  • Table Comparing Lamarck and Darwin:

  • Darwin's Use of Evidence: Darwin used much of the same evidence as Lamarck (e.g., vestigial structures, artificial selection) but made different arguments.

  • Darwin's View on Complexity: Darwin did not accept an arrow of complexity driving through life's history. He argued complexity evolved as life adapted to local conditions.

  • Darwin's Ideas on Heredity: Darwin's ideas on heredity were not entirely modern; he struggled with how traits were passed on and even considered (and rejected) inheritance of acquired characteristics.

  • Lamarckian Inheritance Today: Known mainly from textbooks as a historical contrast to modern genetic inheritance (Mendel's work).

  • Lamarck's Credit: Despite being largely incorrect in his mechanisms, Lamarck is credited with envisioning evolutionary change for the first time.

  • [Instructor Emphasis]: "where they actually fell apart is at this point, Lamarck thought that there is no extinction... Whereas Darwin believed in extinctions."

B. Developmental Similarities: Karl von Baer (Slides 38-41)

Slide 38: Developmental Similarities: Karl von Baer - Early Observations

  • Question: How does life begin?

  • 19th Century Embryology: Naturalists used microscopes to study embryos.

  • Peculiar Similarities:

    • Embryos of different species (e.g., chicken vs. fish) share striking similarities despite adult differences.

    • Both develop from a single cell into tube-shaped bodies.

    • Early shared traits: e.g., a set of arching blood vessels in their necks (pharyngeal arches/gill arches).

      • In fish, these vessels retain this arrangement for gills (oxygen uptake from water).

      • In chickens, mammals, amphibians, and reptiles, they are reworked into different anatomies for lung-based oxygen uptake.

  • Early Interpretation (Recapitulation):

    • Some German researchers claimed these similarities showed life formed a series from simple to lofty forms.

    • They proposed that embryos "recapitulate" this series during development (e.g., humans start as a worm, become a fish, then a reptile, etc.).

    • Some claimed recapitulation was evidence that life had changed over time, with higher forms emerging.

  • Transcript Elaboration: "So in our nine-month incubation in our mother's wombs, we actually recapitulate the entire 3 billion year evolutionary process in a fast-forwarded form in this theory."

Slide 39: von Baer: Recapitulation is Kaput

  • Karl von Baer's Critique (1828):

    • The Estonian-born embryologist launched a "withering attack" on the theory of recapitulation.

    • Careful study of embryos showed it was impossible to arrange them in a meaningful linear series.

    • Vertebrate vs. Invertebrate Development: From early stages, vertebrates share an anatomy that invertebrates (like insects or worms) never acquire.

    • Clashes within Vertebrates:

      • A human does not develop a wing or hoof before forming a hand.

      • Humans, birds, and horses all begin with limb buds, which then diverge into different adult limbs.

  • "Kaput" means broken or finished in German.

  • Image: Portrait of Karl von Baer.

Slide 40: Compelling Evidence for Evolution (Darwin's Use of von Baer's Work)

  • Von Baer's Stance: Baer was not a fan of evolution.

  • Darwin's Interpretation: Darwin used von Baer's work to provide compelling evidence in Origin of Species.

    • A species inherits its developmental program from its ancestors.

    • Closely related species would be expected to have similar (but not identical) embryos.

    • As lineages evolve, natural selection modifies their embryos, but some vestiges of common ancestry survive (e.g., limited resemblance to fish in early human embryonic stages).

  • Darwin's View of Life's Pattern:

    • Life was not arrayed in a linear series (lower to higher).

    • He saw life branching like a tree as new species emerged.

    • This branching was reflected in similar developmental paths that ultimately produced diverse structures (hooves, claws, hands).

  • Image: A ladder representing linear progression vs. a branching tree representing Darwin's view.

Slide 41: Bridging Vertebrates and Invertebrates (Tunicates)

  • Baer's Claim Challenged: Baer claimed vertebrates couldn't be aligned with invertebrates.

  • Mid-1800s Embryology: Showed the division was not unbridgeable.

  • Sea Squirts (Tunicates):

    • These invertebrates develop a notochord (a stiff rod in the back) as embryos, similar to vertebrate embryos.

    • In vertebrates, the notochord contributes to the disks between vertebrae (part of the spinal column).

    • If this shared notochord indicated common ancestry, sea squirts should be close relatives of vertebrates.

  • DNA Evidence: Studies on sea squirt DNA confirm they are the closest known invertebrate relatives of vertebrates.

  • Images: Adult tunicates (sea squirts) on a reef; a tunicate larva with a clearly labeled notochord.

C. Natural Selection: Charles Darwin & Alfred Russel Wallace (Slides 42-48)

Slide 42: Natural Selection: Charles Darwin & Alfred Russel Wallace - Introduction

  • Darwin's Impact: Darwin's Origin of Species (1859) turned biology upside down.

  • Misconception: The theory of evolution did not spring fully formed from Darwin's mind without precedent.

  • Pre-Darwinian Knowledge (Raw Material for Darwin's Theory):

    • Geologists & Paleontologists: Had shown Earth was old, life had changed over time, and many species had become extinct.

    • Embryologists & Naturalists (early 1800s): Had discovered much evidence relevant to Darwin's theory, sometimes unwittingly.

  • Image: Portrait of Charles Darwin.

Slide 43: Pre-Darwinian Ideas About Evolution

  • Darwin's Genius:

    • Showed how evidence favored evolution from a common ancestor.

    • Offered a plausible mechanism for how life might evolve: natural selection.

  • Previous Evolutionary Theories (e.g., Lamarck):

    • Depended on speculation to explain how life changed.

    • Typically claimed evolution was guided by some long-term trend (e.g., Lamarck's drive from simple to complex forms).

    • Some German biologists conceived of life evolving according to predetermined rules (like embryonic development).

  • Darwin and Wallace's Contribution (mid-1800s):

    • Independently conceived of a natural, observable way for life to change: the process Darwin called natural selection.

Slide 44: Darwin's Finches (Galapagos Islands)

  • Image Description: A phylogenetic tree of Galapagos finches, showing different beak shapes. Species are grouped by genus (G. for Geospiza, C. for Camarhynchus or Certhidea). The tree originates from a "Common ancestor."

  • Caption/Text: "A visit to the Galapagos Islands in 1835 helped Darwin formulate his ideas on natural selection. He found several species of finch adapted to different environmental niches. The finches also differed in beak shape, food source, and how food was captured."

  • Significance: This observation of variation and adaptation was crucial for Darwin's thinking.

Slide 45: The Pressure of Population Growth (Influence of Malthus)

  • Inspiration from Economics: Darwin and Wallace found inspiration in economics.

  • Thomas Malthus (1797): Published Essay on the Principle of Population.

    • Warned that policies to help the poor were doomed due to relentless population growth.

    • A nation could easily double its population in decades, leading to famine and misery.

  • Application to Nature:

    • Darwin and Wallace realized animals and plants also experience population pressure.

    • Species could reproduce to their full potential, quickly overrunning the world, but they don't.

    • Reasons for Limited Population Growth:

      • Many die before adulthood.

      • Vulnerable to environmental assaults (droughts, cold).

      • Food supply is not infinite.

      • Individuals must compete for limited resources.

Slide 46: Selection of Traits

  • Struggle for Existence: Survival and reproduction are not purely by chance.

  • Advantageous Traits: If an animal has a trait that helps it withstand environmental challenges or breed more successfully, it may leave more offspring.

  • Increased Frequency: On average, the advantageous trait will become more common in subsequent generations.

  • Analogy with Artificial Selection (Pigeon Breeding):

    • Darwin studied pigeon breeders.

    • Breeders select individual birds with desired traits (e.g., a neck ruffle) to reproduce.

    • Similarly, nature unconsciously "selects" individuals better suited to surviving their local conditions.

  • Outcome of Natural Selection: Given enough time, natural selection might produce new types of body parts (e.g., wings, eyes).

  • Images: Wild rock pigeon (top), and two derived breeds: carrier pigeon (bottom left) and Brunner pouter (bottom right).

Slide 47: Darwin and Wallace Develop Similar Theory

  • Darwin's Timeline: Began formulating his theory in the late 1830s, worked quietly for 20 years to amass evidence.

  • Wallace's Work: Explored wildlife in South America and Asia. Supplied Darwin with birds for study.

  • Wallace's Independent Theory: Decided to publish his own ideas on evolution and sent his theory to Darwin in 1858. To Darwin's shock, it nearly replicated his own.

  • Joint Presentation (1858):

    • Charles Lyell and Joseph Dalton Hooker arranged for both Darwin's and Wallace's theories to be presented at a meeting of the Linnean Society.

  • Publications:

    • Darwin: Published On the Origin of Species in 1859, based on his extensive work.

    • Wallace: Continued travels and focused on the importance of biogeography.

  • Images: Portrait of Alfred Russel Wallace; cover of Darwin's On the Origin of Species.

Slide 48: Reception of Darwin's Theory

  • Origin of Species Impact: A bestseller and one of the most influential scientific books.

  • Acceptance of Evolution: Within a few decades, most scientists accepted evolution and the descent of species from common ancestors.

  • Acceptance of Natural Selection: Natural selection had a harder time finding acceptance.

    • In the late 1800s, many "Darwinists" actually preferred Lamarckian explanations for how life changed.

  • Solidification of Natural Selection: It took the discovery of genes and mutations in the 20th century to make natural selection "not just attractive as an explanation, but unavoidable."

  • Image: Cover of Darwin's On the Origin of Species.

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 6: Kingdom of Animalia & Evolutionary Concepts)

  • Kingdom Animalia Classification: Broadly divided into Vertebrates (with a backbone, endoskeleton) and Invertebrates (without a backbone, 97% of animals).

  • Vertebrates (Phylum Chordata):

    • Can be ectotherms (cold-blooded, e.g., fish, amphibians, reptiles) or endotherms (warm-blooded, e.g., birds, mammals).

    • Classes:

      • Fish: Cold-blooded, gills, scales, fins, swim bladder. Sub-classes: Bony, Cartilaginous, Jawless.

      • Amphibians: Smooth skin, aquatic larval stage, metamorphosis, cold-blooded. (e.g., frogs, salamanders).

      • Reptiles: Dry/scaly skin, mostly terrestrial, cold-blooded, soft-shelled eggs. (e.g., lizards, snakes, turtles).

      • Birds: Warm-blooded, feathers, hollow bones, flight (most), hard-shelled eggs.

      • Mammals: Hair/fur, live birth (most), milk, complex brains, warm-blooded.

  • Invertebrates: Classified by phyla and body symmetry (radial, bilateral, asymmetric).

    • Key Phyla/Groups: Cnidarians (radial, stinging cells), Sponges (asymmetric, filter feeders), Roundworms (abundant, predators/parasites), Arthropods (exoskeleton, jointed legs - Crustaceans, Arachnids, Insects), Flatworms (bilateral, often parasitic), Annelids (segmented worms), Mollusks (soft bodies, many with shells), Echinoderms (spiny skin, radial, tube feet).

  • Early Evolutionary Thought (Pre-Darwin/Contemporary):

    • Jean Baptiste Lamarck: Proposed evolution by natural processes. Key ideas:

      • Use and Disuse: Organs change based on usage.

      • Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics: Lifetime changes passed to offspring.

      • Drive towards increasing complexity; no extinction.

    • Karl von Baer: Criticized recapitulation theory. Emphasized divergence in embryonic development from common starting points. His work on embryology provided evidence for common ancestry used by Darwin. Tunicates (sea squirts) highlighted as invertebrate relatives of vertebrates due to larval notochord.

  • Natural Selection (Darwin & Wallace):

    • Independently developed theory.

    • Influenced by Malthus (population pressure, struggle for existence).

    • Mechanism: Variation exists; individuals with advantageous traits for survival/reproduction leave more offspring; these traits become more common over generations.

    • Origin of Species (1859) by Darwin was pivotal.

    • Evolution accepted relatively quickly; natural selection as the mechanism took longer, solidified by genetics.

I. Early Considerations and Discoveries in Human Evolution

Slide 1: Title Slide

Slide 2: Fossil Hominids, Human Evolution: Thomas Huxley & Eugene Dubois

  • Core Concept: Charles Darwin's initial strategic omission of human evolution from Origin of Species and the early state of fossil evidence.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • When Charles Darwin wrote Origin of Species, he recognized that humans exhibited hereditary variation and differential reproduction, the key ingredients for natural selection.

    • However, Darwin chose not to write about human origins in his first book on evolution, largely as a strategic decision due to prevailing prejudices.

      • In 1857, two years before Origin of Species was published, Alfred Russel Wallace asked Darwin if he would discuss the origin of mankind.

      • Darwin replied, "I think I shall avoid the whole subject, as so surrounded with prejudices, though I fully admit that it is the highest and most interesting problem for the naturalist."

    • Darwin was also aware of the lack of a significant fossil record at the time to develop a robust hypothesis about human evolution.

    • While some stone tools had been found alongside fossils of extinct mammals, in the 1800s, these were generally considered to be only a few thousand years old and attributed to "lost tribes of savages."

Slide 3: First human fossils discovered (Neanderthal)

  • Core Concept: The discovery and initial interpretation of the first Neanderthal fossil.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • The first partial fossil human remains came to light in 1857, but naturalists struggled to recognize their true significance.

    • German miners in the Feldhofer Grotto (Neander Valley) discovered a skullcap.

    • Characteristics of the Skullcap: Looked somewhat human but was remarkably thick and had a massive brow ridge.

    • Initial Debate:

      • Did it belong to an ancient, extinct human-like species?

      • Or was Neanderthal man simply an extreme variant of Homo sapiens?

    • Herman Schaaffhausen, one of the first German naturalists to describe the skull, believed the latter. He ignored evidence that the skull was found with extinct cave bear and mammoth fossils, claiming it was from a "recent barbarian," possibly from wild tribes mentioned by Roman historians.

  • Image Description: Two skull profiles are shown for comparison:

    • Neanderthal: Depicted with a more sloping forehead, prominent brow ridge, and a larger, more projecting occipital region (back of the skull). The jaw appears robust.

    • Modern human: Depicted with a more vertical forehead, less prominent brow ridge, and a more rounded occipital region. The jaw appears less robust.

  • Transcript Elaboration: The instructor notes that the discovery in the Neander Valley "started actually the whole research on species that appeared before Homo sapiens."

Slide 4: Thomas Huxley's View on Neanderthals

  • Core Concept: Thomas Huxley's interpretation of the Neanderthal skull, influenced by Euro-centric biases of his time.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • Shortly after Darwin published Origin of Species, Thomas Huxley (a strong supporter of Darwin) considered the Neander Valley skull.

    • Huxley shared some Euro-centric notions prevalent at the time.

    • It was thought that Europeans had the "best-developed brains" compared to, for example, Australian Aborigines, whose skulls were perceived as having lower profiles and thicker brows.

    • This bias led Huxley to consider Neanderthals as occupying a "slightly lower position within Homo sapiens."

  • Image Description: A portrait of Thomas Huxley.

  • Transcript Elaboration: The instructor characterizes Huxley's view as "kind of a racist comment."

Slide 5: Darwin publishes on human origins

  • Core Concept: Darwin's publication of The Descent of Man and his arguments regarding human evolution, including the concept of sexual selection.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • Amid ambiguous developments regarding early fossils, Darwin decided to address human origins.

    • In 1871, he published The Descent of Man and Selection in Relation to Sex.

    • Darwin's Arguments:

      • All known evidence was consistent with humans having evolved from a common ancestor shared with apes.

      • He speculated that Africa was their place of origin and that human ancestors had gradually taken on their current form since then.

      • He suggested that natural selection was not the only evolutionary pressure; sexual selection (e.g., women preferring certain traits in men) might have also played a role, potentially giving rise to differences between races.

        • Transcript Elaboration on Sexual Selection: "Sexual selection basically describes the chance of mating with another individual from the population is not equally likely. That is, the female individual makes a preference on one member over the other."

    • Alfred Russel Wallace's Disagreement: Darwin's ideas did not persuade Wallace.

      • Wallace believed human oversized brains were "far more powerful than necessary" for mere survival (compared to an ape's).

      • He concluded that the creation of humans must have been the work of divine intervention.

  • Transcript Elaboration: The instructor notes, "this is, that is, here where religious doctrines start coming into play."

Slide 6: More human fossils discovered (Pithecanthropus erectus)

  • Core Concept: Further Neanderthal discoveries and Eugene Dubois's discovery of Pithecanthropus erectus in Java.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • Fossils were crucial for resolving the debate on human evolution but were slow to be discovered.

    • 1886: Neanderthal fossils were discovered for a second time, in Spy, Belgium. These included a jaw and other skeletal parts, found in ancient rocks, demonstrating Neanderthals were not recent "barbarians."

    • Eugene Dubois, a young Dutch anatomist, traveled to Indonesia in 1887 hoping to find early human fossils.

      • He chose Indonesia because orangutans lived there, and he secured a position as a medical officer.

      • After four years, he found fossil remains in eastern Java (Solo River) of a creature that was "not quite human, but not quite ape."

      • Characteristics of Dubois's find: It stood upright, but its brain was too small to qualify as human.

      • It became known as Pithecanthropus erectus, meaning "upright ape-man."

  • Image Description: A portrait of Eugene Dubois. Image courtesy of the National Museum of Natural History: Naturalis, Leiden, The Netherlands.

Slide 7: Skull Comparison Image (Neanderthal, Homo erectus, Modern Human)

  • Image Description: Three skull profiles are shown side-by-side for comparison:

    • Neanderthal: Sloping forehead, prominent brow ridge, projecting mid-face, larger cranial capacity than H. erectus but often a different shape than modern humans (longer and lower).

    • Homo erectus: More receding forehead than Neanderthal, very prominent brow ridge, smaller cranial capacity than Neanderthal or modern human, face more prognathic (projecting forward) than modern humans.

    • Modern human: Vertical forehead, reduced brow ridge, larger and more rounded cranium, flatter face.

Slide 8: Fossil evidence and the acceptance of human evolution (Dubois's Homo erectus)

  • Core Concept: The initial skepticism and eventual scientific acceptance of Dubois's Homo erectus discovery.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • Dubois returned to Europe in 1895 to present his discovery but faced stiff opposition.

    • Skepticism:

      • Some questioned if the ape-like skull and human-like femur came from the same individual.

II. The Expanding Hominid Fossil Record and Evolutionary Patterns

Slide 9: Twentieth-century discoveries and the "bushy" tree of hominid evolution

  • Core Concept: The 20th century saw a significant increase in hominid fossil discoveries, revealing a complex, branching pattern of evolution rather than a simple linear progression, and confirming an African origin.

  • Key Takeaways:

    • The 20th century yielded many more fossils of humans and hominids (members of the human taxonomic family, Hominidae, including modern humans and their direct ancestors).

    • Today, around twenty hominid species have been identified, with the oldest dating back approximately six million years.

    • These fossils point to an African origin for hominids, as Darwin had proposed.

    • Pattern of Evolution:

      • Hominid evolution was initially sometimes depicted as a single line of descent with a steady progression from primitive to more advanced forms.

      • However, the fossil evidence suggests a different pattern: hominid evolution produced a "dense thicket of branches," with several species co-existing at any given time, except for the last 30,000 years or so (during which Homo sapiens has been the only surviving hominid species). [Instructor Emphasis]

    • DNA Evidence: Knowledge from DNA comparisons of humans, apes, and even Neanderthals has added to the wealth of data.

    • While many questions remain, scientists have a growing "treasury of evidence."

Slide 16: Misconceptions about evolutionary theory and processes

  • Evolution is a theory about the origin of life.

  • Evolutionary theory implies that life evolved (and continues to evolve) randomly, or by chance.

  • Evolution results in progress; organisms are always getting better through evolution.

  • Individual organisms can evolve during a single lifespan.

  • Evolution only occurs slowly and gradually.

  • Because evolution is slow, humans cannot influence it.

  • Genetic drift only occurs in small populations.

  • Humans are not currently evolving.

  • Species are distinct natural entities, with a clear definition, that can be easily recognized by anyone.

Slide 17: Misconceptions about natural selection and adaptation

  • Natural selection involves organisms trying to adapt.

  • Natural selection gives organisms what they need.

  • Humans can't negatively impact ecosystems, because species will just evolve what they need to survive.

  • Natural selection acts for the good of the species.

  • The fittest organisms in a population are those that are strongest, healthiest, fastest, and/or largest.

  • Natural selection is about survival of the very fittest individuals in a population.

  • Natural selection produces organisms perfectly suited to their environments.

  • All traits of organisms are adaptations.

Slide 18: Misconceptions about evolutionary trees & Misconceptions about population genetics

  • Misconceptions about evolutionary trees:

    • Taxa that are adjacent on the tips of phylogeny are more closely related to one another than they are to taxa on more distant tips of the phylogeny.

    • Taxa that appear near the top or right-hand side of a phylogeny are more advanced than other organisms on the tree.

    • Taxa that are nearer the bottom or left-hand side of a phylogeny represent the ancestors of the other organisms on the tree.

    • Taxa that are nearer the bottom or left-hand side of a phylogeny evolved earlier than other taxa on the tree.

    • A long branch on a phylogeny indicates that the taxon has changed little since it diverged from other taxa.

  • Misconceptions about population genetics:

    • Each trait is influenced by one Mendelian locus.

    • Each locus has only two alleles.

Slide 19: Misconceptions about evolution and the nature of science & Misconceptions about the acceptance of evolution

  • Misconceptions about evolution and the nature of science:

    • Evolution is not science because it is not observable or testable.

    • Evolution is 'just' a theory.

    • Evolutionary theory is invalid because it is incomplete and cannot give a total explanation for the biodiversity we see around us.

    • Gaps in the fossil record disprove evolution.

  • Misconceptions about the acceptance of evolution:

    • The theory of evolution is flawed, but scientists won't admit it.

    • Evolution is a theory in crisis and is collapsing as scientists lose confidence in it.

    • Most biologists have rejected 'Darwinism' and no longer agree with the ideas put forth by Darwin and Wallace.

Summary & Review Points (Lecture 7: Human Evolution)

  • Darwin's Initial Approach: Darwin strategically avoided discussing human evolution in Origin of Species due to societal prejudices and lack of fossil evidence, despite recognizing humans fit natural selection principles.

  • Early Fossil Discoveries:

    • Neanderthal (1857): First human-like fossil (skullcap) found in Neander Valley, Germany. Initially debated whether it was an ancient species or an extreme Homo sapiens.

    • Thomas Huxley: Viewed Neanderthals as a "lower position within Homo sapiens," influenced by Euro-centric biases.

    • Pithecanthropus erectus (now Homo erectus): Discovered by Eugene Dubois in Java (1887-1890s). Showed upright posture but a smaller brain than modern humans. Faced initial skepticism but later accepted.

  • Darwin's The Descent of Man (1871): Argued humans evolved from an ape-like common ancestor, likely in Africa, and introduced sexual selection as a factor in human evolution. Wallace disagreed, citing divine intervention for human brains.

  • The "Bushy" Tree of Hominid Evolution: Twentieth-century fossil discoveries revealed that hominid evolution was not linear but a complex branching pattern with multiple species co-existing. An African origin is supported.

  • Humans' Place in Primate Phylogeny: Genetic data (only ~1% DNA difference) confirms humans and chimpanzees are each other's closest living relatives, forming a clade.

  • Key Hominid Lineages & Milestones:

    • ~6 mya: Split from chimpanzee lineage in Africa.

    • Ardipithecus ramidus: An early hominid.

    • Australopithecus species (e.g., A. anamensis, A. afarensis "Lucy", A. africanus): Early bipedal hominids in Africa.

    • Paranthropus species (e.g., P. boisei, P. robustus): Robust australopithecines, a side branch.

    • Homo habilis: Early Homo species, associated with tool use, larger brains.

    • Homo erectus / H. ergaster: Larger brains, more sophisticated tools (hand axes), first hominids to spread out of Africa.

    • Homo heidelbergensis: Likely ancestor to Neanderthals and Homo sapiens.

    • Homo neanderthalensis: Adapted to cold climates in Europe and Asia, co-existed with early H. sapiens.

    • Homo sapiens: Emerged in Africa ~300,000 ya, developed complex cultures, and eventually became the sole surviving hominid species, spreading globally.
