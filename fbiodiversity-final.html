**Answer for John Endler's Guppy Experiments:**


**a. Initial Observation or Hypothesis Being Tested:**
Endler observed that in wild guppy populations in Trinidad, male guppy coloration varied depending on the predation pressure in their specific stream locations. In areas with high numbers of dangerous predators, male guppies tended to be duller and less colorful. Conversely, in areas with fewer or less dangerous predators, male guppies were significantly more colorful. This led to the hypothesis that male guppy coloration is a trait influenced by a balance between natural selection (predation favoring duller, camouflaged males) and sexual selection (female preference favoring more colorful males). The experiment aimed to test if populations would evolve different coloration patterns when the predation pressure was altered.


**b. Experimental Design:**
Endler conducted a field experiment.
*   **Manipulation:** He captured guppies from a high-predation site (where males were predominantly dull-colored) and transplanted them to a new, isolated stream section that previously had no guppies but contained only one species of less dangerous predator (or no significant predators).
*   **Control/Comparison:** He also monitored guppy populations in their original high-predation sites and in natural low-predation sites (where males were already colorful) as points of comparison.
*   **Duration:** The transplanted population was allowed to reproduce and evolve for approximately 15 guppy generations (about two years).
*   **Measurement:** After this period, Endler sampled the transplanted population and quantified male coloration (e.g., number and brightness of spots).


**c. Main Results Obtained:**
After 15 generations in the new low-predation environment, the male guppies in the transplanted population had evolved to be significantly more colorful and conspicuous than their ancestors from the high-predation site. Their coloration patterns became similar to those of guppies naturally found in low-predation environments. In contrast, the control populations in high-predation areas remained predominantly dull-colored.


**d. Primary Conclusion and its Broader Significance:**
The primary conclusion was that guppy populations can evolve rapidly in response to changes in selective pressures, specifically the intensity of predation. When predation pressure was reduced, sexual selection exerted by female preference for colorful males became a more dominant force, driving the evolution of brighter male coloration.
**Broader Significance:** This experiment provided strong, direct evidence for:
*   **Rapid evolution:** Significant evolutionary change can occur in relatively short ecological timescales.
*   **Natural selection in action:** It demonstrated how a specific environmental factor (predation) acts as a selective force.
*   **The interplay of selective forces:** It highlighted how traits can be shaped by a balance between different types of selection (natural selection vs. sexual selection).
*   **The role of genetic variation:** The initial population must have possessed genetic variation for coloration for this evolutionary response to occur.

**Answer for Conover and Munch's Atlantic Silverside Experiment:**


**a. Initial Observation or Hypothesis Being Tested:**
The researchers hypothesized that size-selective harvesting (fishing) could cause evolutionary changes in the average body size of fish populations. Specifically, they tested whether consistently removing the largest fish would lead to the evolution of smaller-bodied fish, and conversely, whether removing the smallest fish would lead to the evolution of larger-bodied fish.


**b. Experimental Design:**
*   **Organism:** Atlantic silversides.
*   **Setup:** Six large laboratory tanks were established, each initially stocked with 1,000 fish. The experiment ran for four generations.
*   **Treatments (Selective Harvesting):**
    1.  **"Harvest Largest" (Populations A1, A2 - 2 replicates):** In each generation, before spawning, the 900 largest fish were removed. The remaining 100 smallest fish were allowed to reproduce.
    2.  **"Harvest Smallest" (Populations C1, C2 - 2 replicates):** The 900 smallest fish were removed. The remaining 100 largest fish were allowed to reproduce.
    3.  **"Random Harvest" (Control - Populations B1, B2 - 2 replicates):** 900 fish were removed randomly, without regard to size. The remaining 100 fish of random sizes reproduced.
*   **Replication:** Each treatment was duplicated to ensure reliability and guard against accidental loss of a population.


**c. Main Results Obtained:**
The average body size of the fish populations changed significantly and predictably according to the harvesting regime:
*   **"Harvest Largest" (Smallest reproduced):** The average body size of the fish in these populations *decreased* steadily over the four generations.
*   **"Harvest Smallest" (Largest reproduced):** The average body size of the fish in these populations *increased* steadily over the four generations.
*   **"Random Harvest" (Control):** There was *no significant directional change* in the average body size of the fish in these populations over the four generations.


**d. Primary Conclusion and its Broader Significance:**
The primary conclusion was that size-selective harvesting directly causes rapid evolutionary changes in fish body size. The direction of evolution (towards smaller or larger size) depended on which size class was preferentially removed from the breeding population.
**Broader Significance:**
*   **Fisheries-induced evolution:** This experiment provided strong experimental evidence for a phenomenon observed in wild fisheries – that consistently targeting larger fish can lead to wild populations evolving to mature at smaller sizes and have smaller average adult sizes. This has significant implications for the sustainability and long-term yield of fisheries.
*   **Human impact as a selective force:** It demonstrates how human activities can act as powerful agents of selection, driving evolutionary change in other species.
*   **Rapid evolution:** It showed that substantial evolutionary change in a life-history trait (body size) can occur over just a few generations.

**Answer:**


**a. What does the theory propose about the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts?**
The Endosymbiotic Theory proposes that mitochondria and chloroplasts, key organelles in eukaryotic cells, originated as free-living prokaryotic organisms that were engulfed by an ancestral host cell (likely an early eukaryotic or pre-eukaryotic cell, possibly an archaeon in the case of mitochondria). Instead of being digested, these engulfed prokaryotes established a symbiotic (initially perhaps parasitic, later mutualistic) relationship with the host cell.
*   **Mitochondria** are proposed to have originated from an engulfed aerobic alpha-proteobacterium. The host cell would have benefited from the efficient ATP production of the aerobic bacterium, while the bacterium benefited from a protected, nutrient-rich environment.
*   **Chloroplasts** are proposed to have originated from an engulfed photosynthetic cyanobacterium. This event occurred later in a lineage that already possessed mitochondria, giving rise to photosynthetic eukaryotes (algae and plants). The host cell benefited from the sugars produced by photosynthesis.


**b. Describe at least three distinct lines of evidence that support this theory.**
1.  **Own DNA and Genetic System:** Both mitochondria and chloroplasts contain their own **circular DNA molecules**, similar in structure to prokaryotic chromosomes, not the linear chromosomes found in the eukaryotic nucleus. They also possess their own machinery for transcribing and translating this DNA, including **70S-type ribosomes** which are characteristic of prokaryotes (eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S).
2.  **Double Membranes:** Mitochondria and chloroplasts are surrounded by **two (or more) membranes**. The inner membrane has a composition similar to prokaryotic cell membranes (e.g., lacking cholesterol, presence of specific lipids like cardiolipin in mitochondria), while the outer membrane is thought to be derived from the host cell's plasma membrane during the engulfment process (phagocytosis).
3.  **Reproduction by Fission:** Mitochondria and chloroplasts reproduce by a process resembling **binary fission**, similar to how independent prokaryotes divide. This replication is independent of the host cell's nuclear division (mitosis). A eukaryotic cell cannot synthesize new mitochondria or chloroplasts from scratch if they are removed; they must arise from pre-existing ones.
*(Other evidence includes: similar size to bacteria, some organellar genes showing closer phylogenetic relationship to specific bacterial groups than to host nuclear genes).*


**c. How did the accidental experiment with Kwang Jeon's amoebae and X-bacteria provide insights relevant to this theory?**
Kwang Jeon's accidental observation of an X-bacteria infection in his amoeba cultures provided a real-time model for how an initially parasitic or detrimental relationship could evolve into an obligate mutualistic symbiosis, a key step in the endosymbiotic theory.
*   **Initial Stage (Parasitism/Pathology):** The X-bacteria initially made the amoebae sick, and many died, similar to how an engulfed bacterium might initially be a burden or pathogen to a host cell.
*   **Evolution of Dependence (Mutualism):** Over several months (many generations for both organisms), the surviving amoebae and their X-bacteria co-evolved. The bacteria were no longer harmful, and crucially, the amoebae became dependent on a protein produced by the X-bacteria for their own survival. When Jeon killed the bacteria with antibiotics, the host amoebae also died.
*   **Relevance to Endosymbiosis:** This demonstrated that:
    1.  A host-invader relationship can shift towards mutual dependence.
    2.  The host can lose essential functions that become provided by the endosymbiont.
    This parallels the proposed scenario where the ancestral host cell became dependent on the ATP production of the proto-mitochondrion or the sugars from the proto-chloroplast, eventually leading to an inseparable, integrated eukaryotic cell. Jeon's work showed that such a transition to obligate symbiosis is biologically plausible and can occur over relatively short evolutionary timescales.

Alternation of Generations in Plants: Describe the major evolutionary trend observed in the alternation of generations as plants evolved from bryophytes through pteridophytes to seed plants (gymnosperms and angiosperms). For each of these major groups (Bryophytes, Pteridophytes, Seed Plants), discuss: a. Which generation (gametophyte or sporophyte) is dominant? b. The level of independence or dependence of each generation. c. How this trend reflects adaptation to terrestrial life.

**Answer:**


The major evolutionary trend in the alternation of generations in plants, from bryophytes to seed plants, is the **progressive reduction of the gametophyte generation and the increasing dominance, size, and complexity of the sporophyte generation.**


**a. & b. Dominance and Independence/Dependence:**


*   **Bryophytes (e.g., Mosses, Liverworts):**
    *   **Dominant Generation:** The **gametophyte (n)** is the dominant, conspicuous, photosynthetic, and long-lived generation. This is the "leafy" or "thalloid" plant we typically recognize as a moss or liverwort.
    *   **Independence/Dependence:** The gametophyte is **independent**. The **sporophyte (2n)** is small, unbranched, and **permanently attached to and nutritionally dependent on the gametophyte** for its entire existence. It consists of a foot, seta, and capsule (sporangium).


*   **Pteridophytes (Seedless Vascular Plants, e.g., Ferns):**
    *   **Dominant Generation:** The **sporophyte (2n)** is the dominant, conspicuous, photosynthetic, and long-lived generation. This is the familiar fern plant with roots, stems (rhizomes), and leaves (fronds).
    *   **Independence/Dependence:** The sporophyte is **independent**. The **gametophyte (n)** (called a prothallus) is also **independent and photosynthetic**, but it is very small, relatively short-lived, and less conspicuous than the sporophyte. It typically grows close to the ground.


*   **Seed Plants (Gymnosperms and Angiosperms):**
    *   **Dominant Generation:** The **sporophyte (2n)** is overwhelmingly dominant, large, complex, photosynthetic, and long-lived (e.g., a pine tree, an oak tree, a rose bush).
    *   **Independence/Dependence:** The sporophyte is **independent**. The **gametophyte (n)** generation is **extremely reduced in size (microscopic)** and **completely dependent on the sporophyte** for nutrition and protection.
        *   **Male gametophyte:** The pollen grain (develops within microsporangia of the sporophyte).
        *   **Female gametophyte:** The embryo sac (in angiosperms) or female gametophyte with archegonia (in gymnosperms), develops within the ovule, which is part of the sporophyte.


**c. How this trend reflects adaptation to terrestrial life:**
The increasing dominance of the diploid sporophyte and reduction of the haploid gametophyte reflects increasing adaptation to the challenges of terrestrial life:
1.  **Protection from UV Radiation and Desiccation:** Diploidy (2n) provides genetic redundancy, offering a buffer against mutations caused by higher UV radiation on land. The sporophyte, being diploid, is better protected. The vulnerable, delicate gametophyte became increasingly protected within the tissues of the sporophyte.
2.  **Dispersal:** The sporophyte produces spores (in bryophytes and pteridophytes) or seeds (in seed plants). Spores and seeds are well-adapted for dispersal in terrestrial environments (e.g., wind dispersal). The evolution of the seed, a sporophyte-derived structure containing the next generation's sporophyte embryo and protected gametophyte, was a major adaptation for terrestrial reproduction and dispersal.
3.  **Vascular Tissue Development:** The dominant sporophyte generation was where complex vascular tissues (xylem and phloem) evolved, allowing for efficient transport of water and nutrients, enabling plants to grow larger and exploit drier terrestrial habitats. Gametophytes, lacking extensive vascular tissue, remained small and tied to moist environments.
4.  **Independence from Water for Fertilization (in Seed Plants):** The reduction of the male gametophyte to a pollen grain, which can be transported by wind or animals, eliminated the need for free water for sperm to swim to the egg (a requirement for bryophytes and pteridophytes). This was a critical adaptation for colonizing drier terrestrial habitats. The female gametophyte remained protected within the ovule on the parent sporophyte.

4. Key Milestones in Hominid Evolution: The evolution of humans from earlier hominids involved several significant changes. Discuss three of the following key milestones in hominid evolution. For each milestone you choose, identify an associated hominid group (if applicable) and explain its evolutionary significance:

  • Bipedalism

    • Tool use and development

      • Brain size increase (encephalization)

        • Migration out of Africa

          • Development of complex culture/symbolic thought

Answer (Discussing Three Milestones):

1. Bipedalism:

  • Associated Hominid Group(s): One of the earliest defining hominid traits. Evidence of bipedalism is seen in early hominids like Ardipithecus ramidus (though with some arboreal adaptations) and more definitively in Australopithecus species (e.g., Australopithecus afarensis - "Lucy," Laetoli footprints).

  • Evolutionary Significance:

    • Frees the hands: Allowed for carrying objects (food, tools, offspring), tool use, and manipulation of the environment.

    • Energy efficiency: Bipedal locomotion can be more energy-efficient for long-distance travel across open savannas compared to quadrupedalism in apes.

    • Thermoregulation: An upright posture reduces the body surface area exposed to direct overhead sun and increases exposure to cooling breezes, which could have been advantageous in hot, open environments.

    • Enhanced vision: Standing upright provides a better view over tall grasses to spot predators or resources. Bipedalism is considered a foundational adaptation that predates significant brain expansion and complex tool use, setting the stage for subsequent hominid evolution.

2. Tool Use and Development:

  • Associated Hominid Group(s):

    • Earliest simple stone tools (Oldowan tradition - choppers, flakes) are associated with Homo habilis (around 2.5-2.6 mya), though some evidence suggests even earlier australopithecines might have used rudimentary tools.

    • More sophisticated, symmetrical hand axes (Acheulean tradition) are characteristic of Homo erectus / Homo ergaster (starting around 1.7 mya).

    • Further refinements and diversification of toolkits (e.g., Mousterian tools by Homo neanderthalensis; Upper Paleolithic tools by Homo sapiens) occurred with later Homo species.

  • Evolutionary Significance:

    • Improved foraging and processing: Tools allowed access to new food sources (e.g., scavenging meat, marrow extraction, processing tough plant materials) that were otherwise inaccessible.

    • Defense: Tools could have been used for defense against predators.

    • Cognitive development: The planning, foresight, and manual dexterity required for tool making and use likely drove cognitive evolution and brain development.

    • Cultural transmission: Tool-making techniques were likely learned and passed down, indicating early forms of culture. The development of increasingly complex tool technologies reflects growing cognitive abilities and adaptive flexibility in hominids.

3. Brain Size Increase (Encephalization):

  • Associated Hominid Group(s): While early hominids like Australopithecus had ape-sized brains (around 400-550 cc), a significant trend of increasing brain size (encephalization) is observed within the genus Homo.

    • Homo habilis: ~600-750 cc.

    • Homo erectus: ~800-1100 cc.

    • Homo heidelbergensis: ~1100-1400 cc.

    • Homo neanderthalensis: ~1200-1750 cc (on average, slightly larger than modern humans, but different skull shape).

    • Homo sapiens: ~1200-1600 cc (modern average ~1350 cc).

  • Evolutionary Significance:

    • Enhanced cognitive abilities: Larger brains are associated with increased intelligence, problem-solving skills, complex social interactions, planning, and eventually, language and symbolic thought.

    • Facilitated complex behaviors: Enabled the development of more sophisticated tool use, hunting strategies, social organization, and adaptation to diverse environments.

    • Metabolically expensive: Large brains require a significant amount of energy, suggesting that changes in diet (e.g., increased meat consumption, cooking) and foraging efficiency were necessary to support their evolution. The increase in brain size is a hallmark of human evolution, underpinning many of the unique cognitive and behavioral traits of our species.

5. Comparing Major Animal Phyla: Choose two of the following major invertebrate phyla: Porifera, Cnidaria, Platyhelminthes, Nematoda, Annelida, Mollusca, Arthropoda, Echinodermata. For each of the two phyla you choose, describe: a. Their characteristic body symmetry. b. At least two other distinct structural or functional features that define the phylum. c. Provide one common example of an animal from each phylum.

Answer (Example choosing Arthropoda and Mollusca):

Phylum 1: Arthropoda

a. Characteristic Body Symmetry: Arthropods exhibit bilateral symmetry, meaning their body can be divided into two mirror-image halves along a single longitudinal plane.

b. Two Other Distinct Structural or Functional Features:

  1. Exoskeleton: Arthropods possess a hard, external skeleton made primarily of chitin (and often proteins and calcium carbonate). This exoskeleton provides support, protection, and attachment points for muscles. It is non-living and must be periodically shed (molted or ecdysis) to allow for growth.

  2. Jointed Appendages: A defining feature is the presence of jointed appendages (e.g., legs, antennae, mouthparts). These appendages are highly versatile and have been modified for various functions such as walking, swimming, feeding, sensing, and reproduction. The joints allow for a wide range of movement despite the rigid exoskeleton. (Other features include: segmented body typically divided into regions like head, thorax, and abdomen; open circulatory system; specialized respiratory structures like gills, tracheae, or book lungs).

c. Common Example: A common example of an arthropod is an ant (Class Insecta). (Other examples: spider, crab, millipede).

Phylum 2: Mollusca

a. Characteristic Body Symmetry: Most mollusks exhibit bilateral symmetry. (Some gastropods undergo torsion during development, leading to a somewhat asymmetrical adult form, but the underlying plan is bilateral).

b. Two Other Distinct Structural or Functional Features:

  1. Mantle: Mollusks have a specialized fold of tissue called the mantle that drapes over the visceral mass. The mantle is responsible for secreting the shell (if present) and often forms a mantle cavity that houses the gills or lungs and is involved in respiration and excretion.

  2. Muscular Foot: Most mollusks possess a large, muscular foot which is primarily used for locomotion (e.g., crawling in snails, digging in clams). The foot has been modified for different functions in various molluscan groups (e.g., tentacles and arms in cephalopods). (Other features include: a visceral mass containing most internal organs; many possess a radula, a rasping, tongue-like organ for feeding; diverse respiratory structures like gills or a lung-like mantle cavity).

c. Common Example: A common example of a mollusk is a garden snail (Class Gastropoda). (Other examples: clam, squid, octopus).
