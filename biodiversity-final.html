Section II: True/False Questions

  • Rationale for False Statements: Designed to test common misconceptions or specific details that differentiate groups/concepts. The bonus encourages deeper understanding.

  1. False. Archaea lack peptidoglycan; some bacteria also lack cell walls (e.g., Mycoplasma).

  2. False. Random harvesting led to no significant change in average body size. Removing the smallest fish led to an increase.

  3. True. (Key feature of pteridophyte life cycle)

  4. False. Fungi are heterotrophic (saprophytes or parasites) and absorb nutrients; ergosterol is in their cell membranes, not used for photosynthesis.

  5. True. (Key milestone in human evolution)

  6. True. (Defining characteristic of angiosperms)

  7. False. Protists are highly diverse; many are unicellular, but some are colonial or multicellular (e.g., kelp). Many are autotrophic (algae) or mixotrophic.

  8. False. Lamarck proposed inheritance of acquired characteristics and use/disuse. Natural selection was proposed by Darwin and Wallace.

  9. False. Arthropods have an exoskeleton, jointed appendages, and bilateral symmetry. Radial symmetry and water vascular system are characteristic of Echinoderms.

  10. True. (Key evidence for endosymbiotic theory)

  11. False. The gametophyte generation in gymnosperms is highly reduced and dependent on the dominant sporophyte.

  12. False. Birds are endothermic (warm-blooded).

  13. False. Binary fission is asexual and produces genetically identical daughter cells; genetic diversity in prokaryotes comes from mutation, conjugation, etc.

  14. True. (Historical significance of Dubois's find)

  15. False. In the haplontic life cycle, the haploid gametophyte is the dominant phase; the zygote is the only diploid stage.

Section III: Short Answer Questions

  • Rationale: Each question targets a major theme or emphasized area.

    1. Experimental Design: Directly addresses instructor's emphasis. Allows student to show detailed understanding of one key experiment.

    2. Endosymbiotic Theory: Tests understanding of a fundamental evolutionary concept and the ability to recall supporting evidence. The Kwang Jeon part links to a specific case study.

    3. Alternation of Generations: Assesses understanding of a core botanical concept and its evolutionary progression across major plant groups, requiring comparison and synthesis.

    4. Hominid Evolution: Focuses on key trends and the ability to link them to specific hominid advancements, showing an understanding of the broader narrative.

    5. Animal Phyla Comparison: Tests knowledge of invertebrate diversity and the ability to recall and compare defining features of major groups. Offering a choice allows students to play to their strengths.
